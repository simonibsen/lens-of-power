# Principles: Rule of Law Report 2026

SOURCE: Civil Liberties Union for Europe (Liberties), *Rule of Law Report 2026* (2026)

PERSPECTIVE: Hybrid (5/4 — civil society watchdog network with member organisations across 22 EU countries; observer with advocacy function)

CENTRAL THESIS: Rule of law across the EU is characterised by "concerted dismantling and cumulative decline." The European Commission's monitoring mechanism — four years of recommendations, 93% of them repeated from previous years, 61% showing no progress, zero fully implemented — has become a structure that produces the appearance of accountability without its substance. The report's core finding is not that particular countries are failing, but that the monitoring mechanism itself is structurally incapable of producing the outcomes it claims to pursue.

## IC-5 disclosure

The LLM's training data likely overrepresents institutional and procedural perspectives on rule of law — the perspective of the Commission, of court systems, of formal democratic theory. The Liberties report is itself produced by civil liberties organisations whose institutional position makes certain things visible and others invisible:

- **Visible from this position**: Erosion of rights protections, non-implementation of recommendations, civic space restriction, media capture, judicial independence threats. The report is methodologically strong in documenting what governments fail to do.
- **Invisible from this position**: The genuine governance challenges governments cite to justify restrictions (security threats, migration pressures, fiscal constraints, administrative complexity). The perspective of governments implementing the measures the report criticises is structurally absent. When the report documents "regression" in checks and balances across 13 countries, it does not engage with the possibility that some restrictions reflect legitimate (if misguided) responses to real problems rather than deliberate erosion.
- **Blind spot on position**: The report monitors governments from outside government. It cannot see whether non-implementation reflects deliberate obstruction, genuine resource constraints, competing priorities, or institutional incapacity. The report treats all non-implementation as equivalent, which conflates countries where the political will is absent with countries where the capacity is absent.
- **Denmark specifically**: The contributing organisations (Nyt Europa, Danish Institute for Human Rights, Justitia, Danish Union of Journalists) are advocacy-oriented civil society actors. Their documentation of civic space restriction and informal pressure is valuable precisely because these dynamics are invisible to formal indicators — but their institutional interest in framing civic space as "under threat" should be noted.

Positional lens: The report writes from a composite Position 5/4 (observer-advocate) addressing Position 1 (EU institutions and national governments). It speaks about Position 2 (the governed) but does not speak from that position. The governed population's own experience of rule of law — whether courts work for them, whether legal aid is accessible, whether recommendations matter to their lives — is present only as statistics, not as testimony.

TYPE: Annual civil society monitoring report across 22 EU member states, covering justice systems, anti-corruption, media freedom, and checks and balances

LAYERS ADDRESSED: Institutional (primary), Legal & Regulatory, Thought & Narrative, Surveillance & Information

PRINCIPLE CANDIDATE: yes — the report identifies several structural mechanisms through which rule-of-law erosion operates, including the implementation gap as a self-sustaining system, informal erosion beneath formal adequacy, and the monitoring process itself as appearance management

INSTRUMENT CANDIDATE: no — the report's country-by-country assessment methodology is specific to EU rule-of-law monitoring and does not generalise as a framework instrument

RATIONALE: The report's value to the framework is not its country-specific findings but its documentation of structural mechanisms that operate across 22 countries simultaneously. It provides a rare empirical basis for several framework patterns — particularly the implementation gap as a structural feature rather than a temporary shortcoming. The Denmark chapter provides granular evidence connecting to prior analyses of Danish institutional dynamics.

### SYNTHESIS SUMMARY

Liberties yields 7 principles and no instrument proposals. The report's distinctive contribution is its documentation of the EU monitoring mechanism as itself a power structure: a system that produces accountability-shaped outputs (recommendations, reports, assessments) without accountability-shaped outcomes (implementation, reform, enforcement). This is the first extraction from a monitoring body that reveals the monitor's own structural limitations. The strongest novel contribution is the implementation gap as mechanism (P1), which documents how recommendation-without-enforcement becomes a self-legitimating cycle. The Denmark material (P6, P7) connects directly to prior analyses of Danish institutional dynamics.

## Principles

### P1: The Implementation Gap as Self-Legitimating Mechanism

PRINCIPLE: A monitoring system that issues recommendations without enforcement mechanisms produces a self-legitimating cycle: the act of recommending creates the appearance of oversight; the absence of enforcement ensures the recommendations are not implemented; the non-implementation generates the need for continued monitoring; the continued monitoring generates the appearance of continued oversight. The system's failure is its function.

LAYERS: Institutional, Legal & Regulatory

MECHANISM: The European Commission's Rule of Law Cycle demonstrates this in pure form. Since 2022, 93% of recommendations have been repeated from previous years. 61% show no progress. Zero have been fully implemented. Yet the cycle continues — each year producing a new report, new assessments, new recommendations. The mechanism operates through three reinforcing dynamics: (1) Recommendations are formulated at a level of generality that makes compliance assessment ambiguous (e.g., "continue to advance with the process to reform" — a formulation the Denmark chapter notes is "difficult to measure the level of compliance with"). (2) No threshold triggers escalation — there is no point at which repeated non-implementation automatically activates enforcement tools. (3) The existence of the monitoring process itself is cited as evidence that rule-of-law concerns are being addressed, reducing pressure for more consequential action.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: "Four consecutive rule of law reporting cycles, with repeated recommendations...have produced no structural reform" and "the reporting process itself is undermined when issues that are consistently raised...become entrenched...and the Commission accepts minimal progress...as genuine effort." 93% of 2025 recommendations were repetitions. Across all four pillars — justice, anti-corruption, media, checks and balances — the same structural deficiencies documented in 2022 persist in 2026, often in intensified form.

GENERALISES TO: Corporate compliance programmes that produce reporting without behavioural change. International human rights treaty monitoring where periodic review becomes the endpoint rather than the beginning. Environmental regulation where monitoring produces data without producing remediation. Academic accreditation where self-study and review cycles substitute for substantive quality change. Any monitoring system where the process of assessment becomes the product, displacing the outcomes the assessment was designed to produce.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends Axiom 7 (stated purpose vs. function) — the monitoring system's stated purpose is to prevent rule-of-law erosion; its function is to produce the appearance of prevention while allowing erosion to continue. Extends the Inversion of Stated Purpose pattern with a specific mechanism: recommendation-without-enforcement as a structural design that inverts the monitor's stated purpose.

STATUS: observed

### P2: Informal Erosion Beneath Formal Adequacy

PRINCIPLE: The most consequential erosion of democratic protections occurs not through formal legal changes but through political discourse, administrative practice, and funding manipulation that leave the formal framework intact while degrading its practical operation. Systems with strong formal indicators are the most vulnerable to this form of erosion because the formal indicators themselves become a shield against scrutiny.

LAYERS: Institutional, Thought & Narrative

MECHANISM: The report documents this across multiple countries but most granularly in Denmark, where: (1) The country ranks first globally in the World Justice Project's Rule of Law Index while simultaneously classified as a "slider" — the formal ranking masks practical deterioration. (2) Civic space restriction operates through "delegitimising political rhetoric, the politicisation of public funding, selective use of administrative tools, and informal signalling about the limits of acceptable civic engagement" — none of which register in formal indicators. (3) Anti-corruption safeguards are absent not because of legal regression but because "trust-based governance" substitutes informal norms for enforceable rules, and the absence of formal corruption is cited as evidence that formal safeguards are unnecessary. (4) The gap between the Commission's assessment ("limited progress," "some further progress") and civil society's assessment ("no progress") reflects different measurement — the Commission measures formal steps; civil society measures practical outcomes.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Denmark chapter: "This form of backsliding is particularly difficult to detect through traditional rule-of-law indicators, which tend to prioritise legislative amendments, institutional design, and formal procedural safeguards. As a result, developments that materially affect checks-and-balances in practice are under-represented in the Commission's analysis." Also: "High international rankings risk masking persistent deficiencies and reducing the perceived urgency for reform." The report documents that Denmark's "strong legal framework masks mounting pressures on checks and balances and civic space."

GENERALISES TO: Corporate governance where formal compliance frameworks coexist with substantive capture. Academic institutions where formal peer review coexists with systemic publication bias. Financial regulation where formal capital adequacy requirements coexist with systemic risk accumulation. Any institutional context where what is measured diverges from what matters, and the favourable measurement is used to forestall examination of the unfavourable reality.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends Axiom 3 (fifth mode — hegemonic naturalisation) — the formal adequacy of the legal framework is the hegemonic background against which erosion operates. The formal framework is not hidden; it is visible, measurable, and frequently cited. But it constitutes the background against which practical erosion becomes invisible. The analyst who asks "is the legal framework adequate?" finds "yes" and stops looking. The question the framework makes difficult to ask is: "what is happening beneath the formal adequacy?"

STATUS: observed

### P3: Functional Opacity Through Structural Fragmentation

PRINCIPLE: Anti-corruption and integrity frameworks that distribute oversight responsibilities across multiple bodies, rely on informal norms rather than enforceable rules, and treat corruption risks as isolated incidents rather than systemic patterns produce structural opacity as a default — not through concealment but through fragmentation that prevents any single point of oversight from seeing the whole.

LAYERS: Institutional, Legal & Regulatory, Surveillance & Information

MECHANISM: Denmark provides the purest example. Despite ranking as one of the world's least corrupt countries: (1) No comprehensive national anti-corruption or integrity strategy exists. (2) No mandatory lobby register. (3) No binding revolving-door rules for ministers and senior officials. (4) No adequate control of asset declarations. (5) No code of conduct or register of external interests for members of parliament. (6) Political financing allows donor anonymity above disclosure thresholds and circumvention through intermediaries. These are not gaps in an otherwise functional system — they are a system that functions precisely by making influence invisible. The mechanism is reinforced by the self-perception that Denmark does not have a corruption problem: "integrity concerns are treated as isolated incidents rather than systemic vulnerabilities." The very success of the informal system at preventing overt corruption becomes the argument against formalising protections, even as the informal system creates structural vulnerability to less visible forms of influence.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: GRECO classified Denmark as being in "insufficient compliance." OECD highlighted limited implementation and continued reliance on reactive enforcement. Transparency International Denmark documented "recurring cases of alleged influence peddling, nepotism, and ethical ambiguity across sectors between 2000 and 2024, pointing to systemic preventive blind spots rather than isolated incidents." The report notes: "political leaders have downplayed these risks" and "transparency reforms are resisted because corruption is not perceived as a systemic problem."

GENERALISES TO: Regulatory systems that distribute oversight across multiple agencies with overlapping but incomplete jurisdictions. Financial systems where opacity results from structural complexity rather than deliberate concealment. Any system where the absence of visible corruption is taken as evidence that anti-corruption mechanisms are unnecessary, creating the conditions for invisible corruption.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends the Division of Learning pattern — the structural fragmentation creates an asymmetry of knowledge where those who influence policy can see the whole system (their own networks, career trajectories, and informal relationships) while those who might scrutinise influence cannot aggregate the dispersed pieces into a legible picture

STATUS: observed

### P4: Recommendation Drift as Accountability Erosion

PRINCIPLE: When monitoring recommendations are reformulated across reporting cycles — rephrased, narrowed, softened, or redirected — the original standard against which compliance was measured is displaced. Each reformulation creates ambiguity about what constitutes compliance, reducing the accountability pressure the recommendation was designed to create. The recommendation does not disappear; it transforms into a less demanding version of itself.

LAYERS: Institutional, Thought & Narrative

MECHANISM: The report documents this across multiple domains. In Denmark's justice system: the 2022 recommendation to "ensure adequate human and financial resources for the justice system" was rephrased in 2024 to "review of the legal aid system" — shifting from a demand for resources to a demand for a review of whether resources are needed. As the Denmark chapter notes: "This formulation of the recommendation fails to address the deeper problem" and "only focuses on the review process and does not provide expectation on implementation, timeline, or alignment with concrete European standards." In anti-corruption: the Commission's 2022 recommendation on political financing was not reiterated after 2022 despite stalled legislation and continued criticism, "risk[ing] conveying that the issue has lost priority for the Commission." In media: recommendations formulated with "continue to" language create a baseline where any activity can be reported as compliance.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: The executive summary notes: "a recommendation that stipulates 'continue to' is difficult to measure the level of compliance with." The Denmark chapter details how the legal aid recommendation was "changed from its original 2022 wording" in ways that "fail to address the deeper problem." Across the report, "the vast majority of recommendations first issued in 2022 remain unimplemented" — but the formulations have evolved in ways that make non-implementation less visible.

GENERALISES TO: Treaty compliance where periodic review softens initial commitments. Performance management systems where goals are adjusted downward to match performance rather than performance being adjusted upward to match goals. Any accountability system where the standard of measurement evolves to accommodate the measured entity's failures.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends Axiom 6 (language as first instrument) — the reformulation of recommendations is a language operation that changes the terms of accountability. The recommendation does not say "we accept non-compliance"; it says "continue to advance with the process" — language that makes non-compliance difficult to name.

STATUS: observed

### P5: Civic Space Restriction Through Conditional Legitimacy

PRINCIPLE: Democratic governments restrict civic space not by banning organisations or criminalising dissent but by establishing conditions under which civil society participation is considered legitimate — conditions that exclude organisations whose activities challenge government policy. The mechanism operates through funding conditionality, delegitimising rhetoric, and administrative burden, producing self-censorship without formal prohibition.

LAYERS: Institutional, Thought & Narrative, Economic

MECHANISM: The report documents this across the EU, most granularly in Denmark: (1) **Funding conditionality**: 25% of CSOs in a recent survey reported experiences of conditional or agenda-linked funding. Conservative MP Helle Bonnesen publicly argued CSOs bringing legal cases against the state should not receive public funding. Similar arguments appear in a joint political programme by four right-wing parties. (2) **Delegitimising rhetoric**: PM Mette Frederiksen and former Conservative Party leader Søren Pape characterised climate activists as "anti-democratic." The Denmark chapter notes: "a growing number of individuals and organisations refrain from participating in public debate out of fear of backlash or negative consequences." (3) **Consultation capture**: Public consultations with deadlines of less than 10 days have increased since 2021. CSOs reported their consultation inputs "were later used to justify political positions that directly contradicted their advocacy, creating a sense that participation could be used against them." (4) **Administrative burden**: Difficulties opening bank accounts, complex compliance requirements, and uneven access to public infrastructure constrain operational capacity. Across the EU more broadly: Slovakia's NGO law requires mandatory public disclosure of personal data for donors. Bulgaria proposed a "Russian-style" Foreign Agents Registration Act. The Netherlands proposed a "Law on the Transparency of Civil Society Organisations." The EU itself opened a Parliamentary scrutiny working group to investigate civil society. The Commission proposed legislation on "foreign interest representation services."

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Checks and balances chapter: "While some governments overtly attempt to limit the space for independent civil society organisations, even those that ostensibly aim to create a fair, transparent system, fail to do so and open the space for abuse from more malicious actors." EU Fundamental Rights Agency found 85% of respondents said funding concerns threaten some, all, or most of their work; 44% reported unexpected funding cuts. Denmark chapter: "These pressures are largely informal and therefore difficult to capture through index-based assessments, but their cumulative effect risks narrowing the space for independent oversight and dissent."

GENERALISES TO: University funding models that produce self-censorship in research topics. Media advertising dependency that constrains editorial independence. Any institutional context where participation is formally open but practically conditional on alignment with the funding entity's preferences — the right to speak exists; the capacity to speak is selectively distributed.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends the Anticipatory Obedience pattern — civic space restriction through conditional legitimacy produces anticipatory obedience in civil society organisations, which self-censor to maintain funding and access rather than waiting for formal prohibition. Also extends Axiom 9 (manufactured compliance without explicit coercion) — the mechanism produces compliance through funding dependency and social stigmatisation rather than legal prohibition.

STATUS: observed

### P6: The Monitoring Paradox — Legibility as Shield

PRINCIPLE: Countries that rank highest on formal rule-of-law indicators are the most resistant to scrutiny of substantive rule-of-law erosion, because the indicators themselves are cited as evidence that scrutiny is unnecessary. The monitoring system cannot see what its own metrics obscure.

LAYERS: Institutional, Surveillance & Information

MECHANISM: Denmark exemplifies this paradox. It ranks first globally in the World Justice Project's Rule of Law Index. This ranking is cited domestically as evidence that rule-of-law concerns are exaggerated or misplaced. Yet the Liberties report classifies Denmark as a "slider" — a country with historically strong democratic reputation where rule-of-law erosion is underway. The mechanism: (1) High formal rankings reduce the perceived urgency for reform ("we are already the best"). (2) The Commission issues fewer and weaker recommendations to high-ranking countries, even when substantive concerns are documented. (3) Denmark received no recommendations on checks and balances despite documented civic space restriction. (4) The Commission's assessment of Denmark's Access to Public Administrative Documents Act as "some further progress" is contradicted by civil society's assessment of "no progress." The monitoring system's own metrics create a zone of impunity for countries that perform well on formal indicators.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Denmark chapter: "High international rankings risk masking persistent deficiencies and reducing the perceived urgency for reform." The executive summary classifies Denmark among six "sliders" that "are particularly noteworthy given their historically strong democratic reputations, confirming that rule of law erosion is not confined to a small number of outliers." Denmark's justice system shows "regression" despite being ranked first globally. The absence of checks-and-balances recommendations for Denmark despite documented concerns exemplifies the paradox.

GENERALISES TO: Financial regulation where well-capitalised banks receive less supervisory scrutiny despite harbouring concentrated risk. Academic institutions where prestige insulates against quality review. Healthcare systems where aggregate outcome statistics obscure disparities in access. Any monitoring system where the metrics used to assess performance create blind spots in the assessment — where being measured as good makes it harder to identify where one is becoming worse.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends Axiom 8 (what is absent) — the highest-ranking country's problems are absent from the monitoring system not because they are concealed but because the monitoring system's own structure makes them invisible. Also connects to Appearance as Structural Terrain — the ranking is the appearance; the erosion beneath it is the reality.

STATUS: observed

### P7: Convention Scepticism as Selective Compliance Infrastructure

PRINCIPLE: When political actors in established democracies publicly question the binding nature of international conventions and court judgments, they are not withdrawing from the legal order — they are establishing the discursive infrastructure for selective compliance. The mechanism does not require formal withdrawal; it requires normalising the idea that compliance is a political choice rather than a legal obligation.

LAYERS: Thought & Narrative, Legal & Regulatory, Institutional

MECHANISM: Denmark in 2025 demonstrates this in early formation: (1) The government appointed an "Ambassador for Conventions" and launched an investigation into Denmark's obligations under the ECHR — formally framed as "clarificatory" but raising concerns about reinterpretation rather than reinforcement. (2) PM Mette Frederiksen co-authored an open letter with Italy's PM Giorgia Meloni calling for "a new approach to migration" under the conventions. (3) The liberal party Venstre stated readiness to leave the European Convention on Nationality. (4) Following the CJEU's December 2025 ruling that Denmark's "Ghetto law" may constitute direct discrimination, the Danish People's Party publicly stated Denmark should ignore the ruling. (5) The government announced legislation on foreigner deportation that contradicts current ECtHR case law, premised on the expectation that the ECtHR will change its practice — pre-legislating non-compliance under the assumption that the standard will move to accommodate the violation. This connects directly to the prior Danish election analysis, which documented institutional reproduction of centrism on immigration. The convention scepticism is not an independent development — it is the legal-institutional expression of the same political dynamics that produce centrist consensus on restrictive immigration policy.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Denmark chapter: "While Denmark has not formally withdrawn from any international obligations or refused to implement a judgment, the normalisation of political narratives questioning compliance with international law constitutes an emerging structural risk to the rule of law." Also: "Denmark's political orientation towards non-compliance and actively wanting to withdraw from conventions are also a critical warning sign that the political environment has started to change regarding its international commitments."

GENERALISES TO: Trade agreements where parties maintain membership while selectively implementing obligations. International labour standards where ratification is decoupled from enforcement. Environmental treaties where parties accept targets while pre-establishing the discursive basis for non-compliance. Any legal framework where the binding nature of obligations is a function of political will rather than structural enforcement — maintaining formal membership while degrading substantive compliance.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: connects to the Danish election analysis (institutional reproduction of centrism) and the Greenland analysis (alliance as revealed power structure). The convention scepticism documented here is the legal-institutional mechanism through which the political dynamics identified in those analyses express themselves in the rule-of-law domain. Also extends Axiom 3 (visible mechanism, visible contingency, prohibitive cost) — the Danish population can see the convention scepticism, can see that compliance is contingent on political choice, but the cost of challenging a bipartisan consensus on immigration restriction is structurally prohibitive.

STATUS: observed (the discursive infrastructure is documented); inferred (the characterisation of this as "selective compliance infrastructure" rather than legitimate policy debate is an analytical judgment — the null case is that political actors genuinely believe conventions should be reformed and are pursuing that through legitimate channels)

## Patterns identified

### Existing patterns confirmed

- **Inversion of Stated Purpose** (ESTABLISHED): The EU Rule of Law Cycle's stated purpose is to prevent rule-of-law erosion; its documented function is to produce the appearance of prevention. Monitoring mechanism operates as legitimation rather than enforcement. Also manifested in Denmark's anti-corruption framework, where the absence of corruption is used to justify the absence of anti-corruption safeguards.
- **Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity** (ESTABLISHED): The EU monitoring framework changes form across cycles (recommendations are rephrased, reformulated, redirected) without changing the substance of what is monitored or the outcomes produced. 93% recommendation repetition rate across cycles.
- **Appearance as Structural Terrain** (ESTABLISHED): The monitoring process is the appearance; continued erosion is the reality. Denmark's Rule of Law Index ranking is the appearance; documented regression is the reality. Formal civic space classifications ("open," 89/100) are the appearance; informal pressures documented by civil society are the reality.
- **Institutional Abdication** (ESTABLISHED): The Commission recommending without enforcing — possessing but not deploying the enforcement tools it has (Article 7 procedures, infringement proceedings, budgetary conditionality). The Commission has tools but calculates that non-use is safer than deployment.
- **Capture Pipeline** (ESTABLISHED): Judicial appointment mechanisms across multiple EU states subject to political influence (Malta, Latvia, Bulgaria, Slovakia). Media regulatory authority leadership selection under political control (Hungary, Slovakia, Romania). Anti-corruption body dissolution in Slovakia.
- **Anticipatory Obedience** (ESTABLISHED): Journalist self-censorship documented across the EU. Civil society self-censorship in Denmark documented by the national human rights institution. CSOs refrain from challenging government policy to maintain funding access. Media outlets adjust coverage to anticipated government reaction — Slovakia's 500+ hateful Meta ads targeting specific journalists prior to SLAPP suits as signalling mechanism.
- **Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence** (ESTABLISHED): Formal democratic institutions coexisting with substantive capture across multiple EU states. Denmark demonstrates a variant: formal democratic institutions coexisting with substantive informal power networks operating through revolving doors, lobbying, and political financing opacity — in one of the world's formally strongest democracies.

### No new patterns identified

The report confirms and extends existing patterns rather than revealing new structural mechanisms. Its value is the breadth of empirical documentation across 22 countries.

### IC-1 flags

- **Axiom 7 (refinement)**: The Liberties report provides the strongest evidence yet that monitoring systems can *function as* the inversion of their stated purpose — not incidentally but structurally. The monitoring system does not fail to prevent erosion despite trying; the monitoring system's structure ensures erosion continues while producing the appearance of prevention. This suggests Axiom 7 should be understood to encompass not just systems that describe themselves inaccurately but systems whose formal process *is* the gap between stated purpose and actual function.

## Connections to existing principles

- **Snyder P1** (anticipatory obedience): The civic space restriction documented here operates through anticipatory obedience — CSOs self-censor before being formally restricted. Denmark's national human rights institution documents a "chilling effect" — the framework's term for anticipatory obedience in institutional settings.
- **Snyder P2** (institutional decay as prerequisite): The Commission's non-deployment of enforcement tools is institutional abdication from the EU level — the institution that could enforce compliance chooses not to, creating the vacuum in which erosion accelerates.
- **Alley P4** (naming as disruption, then capture): The Liberties report itself is an act of naming. But the report's own analysis suggests that the naming is being captured — the Commission's monitoring process absorbs civil society's findings into a cycle that produces reports rather than outcomes. IC-1 flag on Axiom 10: can the naming function of civil society monitoring be captured by the process it feeds into?
- **Piketty P7** (opacity as enabler): Denmark's anti-corruption gaps — no lobby register, no revolving-door rules, no asset declaration verification — create the structural opacity that enables influence without visibility.
- **Project 2025 P1** (personnel as instrument): Judicial appointment capture across EU states mirrors the personnel-based institutional control documented in Project 2025, adapted to parliamentary rather than presidential systems.

## Denmark cross-references

The Denmark chapter connects to two prior analyses:

- **Danish election analysis** (2026-03-28): The election analysis documented institutional reproduction of centrism through coalition dynamics, media framing, and business mobilisation against wealth tax proposals. The Liberties report reveals the institutional substrate: weak FOI laws, absent lobbying transparency, revolving doors between politics and business, and politicised public funding create the structural conditions within which centrist reproduction operates. The "trust-based governance" the Liberties report describes is the institutional mechanism through which the informal power networks identified in the election analysis operate without formal visibility.
- **Greenland analysis** (2026-03-19): The Greenland analysis documented alliance as revealed power structure — the US-Denmark relationship exposing the real hierarchy beneath formal equality. The convention scepticism documented in the Liberties report is the domestic expression of the same dynamic — Denmark's willingness to challenge international legal obligations emerges most visibly on immigration, the same policy domain where the election analysis documented centrist consensus. The convention scepticism is not independent of the political dynamics; it is their legal-institutional expression.

Together, the three sources reveal a consistent structure: Denmark's formal democratic institutions (ranked first globally) coexist with informal power dynamics (business influence, centrist reproduction, convention scepticism) that operate beneath the formal indicators — exactly the Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence pattern, manifested in one of the world's formally strongest democracies.
