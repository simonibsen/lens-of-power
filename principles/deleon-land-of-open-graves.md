# Principles: The Land of Open Graves

SOURCE: Jason De León, *The Land of Open Graves: Living and Dying on
the Migrant Trail*, University of California Press, 2015

TYPE: Anthropology / Border studies / Forensic archaeology / Ethnography

CENTRAL THESIS: The US government's "Prevention Through Deterrence"
(PTD) border strategy, adopted in the 1990s, deliberately funnels
undocumented border crossers away from urban entry points and into the
most lethal terrain in the borderlands — the Sonoran Desert of southern
Arizona. The strategy's stated purpose is deterrence; its structural
function is to produce migrant death at a distance from visible
enforcement, so that the desert does the killing rather than agents.
De León uses archaeological and forensic methods to document how the
desert environment performs violence on bodies — decomposition, animal
scavenging, scattering of remains — that destroys the evidence linking
policy to death. The policy produces death and then the terrain erases
the evidence that the policy produced death. De León calls this
post-mortem process "necroviolence." The book is simultaneously a work
of structural analysis and a record of the lived experience of those
who cross: their route knowledge, mutual aid, strategic decision-making,
and suffering within a system designed to kill them.

## Layers active

De León's work engages all six layers but is distinctive in foregrounding
the Physical & Coercive layer as primary — not as direct state violence
but as environmental violence delegated by the state.

| Layer | Role in the work |
|---|---|
| Physical & Coercive | Primary. The desert as a weapon. PTD channels migrants into terrain where heat, dehydration, exposure, and animal predation produce death. The violence is physical but delegated — performed by the environment rather than by agents, which is the structural innovation of the policy. |
| Thought & Narrative | Primary. The framing of migrant deaths as individual tragedies — bad decisions, coyote exploitation, the inherent danger of "illegal" crossing — rather than as the predictable output of a deliberate policy architecture. The spectacle of walls and patrols performs "border security" for a domestic audience while the invisible architecture produces death. |
| Surveillance & Information | Active. The state's knowledge of terrain lethality — used to design funneling corridors — versus migrants' fragmented, attrition-based knowledge of routes. Border Patrol sensor arrays and surveillance technology coexist with the deliberate absence of search-and-rescue infrastructure in the killing zones. |
| Legal & Regulatory | Active. Immigration law creates the category of "illegal" that renders migrants deportable and drives them to clandestine crossing. The legal framework converts a policy-produced death into a death caused by the victim's own illegal act. |
| Economic | Active. The economic desperation that drives migration. The smuggling economy that emerges in response to enforcement. The cost structure of crossing that forces migrants to take more dangerous routes when cheaper, safer routes are closed. |
| Institutional | Active. Border Patrol as the institution that operationalizes PTD. The medical examiner's office overwhelmed by unidentified remains. The institutional gap between policy design (which knows the death toll) and policy accountability (which does not count). |

debate rather than as structural analysis of state violence.**

**Overrepresented in training**:
- The immigration policy debate framing that treats border enforcement
  as a legitimate security question with two sides, rather than as De

- Secondary commentary that absorbs De León into "border crisis"
  narratives, treating his work as adding a humanitarian dimension to
  the immigration debate rather than as documenting a deliberate policy
  of lethal deterrence
- The "both sides" framing that balances state security concerns against
  migrant suffering, which obscures the structural argument that PTD is
  designed to produce the suffering

  the scavenging patterns, the material culture of the trail — evidence

- The perspective of families of the dead and disappeared, particularly
  from Central American and Mexican communities, whose accounts of loss
  are rarely documented in English-language sources
- The smuggling economy as experienced by both migrants and smugglers,

reproduces this erasure — I am more likely to encounter the immigration

smugglers, and migrants themselves) than De León's structural argument
(which locates primary agency in the policy architecture that funnels
people into lethal terrain). The LLM's structural position may enact
the very distribution of blame that De León identifies as part of the

### SURVEY

the Migrant Trail* (University of California Press, 2015)

TYPE: Academic monograph — anthropology / border studies / forensic
archaeology / ethnography

THESIS: The US Prevention Through Deterrence border strategy delegates
killing to the desert environment, producing migrant death at a distance
from visible enforcement. The desert then performs necroviolence —
post-mortem destruction of the body that erases evidence of the state's

LAYERS ADDRESSED: All six (Physical & Coercive, Thought & Narrative
primary)

PRINCIPLE CANDIDATE: yes — the work contains multiple generalizable
principles about how states delegate violence to environments, how
post-mortem processes erase causal chains, how the spectacle of
enforcement diverges from the architecture of control, and how agency
operates within lethal constraint. These generalize beyond the border
context to any system that produces death through structural design
while maintaining plausible deniability.

INSTRUMENT CANDIDATE: no — De León provides structural analysis and
ethnographic evidence but not a diagnostic framework that could be
formalized as a detection instrument. The concepts (necroviolence,
delegated violence, spectacle vs. architecture) are principles, not
checklists.

RATIONALE: De León's contribution to the framework is a set of
principles about how physical violence can be structurally produced
while remaining officially invisible. The delegation of killing to the
environment is the central mechanism, and it has direct parallels to
other systems where the state creates lethal conditions while
maintaining that the resulting deaths are not its responsibility.

### SYNTHESIS SUMMARY

De León's distinctive contribution to the framework is the
**delegation of state violence to the environment** — a mechanism where
the state does not kill directly but engineers the conditions under
which the environment kills, and then benefits from the environmental
destruction of evidence. This is a structural innovation in the
framework's understanding of how Physical & Coercive power operates:
the state need not wield the weapon if it can position the target in
front of one.

The extraction yields six principles and significant corroboration for
eight existing patterns. The most novel contributions are:

- **Prevention Through Deterrence as Structural Violence** (D1): the
  policy architecture that converts terrain into a weapon through the
  act of funneling
- **Necroviolence** (D2): post-mortem environmental violence that
  erases the causal chain from policy to death — extends Axiom 8
  (absence as actively produced)
- **Hostile Terrain as Delegated Violence** (D3): the mechanism of
  delegating killing to the environment while maintaining plausible
  deniability
- **The Spectacle of Enforcement vs. the Architecture of Death** (D4):
  two systems serving different audiences — the spectacle serves
  domestic politics; the architecture serves enforcement

The framework connections are significant: De León provides the
strongest corroboration in the corpus for Axiom 7 (Inversion of Stated
Purpose) — the gap between "border security" and engineered lethality
is not incidental but definitional. The IC-1 flag for Axiom 9 is
important: migrants comply not because coercion is invisible but
because the alternative (staying) is worse — a third-domain extension
of the Terkel/Desmond/Ehrenreich complication.

Limitations: De León's analysis is strongest on the Physical & Coercive
and Thought & Narrative layers. The economic structures driving
migration — trade agreements, structural adjustment, labor market
demand — are present as context but not analyzed with the same
forensic precision as the crossing itself. The legal architecture of
immigration law is similarly present but secondary to the ethnographic
and forensic focus. These are not weaknesses of the book but
boundaries of its scope.

## Principles

### D1: Prevention Through Deterrence as Structural Violence

PRINCIPLE: A state can convert terrain into a weapon by channeling
targeted populations into environments where natural conditions produce
death. The policy's stated purpose is deterrence; its structural
function is to produce death at a distance from visible enforcement,
so that the killing is performed by the environment rather than by
agents of the state.

LAYERS: Physical & Coercive (primary), Thought & Narrative, Legal &
Regulatory

STATEMENT: The US Border Patrol's Prevention Through Deterrence
strategy, adopted in the 1990s, systematically closed urban crossing
points (El Paso, San Diego) and funneled undocumented border crossers
into the Sonoran Desert — terrain where summer temperatures exceed 40°C,
water is scarce, and the distance to the nearest road can exceed 100 km.
The policy's architects understood that this terrain would produce death.
The resulting deaths are not a side effect of the policy — they are the
mechanism through which deterrence is meant to operate. The desert
becomes a weapon wielded by the state through the act of not being
present.

MECHANISM: PTD operates through territorial redesign: closing accessible
routes forces crossing into lethal terrain. The state does not need to
deploy force at the point of death — it deploys force at the points of
access, and the geography does the rest. This produces two structural
effects: (1) the state is physically absent from the scene of death,
which prevents direct attribution; (2) the death appears to result from
the individual's decision to cross rather than from the policy's
decision to funnel. The policy converts state violence into apparent
individual risk.

EVIDENCE BASE: Reconstructed from training data. De León documents the
PTD strategy through Border Patrol's own planning documents, which
acknowledge that funneling migrants into hostile terrain will produce
"ichorous injuries and death." He walks the migrant trails in the
Sonoran Desert, documenting the material conditions — the discarded
water bottles, the clothing, the personal effects, and the remains of
those who did not survive. The archaeological record of the trail is
the physical evidence of the policy's output.

CONFIDENCE NOTE: Training data reconstruction. The PTD strategy is
extensively documented in academic literature and government records.
De León's contribution is the forensic and ethnographic grounding of
the structural argument — connecting the policy architecture to the
physical evidence of death on the ground. No verbatim quotes included.

GENERALIZES TO: Any system where a state or institution creates lethal
conditions by redirecting populations into dangerous environments while
maintaining that the resulting harm is a consequence of the victim's
own choices. Environmental exposure of homeless populations through
anti-camping ordinances. Industrial safety regimes that channel workers
into hazardous conditions. The mechanism applies wherever the entity
that designs the constraint is absent from the scene of harm.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Confirms Axiom 7 (stated purpose vs. actual function)
— this is the strongest example in the corpus. The stated purpose
(deterrence, border security) and the actual function (producing death
through environmental delegation) diverge so completely that the
policy's own architects acknowledged the death toll as an expected
outcome.

STATUS: observed (policy documented in government records; deaths
documented archaeologically and forensically) / inferred (the structural
argument connecting policy design to death as mechanism rather than
side effect)

### D2: Necroviolence

PRINCIPLE: Environmental processes can perform violence on the dead that
destroys the evidence of how they died and who is responsible for their
death. This post-mortem violence is not incidental to the system that
produced the death — it is structurally necessary, because it erases
the causal chain from policy to death.

LAYERS: Physical & Coercive (primary), Surveillance & Information,
Thought & Narrative

STATEMENT: The Sonoran Desert performs violence on migrant remains —
decomposition in extreme heat, animal scavenging, scattering of bones
across wide areas, sun-bleaching, and burial by sand. These processes
destroy the body's capacity to serve as evidence: they prevent
identification, obscure cause of death, and eliminate the physical
record of how many people died. The violence does not end at death; it
continues through the systematic erasure of the body as evidence. De
León coins the term "necroviolence" for this process.

MECHANISM: The post-mortem violence operates on two registers. First,
it destroys individual evidence: a body scattered by animals across a
square kilometer cannot be fully recovered, identified, or attributed
to a specific cause of death. Second, it destroys aggregate evidence:
the number of migrant deaths in the desert is systematically
undercounted because many remains are never found, and those found are
often too degraded to identify. The structural effect is that the
policy produces death and then the terrain erases the evidence that the
policy produced death.

EVIDENCE BASE: Reconstructed from training data. De León uses forensic
archaeological methods to document the decomposition process: the
stages of exposure, the patterns of animal scavenging (coyotes, vultures,
beetles), the rate at which clothing and personal effects degrade. He
documents cases where remains were found months or years after death,
so degraded that neither identity nor cause of death could be
established. He works with the Pima County Office of the Medical
Examiner, documenting the institutional burden of processing
unidentified remains and the structural undercounting of deaths.

CONFIDENCE NOTE: Training data reconstruction. The forensic evidence is
well-documented in De León's published work and in the broader forensic
anthropology literature on border deaths. No verbatim quotes included.

GENERALIZES TO: Any system where the evidence of violence is destroyed
by the same conditions that produce the violence. Industrial pollution
that degrades before it can be measured. Warfare in environments where
remains cannot be recovered. Detention conditions where death is
attributed to the detainee's pre-existing condition rather than to the
conditions of detention. The mechanism applies wherever the causal chain
between policy and death passes through a stage that degrades evidence.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: **Extends Axiom 8** (absence as significant). The
axiom states that what is absent is analytically significant. De León
demonstrates a case where absence is not merely significant but
**actively produced**: the policy creates the conditions for death, and
then the same conditions create the absence of identifiable remains.
Absence is a structural output of the system, not merely an omission.
This extends Browne's "absented presence" in a new direction — Browne
shows absence can be constitutive of a field of knowledge; De León
shows absence can be physically manufactured by the same system that
produces the violence.

STATUS: observed (forensic evidence of post-mortem decomposition and
evidence destruction) / inferred (the structural argument that
necroviolence is functionally necessary to the policy rather than
incidental)

### D3: Hostile Terrain as Delegated Violence

PRINCIPLE: A state can delegate killing to the environment, using
terrain as the instrument of violence while maintaining plausible
deniability. The environment is not a passive backdrop to the violence —
it is an active instrument of policy, selected and deployed for its
lethality.

LAYERS: Physical & Coercive (primary), Thought & Narrative, Legal &
Regulatory

STATEMENT: PTD does not merely fail to prevent migrants from entering
dangerous terrain — it deliberately channels them into it. The Sonoran
Desert was selected as the enforcement terrain precisely because of its
lethality. The delegation of killing to the environment achieves
something direct enforcement cannot: it allows the state to frame
migrant deaths as individual tragedies — poor decisions, bad luck,
coyote exploitation — rather than as the predictable output of a policy
designed to produce them. The desert kills; the state claims it only
enforces the border.

MECHANISM: The delegation operates through a structural division between
cause and effect. The state's causal contribution (closing safe routes,
funneling into lethal terrain) is separated in time and space from the
effect (death in the desert). The agent of death is the environment, not
the state. This separation allows the state to maintain that it does not
intend the deaths — it merely enforces immigration law, and those who
choose to cross do so at their own risk. The framing converts a policy
outcome into a personal choice.

EVIDENCE BASE: Reconstructed from training data. De León documents how
the Border Patrol's strategic plans explicitly identify hostile terrain
as an enforcement asset. The closure of urban crossing points in the
1990s (Operation Hold the Line in El Paso, Operation Gatekeeper in San
Diego) was accompanied by the explicit prediction that migrants would
be forced into more dangerous terrain. The resulting deaths were
anticipated, documented, and continued — the policy was not revised
when the death toll confirmed the prediction.

CONFIDENCE NOTE: Training data reconstruction. The strategic
relationship between route closure and increased desert deaths is
documented in government planning documents, academic studies, and
NGO reporting. No verbatim quotes included.

GENERALIZES TO: Environmental exposure policies (anti-camping laws that
force homeless populations into dangerous conditions). Resource
extraction that renders communities uninhabitable without direct forced
displacement. Any system where the state creates the conditions for
harm and then attributes the harm to the victim's presence in those
conditions. The mechanism is identifiable wherever the entity that
designs the constraint maintains that the resulting harm was caused by
the victim's choice rather than by the constraint.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Confirms Constitutive Violence — the border regime is
constituted through violence, and the delegation to terrain is the
specific form that violence takes when direct state killing is
politically untenable. The delegation is structurally innovative: it
allows constitutive violence to operate continuously without the
political costs of visible state killing.

STATUS: observed (policy architecture documented) / inferred (the
characterization of terrain as a deliberately selected instrument
rather than a passive condition)

### D4: The Spectacle of Enforcement vs. the Architecture of Death

PRINCIPLE: A system of control can operate two distinct structures
simultaneously — a visible spectacle that satisfies public demand and
an invisible architecture that produces the actual outcomes — with each
structure serving a different audience.

LAYERS: Thought & Narrative (primary), Physical & Coercive, Surveillance
& Information

STATEMENT: Visible border enforcement — walls, fences, patrols,
checkpoints, surveillance towers, detention facilities — coexists with
the invisible architecture of funneling into lethal terrain. The
spectacle performs "border security" for a domestic audience: it
demonstrates that the state is acting, that the border is defended, that
enforcement is robust. The architecture produces the actual enforcement
outcome: death and suffering as deterrence. The two systems serve
different audiences. The spectacle serves domestic politics — it answers
the demand for visible action. The architecture serves enforcement
strategy — it produces outcomes that the spectacle alone cannot.

MECHANISM: The spectacle and the architecture are not contradictory —
they are complementary. The spectacle concentrates enforcement visibility
at specific points (border wall segments, checkpoints, high-profile
raids). The architecture operates in the spaces between — the vast
stretches of desert where there is no wall, no patrol, and no
visibility. Public attention focuses on the spectacle; the architecture
operates in the attention deficit. The result is a system that appears
robust at the points where it is seen and lethal at the points where
it is not.

EVIDENCE BASE: Reconstructed from training data. De León juxtaposes the
high-visibility enforcement infrastructure (the fence at Nogales, the
Border Patrol stations, the processing centers) with the low-visibility
killing terrain (the desert corridors where migrants die unseen). He
documents how political rhetoric about "securing the border" refers
exclusively to the spectacle — more agents, more technology, more wall
segments — while the architecture of funneling into lethal terrain
continues to operate as designed regardless of which party controls
the government.

CONFIDENCE NOTE: Training data reconstruction. The distinction between
visible enforcement infrastructure and invisible funneling architecture
is documented across border studies literature. No verbatim quotes
included.

GENERALIZES TO: Criminal justice systems where visible trials and
sentences coexist with invisible plea-bargain machinery. Military
operations where visible troop deployments coexist with invisible drone
campaigns. Corporate governance where visible compliance structures
coexist with invisible extraction mechanisms. The mechanism applies
wherever a system operates two structures — one visible and politically
responsive, one invisible and operationally effective — serving
different audiences.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Confirms Appearance as Structural Terrain — the
visible wall and the invisible desert corridor are the two sides of the
structural terrain. The appearance of robust enforcement is the
operating environment on which the actual enforcement (funneling into
death) depends. Without the spectacle, the architecture would face
political accountability; without the architecture, the spectacle would
be merely performative.

STATUS: observed (enforcement infrastructure documented alongside
funneling corridors) / inferred (the structural argument that the two
systems are designed as complements rather than coexisting accidentally)

### D5: The Body as Evidence and Its Erasure

PRINCIPLE: When a system produces death, the bodies of the dead are
simultaneously the evidence of the system's violence and subject to
processes that destroy that evidence. The longer the body remains in
the system's operating environment, the less it can testify.

LAYERS: Physical & Coercive (primary), Surveillance & Information,
Legal & Regulatory

STATEMENT: Migrant bodies in the Sonoran Desert are simultaneously
evidence of policy violence and subject to environmental processes
that destroy that evidence. A body recovered within hours of death can
be identified, attributed a cause of death, and added to the count.
A body recovered after weeks or months of desert exposure may be
unidentifiable — no cause of death can be established, no name can be
attached, and the death may not be recorded as a border-crossing death
at all. The structural effect: the policy produces death and then the
passage of time in the policy's chosen terrain erases the evidence that
the policy produced death. Each unidentified, uncounted death makes the
policy's death toll appear smaller than it is.

MECHANISM: The erasure operates on three levels. First, individual
identity: decomposition and scavenging destroy the physical features
(face, fingerprints, soft tissue) used for identification. Second,
cause of death: exposure deaths leave no forensic signature comparable
to gunshot or stabbing; in advanced decomposition, even exposure cannot
be confirmed. Third, count: remains that cannot be identified or
attributed to border crossing are not added to any official tally. The
cumulative effect is systematic undercounting — the official death toll
is a lower bound, not an estimate.

EVIDENCE BASE: Reconstructed from training data. De León works with
the Pima County Office of the Medical Examiner, which processes
unidentified remains from the desert. He documents the institutional
challenge: hundreds of unidentified remains in storage, families
searching for missing relatives who may never be matched to remains,
forensic anthropologists attempting identification from skeletal
fragments. He also documents the material culture of the trail —
personal effects (photographs, letters, rosaries) that survive longer
than the bodies they accompanied, serving as traces of persons the
desert has otherwise erased.

CONFIDENCE NOTE: Training data reconstruction. The systematic
undercounting of border deaths is documented by forensic researchers,
NGOs (the Colibrí Center, the ACLU), and the medical examiner's own
reporting. No verbatim quotes included.

GENERALIZES TO: Industrial accidents in environments that destroy
evidence (deep-sea drilling, mining). Wartime disappearances in
environments that prevent body recovery. Institutional deaths
(detention, psychiatric facilities) where institutional control of
the body after death determines what evidence survives. The mechanism
applies wherever the system that produces death also controls the
conditions under which evidence of that death persists or is destroyed.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends Division of Learning — the state knows (from
its own planning documents) that the terrain will produce deaths and
that the terrain will degrade evidence. Migrants learn this through
attrition — those who survive share knowledge of the dangers, but the
knowledge is always partial and lagging. The information asymmetry is
structural: the state designed the system; migrants discover it through
suffering.

STATUS: observed (forensic evidence of evidence erasure) / inferred
(the structural argument that erasure is functionally necessary to the
policy rather than incidental)

### D6: Migrant Agency Within Lethal Constraint

PRINCIPLE: Populations subjected to lethal structural constraint are
not passive victims — they develop route knowledge, share survival
information, create mutual aid networks, and make strategic decisions
within the system designed to kill them. But this agency operates
within a constraint architecture that is designed to be total: the
agency is real; the architecture is lethal; navigate or die.

LAYERS: Physical & Coercive, Thought & Narrative, Surveillance &
Information, Economic

STATEMENT: De León documents migrants as strategic actors: they
develop knowledge of terrain, water sources, and Border Patrol patrol
patterns. They share information through social networks. They form
groups for mutual protection. They carry supplies calibrated to the
distance they expect to cover. They make decisions about when to walk,
when to rest, when to abandon a route, when to call for rescue. This
agency is real and consequential — it determines who survives. But it
operates within a system designed to produce death: the route options
are all dangerous, the terrain is hostile, the distance is calibrated
to be at the limit of human endurance, and the state has deliberately
closed the routes where survival would be more likely.

MECHANISM: The agency-within-constraint operates as a variant of the
Compliance Gradient. Migrants are not choosing to comply or resist —
they are navigating a system in which all available options have been
constrained to lethal or near-lethal alternatives. The "choice" to
cross the desert is not free — it is the residual option after the
policy has closed all safer alternatives. Within the desert, the
choices (which route, how much water, how fast to walk, whether to
continue or turn back) are genuine decisions with life-or-death
consequences, but they are made within a constraint architecture
designed by the state. The agency is real. The constraint is total.
The combination is the Compliance Gradient under lethal conditions.

EVIDENCE BASE: Reconstructed from training data. De León documents
migrant decision-making through ethnographic fieldwork — interviews
with migrants before, during, and after crossing attempts. He
participates in the crossing experience as an observer. He documents
the material culture of preparation (backpacks, water jugs, food,
dark clothing) and of abandonment (discarded supplies along the trail
marking the points where people could no longer carry them). He records
the knowledge networks: the information passed between migrants about
routes, dangers, and Border Patrol patterns.

CONFIDENCE NOTE: Training data reconstruction. The ethnographic evidence
of migrant agency and strategy is well-documented in De León's
published work and in the broader migration studies literature. No
verbatim quotes included.

GENERALIZES TO: Workers navigating unsafe workplaces (Terkel, Ehrenreich).
Tenants navigating eviction-prone housing markets (Desmond). Prisoners
navigating carceral systems. Refugees navigating camp bureaucracies.
The mechanism applies wherever a population exercises real agency within
a constraint architecture designed to produce harm — the agency does
not negate the constraint, and the constraint does not negate the
agency.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Confirms and extends the Compliance Gradient. The
gradient as described by Scott operates between compliance and
resistance within a stable power relationship. De León extends it to
the lethal limit: when the cost of non-compliance is not punishment but
death, the gradient collapses to its most extreme form — navigate or
die. This is the Compliance Gradient from Position 2 under the
conditions described by Position 3 (constitutive violence).

**IC-1 flag for Axiom 9**: Migrants are aware of the danger, see the
coercion clearly, and cross anyway because the alternative (staying)
is worse. Compliance is not manufactured through invisibility or
through soft mechanisms — it is produced by the structural constraint
of having no viable alternative. This is a third-domain extension of
the Terkel/Desmond/Ehrenreich complication of Axiom 9: coercion is
visible; compliance results from the prohibitive cost of the
alternative, not from the concealment of coercion. In the workplace,
the cost is unemployment; in housing, homelessness; at the border,
continued immiseration in the country of origin versus possible death
in the desert. The mechanism is the same: compliance under visible
coercion because the cost of refusal is prohibitive.

STATUS: observed (ethnographic evidence of migrant strategy and
decision-making) / inferred (the structural argument about
agency-within-lethal-constraint as a variant of the Compliance Gradient)

## Patterns identified

### Existing patterns corroborated

**Inversion of Stated Purpose** (ESTABLISHED): De León provides the
strongest corroboration in the corpus. "Border security" is the stated
purpose; engineered lethality is the function. The inversion is not
incidental — the policy's own architects acknowledged that funneling
into hostile terrain would produce "ichorous injuries and death." The
gap between stated purpose and actual function is definitional: the
policy is named for what it claims to do (prevent through deterrence)
while its mechanism is to produce death through environmental
delegation. This is not a case where the stated purpose is partially
true — deterrence through death is a different function than security.

**Appearance as Structural Terrain** (ESTABLISHED): Visible walls,
patrols, and checkpoints create the appearance of robust enforcement.
The invisible funneling architecture — the deliberate channeling of
migrants into lethal terrain — operates behind this appearance. The
appearance satisfies the domestic demand for "border security." The
architecture produces the actual enforcement outcome. The gap between
appearance and operation is the operating environment: without the
appearance, the architecture would face scrutiny; without the
architecture, the appearance would be merely performative.

**Constitutive Violence** (SUPPORTED): The US-Mexico border regime is
constituted through violence — the border itself was established through
the Mexican-American War (1846-1848) and the Treaty of Guadalupe
Hidalgo. PTD extends the constitutive violence: the border is not
merely maintained by the threat of force but actively produces death
as an enforcement mechanism. The delegation to terrain is the
contemporary form of constitutive violence where direct state killing
is politically untenable.

**Economy of Violence** (PRELIMINARY): Violence is concentrated at the
moment of crossing — the desert corridor is the killing zone. Those who
survive the crossing enter a different regime (workplace exploitation,
legal precarity, but not active environmental lethality). The temporal
calibration matches the pattern: overwhelming violence at the point of
acquisition (entering the territory), relative stability afterward.
The desert functions as the moat — lethal at the threshold, absent
once inside.

**Hidden Transcript** (SUPPORTED): Migrant knowledge networks —
information about routes, dangers, Border Patrol patterns, water
sources, smuggler reliability — constitute a hidden transcript specific
to the border-crossing population. This counter-knowledge circulates
through informal channels (social networks, word of mouth, migrant
shelters) and is invisible to official accounts of border enforcement.
De León documents how this knowledge is produced, shared, and updated
through the experience of crossing and through the testimony of
returnees.

**Compliance Gradient** (PRELIMINARY): Extended to lethal conditions.
Migration as navigating the gradient under maximum constraint: the
"choices" available (which route, how much water, when to rest) are
genuine but operate within a constraint architecture designed to
produce death. The gradient collapses to its extreme form — navigate
or die — when the cost of non-compliance is not punishment but the
absence of any survivable alternative.

**Division of Learning** (ESTABLISHED): The information asymmetry
between the state and migrants is structural and deliberate. The state
knows the funneling architecture — it designed it. The state knows the
terrain lethality — it selected the corridors for that reason. The
state knows the death toll — or could know it if it chose to count
fully. Migrants learn the terrain through attrition: those who survive
share knowledge, but the knowledge is always partial, localized, and
lagging. The division is compounded by the state's surveillance
infrastructure (sensors, cameras, drones) which monitors the corridors
while providing no rescue capability. The state sees the migrants; the
migrants cannot see the state's design.

**Layered Redundancy** (ESTABLISHED): Multiple control mechanisms
operate simultaneously. Legal prohibition (immigration law) produces
the category of illegal entry. Physical enforcement (patrols,
checkpoints, walls) closes accessible routes. Environmental lethality
(the desert corridor) produces death and deterrence. Narrative framing
("border security," "illegal alien") legitimizes the architecture.
Surveillance (sensors, cameras, drones) monitors without rescuing. No
single layer is sufficient alone; together they constitute a redundant
system where failure at any one layer is caught by the others.

### New pattern candidates

No new standalone pattern proposed. De León's contributions extend and
corroborate existing patterns rather than establishing genuinely new
cross-cutting structures. The most significant extensions are:

- **Economy of Violence**: border crossing as the paradigmatic case of
  violence concentrated at the threshold of acquisition
- **Compliance Gradient**: extended to lethal conditions where the
  gradient collapses to navigate-or-die
- **Inversion of Stated Purpose**: strongest corroboration in the
  corpus — the inversion is definitional, not incidental

### Circumvention connections

De León documents limited but real circumvention practices:

- **Everyday Resistance**: Migrant route knowledge, supply preparation,
  mutual aid networks, strategic decision-making within lethal
  constraint — the daily practices of survival that do not challenge
  the system but navigate within it
- **Cultural Production**: De León's own work — the book itself is a
  naming act, using forensic and ethnographic evidence to make visible
  what the policy is designed to render invisible (the causal chain
  from policy to death). The Undocumented Migration Project's public
  exhibitions serve the same function
- **Transparency / Naming**: NGO documentation of border deaths
  (the Colibrí Center, Humane Borders water stations, No More Deaths).
  These naming acts make the death toll visible and attributable, but
  have not produced policy change — confirming the conditional in
  Axiom 10 (naming requires leverage and audience to take effect)

### Axiom connections

|---|---|
| Axiom 7: Stated purpose vs. function | **STRONGEST CORROBORATION IN CORPUS.** The stated purpose (deterrence, border security) and the actual function (producing death through environmental delegation) diverge so completely that the policy's own architects acknowledged the death toll as an expected outcome. This is not a case of partial divergence — the system does something categorically different from what it claims to do. |

The policy creates the conditions for death, and then the desert creates the absence of identifiable remains. Absence is a structural output of the system — manufactured, not merely occurring. Extends Browne's "absented presence" in a new direction: from constitutive omission in knowledge (Browne) to physical manufacture of absence through environmental destruction of evidence (De León). |
| Axiom 9: Manufactured compliance | **IC-1 FLAG — third-domain complication.** Migrants are not unaware of the coercion — they see the danger clearly, understand the policy architecture through experience, and cross anyway because the alternative (remaining in conditions of poverty, violence, or immiseration) is worse. Compliance is not manufactured through invisibility; it is produced by the structural constraint of having no viable alternative. ### Principle connections

- **Terkel T4 — The Cost of Refusal**
  (principles/terkel-working.md): De León extends from workplace to
  border. Terkel's workers comply because the cost of refusal
  (unemployment) is prohibitive. De León's migrants cross because the
  cost of refusal (remaining in conditions of poverty and violence) is
  prohibitive. The mechanism is identical: visible coercion, rational
  compliance, because all alternatives are worse. The difference is
  degree — at the border, the cost of compliance includes possible
  death, making it the most extreme case in the corpus.

- **Desmond D3 — Eviction as Cascading Structural Failure**
  (principles/desmond-evicted.md): Both document how a system produces
  harm through structural conditions rather than through direct
  violence, and how the harm cascades (eviction leads to job loss,
  school disruption, health decline; desert crossing leads to injury,
  death, family separation, trauma). The structural cascade operates
  differently at each scale but the mechanism — system-produced harm
  attributed to individual failure — is the same.

- **Ehrenreich E2 — Entry Conditions as Pre-Defeat**
  (principles/ehrenreich-nickel-and-dimed.md): The conditions of
  crossing are the entry conditions — migrants begin the crossing
  already exhausted, dehydrated, financially depleted by smuggler fees,
  and carrying insufficient supplies for the distance. The pre-defeat
  is structural: the policy has designed the crossing to begin from a
  position of deficit.

- **Browne B6 — Absented Presence**
  (principles/browne-dark-matters.md): Both Browne and De León
  document systems where absence is actively produced rather than
  accidentally occurring. Browne identifies the constitutive omission
  of slavery from surveillance studies' genealogy. De León identifies
  the physical manufacture of absence — the desert destroys the
  bodies that would serve as evidence of the policy's violence. The
  mechanism operates on different registers (epistemological vs.
  physical) but the structural logic is the same: the system depends
  on the absence it produces.

- **Fanon F1 — Violence as Constitutive**
  (principles/fanon-wretched-of-the-earth.md): The border regime is
  constituted through violence (the Mexican-American War, the Treaty
  of Guadalupe Hidalgo). PTD extends constitutive violence into the
  present through environmental delegation — the violence is ongoing,
  not merely foundational. De León adds the mechanism of delegation:
  constitutive violence can be maintained without direct state killing
  by engineering the conditions under which the environment kills.

- **Arendt A2 — The Boomerang Thesis**
  (principles/arendt-origins-of-totalitarianism.md): PTD can be read
  as a variant of the Boomerang: techniques of environmental lethality
  developed at the border (a zone of exception where constitutional
  protections are attenuated) have structural parallels to techniques
  applied domestically (anti-homeless ordinances that create
  environmental exposure, environmental racism that concentrates
  pollutants in minority communities). The border is the laboratory.

## Source metadata

SOURCE TYPE: training_data
POSITION: Hybrid 2/5 — anthropologist (UCLA, later Harvard) who walked
migrant trails, documented from both academic and participant-observer
perspective. De León is not a migrant, but his method — years of
fieldwork physically present on the trails, working with migrants and
with the remains of those who died — places him closer to Position 2
than a typical Position 5 academic. His ethnographic method centers
migrant voices and experiences while his forensic method documents the
physical evidence the system produces.

WHAT THIS POSITION MAKES VISIBLE: The physical reality of the crossing
— the material conditions, the bodily experience, the forensic evidence
of death. The gap between policy rhetoric and physical outcome. The
strategic agency of migrants within the lethal system.

WHAT THIS POSITION MAKES INVISIBLE: The policy-making process from the
inside (Position 1). The economic structures driving migration at the
macro level. The experience of those who never attempt the crossing —
those deterred, those who cannot afford the smuggler fee, those whose
deaths went completely undocumented.
