## IC-5 disclosure

My training data likely overrepresents the quantitative-institutional
perspective on institutional investor housing purchases. The academic
paper uses administrative data (deed records, Census, ACS) and
econometric methods (two-stage least squares with shift-share
instruments). This is a Position 5 analysis of a Position 2 experience:
the lived reality of Black families outbid by institutional investors,
displaced from homeownership, and subjected to declining property values
and rising crime is not represented in the paper's methodology. The
tenants and would-be homebuyers do not speak. Their structural position
is measured but not narrated. Grassroots accounts from Atlanta-area
housing organizers, tenant unions, and displaced families would provide
the Position 2 complement this extraction lacks.

The companion paper (Polimeni and An) uses similar administrative methods
to map corporate SFR portfolios and estimate community wealth loss.
Neither paper captures the experience of paying rent to an institutional
landlord who has no stake in the community, or the generational
consequence of being locked out of wealth-building through homeownership.

### SYNTHESIS SUMMARY

**Novel contribution**: This is the framework's first quantitative-
empirical source documenting the racial mechanics of institutional
investor housing acquisition at scale. Where Desmond (*Evicted*)
documents the micro-level dynamics of housing extraction from the poor,
An documents the macro-level dynamics of housing extraction from Black
communities by institutional capital. Where Browne (*Dark Matters*)
theorizes racializing surveillance, An provides quantitative evidence
of racializing investment — the same structural logic applied through
capital deployment rather than monitoring. Where Cobb (*Most Southern
Place*) documents the racial wage as a cross-class alliance prevention
mechanism in the agrarian South, An documents its contemporary
equivalent: institutional investors extract from Black neighborhoods
specifically, while white homeownership rates remain unaffected,
preventing cross-racial solidarity on housing.

**Confirmatory contribution**: Corroborates Manufactured Scarcity as
Control (institutional investors absorb affordable starter homes,
creating scarcity for first-time buyers), Division of Learning
(investors use technology and data analytics to identify and acquire
properties before individual buyers can compete), Constitutive Violence
(the racial targeting builds on the constitutive dispossession of
redlining, blockbusting, and urban renewal — each new form of extraction
operates on terrain created by the last), Formal Transformation/
Structural Continuity (the form shifts from redlining to blockbusting
to subprime lending to institutional investor acquisition, but the
structural extraction from Black communities persists), Compliance
Gradient (Black families cannot resist institutional investors'
all-cash, above-asking offers), and Moral Infrastructure of Economic
Power (market framing presents racially targeted investment as
race-neutral "market efficiency").

**Limitations**: Atlanta metropolitan area, 2007-2016. The post-
foreclosure crisis period may be structurally unique (institutional
investors entered through Fannie Mae REO bulk sales — a specific policy
channel). The causal identification relies on a shift-share instrument
(national investor interest interacted with local foreclosure exposure)
that identifies average effects, not mechanisms for individual
transactions. The $4 billion wealth loss figure and specific percentage-
point estimates are from training data reconstruction and should be
treated as approximate until verified against the published paper.

## Principles

### P1: Racially Targeted Capital Deployment

**PRINCIPLE**: Institutional investors in single-family homes
systematically target neighborhoods with above-average Black populations.
Investors are approximately twice as likely to purchase in majority-Black
neighborhoods as in comparable white neighborhoods, controlling for
income, housing stock, and other neighborhood characteristics. This
targeting is not incidental — it reflects a structural logic where
post-crisis Black neighborhoods offer the combination of depressed
prices, high rental demand, and weak competitive resistance that
maximizes investor returns.

LAYERS: Economic, Surveillance & Information
MECHANISM: Institutional investors use technology-enabled property
identification, data analytics, and all-cash offers to acquire
single-family homes at scale. The targeting of Black neighborhoods is
structurally overdetermined: (1) the 2008 foreclosure crisis hit Black
neighborhoods disproportionately, producing concentrated distressed
inventory; (2) Black homeownership rates were already lower due to
historical redlining, lending discrimination, and wealth gaps, meaning
less competitive resistance from owner-occupant buyers; (3) high rental
demand in these neighborhoods ensures revenue; (4) lower acquisition
costs maximize return on investment. The racial concentration is not the
result of individual racial animus but of capital following the path of
least resistance through terrain shaped by prior racial dispossession.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: In the Atlanta metropolitan area, 2007-2016,
institutional investors purchased approximately 7% of all single-family
homes sold. This investment was concentrated in neighborhoods with
above-average Black populations. Investors were roughly twice as likely
to purchase in these neighborhoods compared to otherwise similar
neighborhoods with lower Black populations. The causal identification
uses a shift-share instrument to rule out reverse causation and
confounding.
CROSS-REFERENCE: Browne (Dark Matters) P1 — Racializing Surveillance.
Browne documents how surveillance technologies are deployed
differentially along racial lines. An documents how investment capital
is deployed differentially along the same lines. The structural logic
is parallel: both identify Black populations as targets for extraction
(surveillance data or housing wealth) through technologies that present
themselves as race-neutral.
GENERALIZES TO: Any capital deployment that follows the contours of
prior racial dispossession — gentrification investment, payday lending
concentration, insurance redlining, food desert economics. The mechanism
is capital exploiting vulnerabilities that prior structural racism
created.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends Constitutive Violence (each wave of racial
extraction operates on terrain constituted by the previous wave);
extends Division of Learning (technology creates asymmetric information
advantage for institutional investors over individual homebuyers)
STATUS: observed (quantitative — measured with causal identification)

### P2: Racially Differential Displacement from Homeownership

**PRINCIPLE**: Concentrated institutional investor purchases reduce
homeownership rates, but the effect falls almost entirely on Black
households. In the Atlanta metropolitan area, institutional investor
activity produced a 4.2 percentage point decline in Black homeownership
with no statistically significant effect on white homeownership. This
is not a race-neutral market effect experienced differently — it is a
structurally racial outcome.

LAYERS: Economic, Institutional
MECHANISM: Institutional investors compete directly with first-time
homebuyers for affordable starter homes. Their advantages — all-cash
offers, speed of closing, bulk purchasing power, algorithmic property
identification — make them systematically more competitive than
individual buyers. But the displacement is racially concentrated
because: (1) Black first-time buyers have lower average down payments
and less access to mortgage credit, making them less competitive against
cash offers; (2) the targeted neighborhoods are disproportionately
Black; (3) the conversion of ownership stock to rental stock in these
neighborhoods reduces the supply of ownership-accessible housing
specifically where Black buyers are looking. White homeownership rates
are unaffected because white buyers operate in neighborhoods and price
segments where institutional investors are less concentrated.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: An's two-stage least squares estimation finds a
1.4 percentage point decline in overall homeownership rates attributable
to institutional investor activity in metro Atlanta, 2007-2016. When
disaggregated by race, the effect is a 4.2 percentage point decline
in Black homeownership and no significant effect on white
homeownership. The racial differential is the central empirical finding.
CROSS-REFERENCE: Cobb (Most Southern Place) P3 — Racial Wage as
Cross-Class Alliance Prevention. Cobb documents how the racial wage
structure prevented poor white and Black workers from recognizing shared
class interests. An documents the contemporary equivalent: white
homeownership is unharmed by institutional investors, preventing
cross-racial solidarity on housing policy. The racial targeting
functions as a wedge — white homeowners have no material reason to
oppose institutional investors because the costs fall on Black
homeownership alone.
GENERALIZES TO: Any market intervention whose costs are racially
concentrated while benefits (or neutrality) are racially distributed —
highway construction through Black neighborhoods, school funding tied
to property values, criminal justice costs borne by specific
communities. The mechanism is structural racial specificity that
prevents cross-racial coalition formation.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: confirms Manufactured Scarcity as Control (affordable
homeownership made scarce for Black families specifically); confirms
Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity (the form of extraction
changes — redlining to subprime to institutional investment — but the
extraction from Black homeownership wealth persists)
STATUS: observed (quantitative — measured with causal identification)

### P3: Community Wealth Extraction Through Institutional Ownership Concentration

**PRINCIPLE**: Concentrated institutional ownership of single-family
homes extracts wealth from communities through multiple simultaneous
channels: conversion of ownership-accessible stock to rental stock
(blocking wealth accumulation), depression of nearby property values
(destroying existing wealth), and rent extraction that flows to
distant corporate entities rather than circulating locally. The
Polimeni and An companion paper estimates that Black families lost more
than $4 billion in home equity over the study period.

LAYERS: Economic, Institutional
MECHANISM: The wealth extraction operates through three concurrent
channels. First, homes converted from owner-occupied to investor-owned
rental no longer build equity for occupant households — rental payments
flow to corporate entities headquartered elsewhere. Second, concentrated
institutional ownership depresses property values in surrounding homes,
destroying the wealth of existing homeowners — An finds this effect
is concentrated almost exclusively in Black neighborhoods. Third, the
reduction in homeownership rates reduces political participation and
community investment, further weakening the neighborhood's capacity to
resist continued extraction. The mechanism is self-reinforcing:
extraction degrades the community, degradation reduces resistance,
reduced resistance enables further extraction.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Polimeni and An map corporate SFR portfolios at the
neighborhood level and document community wealth loss from concentrated
institutional ownership. An finds investor purchases are associated with
decline in property values for nearby homes, with this effect
concentrated in Black neighborhoods. Associated effects include
increases in property crime (2%), violent crime (3%), and drug-related
crime (9%), and reduced political participation.
CROSS-REFERENCE: Desmond (Evicted) P1 — Profit Extraction from Poverty.
Desmond documents how landlords extract profit from poor tenants'
desperation. An documents the macro-level equivalent: institutional
investors extract wealth from Black communities' structural
vulnerability. The mechanism scales from individual landlord-tenant
extraction (Desmond) to corporate-community extraction (An), but the
structural logic is identical — vulnerability created by prior
dispossession becomes the source of profit.
GENERALIZES TO: Any institutional investment pattern that extracts
wealth from communities while degrading the conditions that would
enable resistance — resource extraction in developing economies, private
equity acquisition of local businesses, financialization of public
services. The mechanism is extraction-degradation-extraction as a
self-reinforcing cycle.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: confirms Peacetime Ratchet (in the absence of
catastrophic disruption, wealth flows from Black communities to
institutional investors as a structural default); extends Constitutive
Violence (the $4 billion wealth loss is a contemporary instance of
racially constitutive economic violence)
STATUS: observed (quantitative, with caveat that the $4 billion figure
is from training data reconstruction)

### P4: Technology as Racial Extraction Accelerant

**PRINCIPLE**: Institutional investors use technology — algorithmic
property identification, automated valuation models, bulk data
acquisition, remote transaction processing — to acquire properties at
a speed and scale that individual homebuyers cannot match. The
technology does not create the racial targeting but accelerates and
scales it, converting what would otherwise be individual opportunistic
purchases into systematic extraction.

LAYERS: Surveillance & Information, Economic
MECHANISM: Technology creates a structural Division of Learning between
institutional investors and individual homebuyers. Investors access
proprietary databases of distressed properties, foreclosure filings,
and neighborhood characteristics. Automated systems identify acquisition
targets meeting return thresholds. All-cash offers close within days,
before mortgage-dependent buyers can compete. The technology is
formally race-neutral — algorithms target return-on-investment metrics,
not race. But because the terrain was shaped by racial dispossession
(foreclosure concentration in Black neighborhoods, lower property
values, high rental demand), race-neutral optimization produces racially
concentrated extraction. The algorithm does not need to see race; it
sees the financial signature of racial dispossession.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: An documents that institutional investors use
technology and all-cash offers to outcompete individual first-time
homebuyers for the same affordable starter homes. The speed and
information advantages are structural, not incidental.
CROSS-REFERENCE: Browne (Dark Matters) P1 — Racializing Surveillance.
Browne's concept of "prototypical whiteness as design default" applies
here: the technology was designed for profit maximization, but the
default condition of profitability was shaped by racial history. The
algorithm is the contemporary equivalent of the slave patrol's lantern
(Browne): a technology that performs a formally neutral function
(illumination/optimization) while operating within a racial structure
that determines who is illuminated/targeted.
CROSS-REFERENCE: Zuboff (Surveillance Capitalism) — Division of
Learning. Zuboff documents the asymmetry where one party learns about
another who cannot see the data being collected or how it is used. An
documents the same asymmetry in housing: institutional investors access
data about neighborhoods, properties, and market conditions that
individual buyers cannot see or process at scale.
GENERALIZES TO: Any technology that optimizes within a terrain shaped
by prior structural inequality — credit scoring algorithms that
reproduce redlining, hiring algorithms trained on historical data,
predictive policing that targets already-surveilled communities. The
mechanism is formally neutral technology operating on structurally
non-neutral terrain to produce and scale racial disparity.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends Division of Learning (technology creates
structural knowledge asymmetry that scales racial extraction);
extends Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity (the technology
is the new form; the racial extraction is the continuing structure)
STATUS: inferred (the technology advantage is documented; the specific
algorithms and data systems used by institutional investors are not
detailed in the paper)

### P5: The Post-Crisis Regulatory Channel

**PRINCIPLE**: The post-2008 regulatory environment created a specific
channel for institutional entry into single-family housing. Fannie Mae's
bulk sale of REO (real estate owned) properties to institutional
investors, relaxed regulations on investor purchases, and federal policy
designed to stabilize housing markets by clearing distressed inventory
created the structural conditions for institutional acquisition at
scale. The regulatory response to the crisis — designed to restore
market function — enabled a new form of extraction from the communities
most damaged by the crisis.

LAYERS: Legal & Regulatory, Institutional, Economic
MECHANISM: The 2008 foreclosure crisis produced massive distressed
inventory concentrated in Black and low-income neighborhoods. Federal
policy responded by facilitating bulk sales of foreclosed properties to
institutional investors — Fannie Mae's REO-to-rental program sold
thousands of homes in bulk to corporate buyers. The stated purpose was
market stabilization: clear distressed inventory, restore property
values, provide rental housing. The structural function was to transfer
housing stock from individual (largely Black) owners who had been
foreclosed upon to institutional investors who would extract rental
income. The regulatory architecture that enabled this transfer was
designed at the institutional layer and operated through the legal layer,
producing economic consequences at the community layer.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: An documents that institutional investor activity
in the Atlanta metro area began accelerating after the foreclosure
crisis and that the targeting of Black neighborhoods correlates with
the concentration of foreclosure distress. The regulatory channel
(Fannie Mae bulk sales, favorable financing terms for bulk investors)
is documented in the broader literature on institutional SFR investment.
CROSS-REFERENCE: hooks (Ain't I a Woman) P5 — Structural Effects
Inverted as Blame. The framing of post-crisis institutional investment
as "market stabilization" inverts the structural effect: the policy
response to a crisis that disproportionately harmed Black homeowners
enabled a new form of extraction from the same communities, while
presenting itself as recovery assistance.
GENERALIZES TO: Any regulatory response to crisis that creates channels
for institutional extraction from the populations most harmed by the
crisis — post-disaster reconstruction contracts, bankruptcy law
enabling corporate asset acquisition, deregulation following financial
instability. The mechanism is crisis-response regulation that
facilitates extraction while presenting as recovery.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: confirms Inversion of Stated Purpose (stated: market
stabilization; function: transfer of housing stock from foreclosed
homeowners to institutional investors); confirms Capture Pipeline
(regulatory architecture shaped by institutional investor interests)
STATUS: inferred (the regulatory channel is documented in the broader
literature; An's paper focuses on the effects rather than the policy
architecture)

## Key patterns corroborated

### Manufactured Scarcity as Control
CORROBORATION: Institutional investors purchase affordable starter homes
— the same housing stock targeted by first-time homebuyers. This
absorption of supply creates artificial scarcity in the homeownership-
accessible segment of the market. The scarcity is racially concentrated:
institutional purchases are concentrated in Black neighborhoods,
creating scarcity specifically where Black first-time buyers are looking.
The result is not a general housing shortage but a targeted reduction in
homeownership-accessible housing for Black families.
NOTE: Extends the pattern from Desmond (individual landlord-created
scarcity) to institutional-investor-created scarcity operating at
neighborhood scale. The mechanism shifts from withholding maintenance
to absorbing supply, but the structural effect — captive market,
blocked alternatives, continued extraction — is the same.

### Division of Learning
CORROBORATION: Institutional investors operate with a structural
information advantage: proprietary databases, algorithmic property
identification, automated valuation models, bulk transaction
infrastructure. Individual homebuyers — especially first-time buyers in
Black neighborhoods — cannot access this information or compete with the
speed it enables. The knowledge asymmetry is self-reinforcing: more
acquisitions produce more data, which produces better targeting, which
produces more acquisitions.
NOTE: Extends the pattern from digital surveillance (Zuboff) and tenant
screening databases (Desmond) to real estate acquisition technology. The
Division of Learning here operates as a competitive weapon that
displaces individual buyers through structural information asymmetry.

### Constitutive Violence
CORROBORATION: The racial targeting of institutional investors is not a
novel act of dispossession but the latest iteration in a sequence:
slavery → redlining → blockbusting → subprime lending → post-crisis
institutional acquisition. Each iteration operates on terrain
constituted by the previous one. Institutional investors target Black
neighborhoods because those neighborhoods were damaged by the
foreclosure crisis; the foreclosure crisis hit those neighborhoods
because subprime lending was concentrated there; subprime lending was
concentrated there because redlining had blocked conventional credit;
redlining was concentrated there because segregation had concentrated
Black populations. The $4 billion in wealth loss is constitutive
violence — not in the sense of initial founding violence but as the
continuing operation of a structure founded on prior dispossession.
NOTE: The quantitative evidence makes this corroboration distinctive.
This is not a theoretical claim about constitutive violence — it is a
measured wealth transfer from Black communities to institutional
investors, traceable through the chain of prior dispossession.

### Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity
CORROBORATION: The form of extraction from Black homeownership wealth
has changed across each era — legal exclusion from property (slavery),
spatial exclusion from credit (redlining), predatory inclusion in
credit (subprime), displacement from ownership (institutional investor
acquisition). The forms are radically different. The structure — wealth
extraction from Black communities through the housing market — persists.
Each formal transformation is accompanied by a new legitimating
vocabulary: "property rights," "risk assessment," "market
stabilization," "housing market efficiency." The celebrations of
progress (end of redlining, fair lending laws, crisis recovery) mark
the points of formal transformation, not structural discontinuity.
NOTE: An's quantitative evidence provides a contemporary data point in
a sequence that Cobb (Most Southern Place) traces through the agrarian
period and Browne (Dark Matters) traces through surveillance. The
structural continuity across all three sources — economic extraction
from Black communities — is documented across different eras, different
forms, and different methods.

### Compliance Gradient
CORROBORATION: Black first-time homebuyers cannot resist institutional
investors' structural advantages (all-cash offers, speed of closing,
bulk purchasing power). Their compliance is not with an explicit
command but with a market structure that forecloses their alternatives.
The gradient runs from full compliance (renting from the institutional
investor that bought the home they wanted) through partial resistance
(searching in less-targeted neighborhoods, accepting longer commutes)
to structural exit (leaving the metro area). At no point does open
resistance become viable — there is no entity to resist, no demand to
refuse, only a market structure that produces the outcome.
NOTE: Extends the pattern from workplace (Terkel, Braverman),
housing-tenant (Desmond), and border (De León) contexts to
housing-buyer context. The compliance gradient here is distinctive
because the coercing agent is diffuse (institutional capital) rather
than identifiable (landlord, employer, border patrol).

### Moral Infrastructure of Economic Power
CORROBORATION: The market framing of institutional investor activity
presents racially targeted investment as race-neutral "market
efficiency." Investors are "providing rental housing," "stabilizing
neighborhoods," "clearing distressed inventory." The moral vocabulary
converts wealth extraction into market service. Black families who
lose homeownership opportunities are framed not as victims of
structural extraction but as market participants who were outcompeted
— the moral infrastructure converting structural dispossession into
individual market failure.
NOTE: Extends the Desmond observation (tenants blamed for housing
instability) to the homeownership-displacement context. The moral
vocabulary operates identically: structural extraction reframed as
individual competitive failure within a neutral market.

## IC-1 flags

### Axiom 3 complication: visible mechanism, structural racial channeling

Axiom 3 describes power as most effective when its contingency is
invisible. Institutional investor targeting of Black neighborhoods is
visible — the purchases are public record, the racial concentration is
measurable, the mechanisms are documented in academic research. The
contingency is also visible — these are policy choices (Fannie Mae bulk
sales, regulatory frameworks) that could be otherwise. Yet the
mechanism operates effectively despite visibility because it is
channeled through the market, which naturalizes its outcomes. The
framing "investors are rational market actors responding to price
signals" renders the racial targeting contingent yet hegemonically
naturalized — the background framework within which the targeting is
assessed (market efficiency, property rights, investor returns) is
itself a product of the power arrangement that produced the racialized
terrain. This is Axiom 3's fifth mode (hegemonic naturalization)
operating through market ideology.

### Axiom 9 complication: compliance without identifiable coercer

Axiom 9 describes compliance manufactured without explicit coercion.
An's findings show compliance manufactured without an identifiable
coercing agent. Black first-time homebuyers do not comply with a demand
— they are structurally displaced by market forces. There is no landlord
to resist (Desmond), no employer to confront (Terkel), no border patrol
to evade (De León). The coercion is structural and diffuse:
institutional capital, acting through thousands of individual
transactions, produces the aggregate outcome. This extends the Axiom 9
complication from visible coercion by identifiable agents (workplace,
housing, border) to structural coercion by diffuse capital. Seventh
independent source confirming Axiom 9 operates through structural
constraint, not concealment — but here the constraint is additionally
agent-less.

### Axiom 10 complication: understanding documented, no leverage point

An's research makes the mechanism fully visible — quantitatively
documented, causally identified, published in a peer-reviewed journal.
The understanding is complete. Yet the mechanism continues because
there is no leverage point for the understanding to act upon.
Individual Black homebuyers cannot out-compete institutional investors.
Collective action faces a diffuse target (thousands of corporate
entities, not one landlord or employer). Policy intervention requires
political will that the racial specificity of the harm undermines
(white homeownership is unaffected, so cross-racial coalition pressure
is structurally absent). This is the Fisher condition (reflexive
impotence) applied to racial extraction: the knowledge is complete,
the paralysis is structural.

## IC-2 null case

**Could non-power explanations account for these findings?**

The strongest null case is pure market efficiency: institutional
investors target neighborhoods with the best risk-adjusted returns, and
post-crisis Black neighborhoods happen to offer those returns due to
depressed prices and high rental demand. The racial concentration is an
artifact of price geography, not racial targeting.

This null case is weakened by An's methodology. The shift-share
instrument identifies the causal effect of investor activity on
homeownership decline, controlling for neighborhood characteristics.
The racial differential (4.2pp Black homeownership decline, no white
effect) is not predicted by a race-neutral market efficiency account —
if the mechanism were purely price-based, it would affect homeownership
across racial categories in the targeted price segments. The racial
specificity of the outcome requires a racially structured mechanism,
even if no individual investor makes a racially motivated decision.

A second null case: the homeownership decline was caused by the
foreclosure crisis itself, not by institutional investors. An addresses
this through the instrumental variable strategy, which isolates the
effect of investor purchases from confounding post-crisis trends.

**Verdict**: The null case of race-neutral market operation is
insufficient. The racial specificity of the outcome is the finding
that eliminates the race-neutral account. The power explanation —
capital exploiting racially structured vulnerability to extract wealth
from Black communities — is the explanation that fits the measured
differential impact. IC-2 is satisfied: the null case was considered
and is insufficient.
