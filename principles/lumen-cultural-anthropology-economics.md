## Central thesis

Economic systems are culturally embedded. Three modes of production
(domestic, tributary, capitalist) and three modes of exchange (reciprocity,
redistribution, market) organise human material life with different power
distributions, moral logics, and vulnerability profiles. Structural violence
produces inequality through institutional mechanisms, not individual failure.
The introduction of general-purpose money and capitalist production relations
into non-market societies destroys the moral boundaries and subsistence
alternatives that constrained economic power.

## Principles

### P1: The Commensurability Destruction

PRINCIPLE: General-purpose money destroys moral boundaries between exchange
spheres by making qualitatively different relationships (subsistence,
prestige, kinship) quantitatively commensurable. The destruction is not
incidental — it is the mechanism through which market logic penetrates
non-market social orders.

LAYERS: Economic (primary), Thought & Narrative, Institutional

MECHANISM: Pre-market economies maintain separate exchange spheres with
distinct moral logics. Each sphere has its own currency and conversion rules.
Downward conversion (prestige goods for food) is immoral. General-purpose
money collapses the boundaries between spheres by making everything
convertible to a single metric. Once the boundaries collapse, the moral logic
that prevented certain exchanges — that prevented treating people as
commensurable with cattle, or food as commensurable with prestige — is
structurally dissolved.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: The Tiv (pre-colonial Nigeria) had three spheres:
subsistence goods, prestige goods (slaves, cattle, white cloth, metal bars),
and marriageable persons. Labour was always reciprocally exchanged; land was
communally held. Downward conversion was immoral. "When items in all spheres
became general-purpose money-exchangeable, Tiv couldn't maintain separate
exchangeable-item categories." The Bohannans concluded that "moral money
meanings — how exchange is culturally conceived — have significant material
implications for daily lives." The chapter notes that general-purpose money
"reflects ideas about everything's generalised interchangeability...
increasing unequal exchange opportunities."

GENERALIZES TO: Any context where the introduction of a universal metric
destroys qualitative distinctions that constrained exchange. Credit scoring
(reducing a person to a number). Algorithmic valuation of attention (reducing
human experience to engagement metrics). GDP as sole economic measure
(reducing diverse economic activity to a single monetary figure). The
structural pattern: a universal metric does not merely measure — it
restructures what can be exchanged for what, and in doing so, restructures
social relations.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: extends Axiom 6 (language as first instrument of control —
money is a language that redefines what is commensurable); confirms
Manufactured Scarcity as Control (the destruction of non-market exchange
systems creates dependency on market participation); extends Moral
Infrastructure of Economic Power (the moral logic of separate spheres is the
inverse — moral infrastructure that constrains economic power, destroyed when
general-purpose money arrives)

STATUS: observed

### P2: Structural Violence as Institutional Production of Harm

PRINCIPLE: Structural violence operates not through individual acts of force
but through social structures and institutions that prevent populations from
meeting basic needs. The violence is structural because it is built into
institutional arrangements — it requires no individual perpetrator and
produces no individual defendant.

LAYERS: Economic (primary), Institutional (primary), Legal & Regulatory,
Physical & Coercive

MECHANISM: Political and economic systems create conditions (insufficient
wages, inadequate sanitation, food insecurity, illiteracy) that produce
suffering and death without any individual act of violence. The mechanism
operates through the normal functioning of institutions — not through their
failure. The structural nature of the violence makes it invisible as
violence: it appears as poverty, as illness, as "how things are."

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Haiti — richest 20% hold 64%+ of wealth; poorest 20%
hold 1%. More than 100,000 children under five suffer acute malnutrition.
Only 50% of households have safe water. Seven months after the 2010
earthquake, 40% of displaced persons' camps lacked water access despite
$2.2 billion in international aid. 33% of aid went to the US military; less
than 1% reached the Haitian government.

GENERALIZES TO: Any context where institutional arrangements produce harm
through their normal operation — mass incarceration, healthcare access
asymmetry, housing markets that extract profit from poverty (Desmond), food
deserts, occupational health hazards distributed by class. The structural
pattern: the violence requires no actor because it is the system's default
output.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: confirms Axiom 7 (aid system stated purpose is relief;
function is reproduction of structural inequality); confirms Formal
Transformation, Structural Continuity (Haiti: independence 1804, extraction
continuing through debt, dictatorship, NGO dependency); confirms Relay Class
(NGOs as intermediaries replacing colonial administrators); extends
Constitutive Violence (colonial slavery and 1825 reparations are not
historical but constitutive — the current system operates on the distribution
of power they established)

STATUS: observed

IC-1 NOTE: Strengthens the case for extending Axiom 9. Haiti shows compliance
manufactured not through concealment but through the impossibility of
alternatives — structural violence constrains the choice set to the point
where survival is the only achievable goal. Fifth independent domain (after
workplace, housing, low-wage service, border crossing) confirming that
Axiom 9's current framing overemphasises invisibility.

### P3: The Aid Reproduction Cycle

PRINCIPLE: International aid to structurally disadvantaged populations is
channelled through institutional intermediaries (NGOs, military, contractors)
whose structural interests require the continuation of the conditions the
aid claims to address. The aid system reproduces the power asymmetry it is
nominally designed to correct.

LAYERS: Economic (primary), Institutional (primary), Thought & Narrative

MECHANISM: Aid flows from donor nations through intermediary organisations
rather than to affected populations directly. The intermediaries employ the
recipient country's most educated workers at wages the national government
cannot match, draining institutional capacity from the state. The aid
system's stated purpose (development, relief) diverges from its structural
function (employment for intermediaries, geopolitical influence for donors,
institutional dependency for recipients). Schuller names this "trickle-down
imperialism."

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Less than 1% of post-earthquake aid reached Haiti's
government. The largest single recipient was the US military (33%). NGOs
attracted Haiti's most-educated workers. Haiti's economy already revolved
around international aid and migrant remittances (~25% of GDP) before the
earthquake. "Relief money through these organisations funnelled to structures
perpetuating inequality."

GENERALIZES TO: Any context where the institutional architecture of aid
reproduces the dependency it claims to address. Development finance that
requires structural adjustment. Humanitarian corridors that create permanent
camp populations. Capacity-building programmes that build capacity in
intermediary organisations rather than in the target population.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: confirms Inversion of Stated Purpose (aid system named for
relief, functions as reproduction); confirms Relay Class (NGOs as
intermediary class stepping into the role of departed colonial power); extends
Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity (colonialism → debt →
dictatorship → NGO dependency)

STATUS: observed

### P4: The Autonomy Floor

PRINCIPLE: Ownership of means of production (land, tools) establishes a
floor of economic autonomy that buffers populations against full dependency
on market participation. When this floor exists, populations can retreat to
subsistence during market downturns. When it is removed (the defining feature
of capitalist production), populations have no alternative to market
participation and are structurally vulnerable to whatever terms the market
offers.

LAYERS: Economic (primary), Institutional

MECHANISM: In domestic and tributary modes of production, workers typically
own their means of production. They may be exploited (tribute extraction),
but they retain the structural capacity to feed themselves. In capitalist
production, workers are separated from means of production and do not own
what they produce. This separation eliminates the autonomy floor — the
worker must participate in the labour market on whatever terms are available
because the alternative is not subsistence but starvation.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Wolf's three modes of production framework. The
Guatemalan fair-trade farmers' ability to "intensify corn production" during
hardship because they own land. The chapter's statement that in capitalism,
"workers sell their labour power to capitalists to survive." The Ju/'hoansi
collective ownership producing "lower rates of social domination."

GENERALIZES TO: Any context where the presence or absence of a subsistence
alternative determines bargaining power. The gig economy (no ownership of
platform = no autonomy floor). Housing markets (renters vs. owners during
economic shocks). Debt (indebted populations lose the autonomy floor even if
they technically own assets). The structural pattern: the degree of control
any system can exercise over a population is inversely proportional to that
population's capacity to meet its own needs outside the system.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: confirms Manufactured Scarcity as Control (removing the
autonomy floor manufactures dependency); confirms Axiom 9 (the autonomy
floor's absence is the mechanism that produces compliance — not concealment
but structural impossibility of refusal); extends Compliance Gradient (the
position on the gradient correlates with proximity to the autonomy floor)

STATUS: observed

  Graeber's analysis of how money transforms social relations; the Tiv case
  is a specific ethnographic instance of what Graeber documents historically
- Desmond (*Evicted*) — structural violence in housing maps to P2; the
  autonomy floor (P4) names what Desmond's tenants lack
- Ehrenreich (*Nickel and Dimed*) — the autonomy floor's absence is
  precisely what Ehrenreich documents in low-wage work
- De León (*Land of Open Graves*) — structural violence (P2) operating
  through environmental delegation rather than institutional deprivation
- Scott (*Weapons of the Weak*) — the moral economy concept in this chapter
  originates with E.P. Thompson and is applied by Scott; the Sedaka
  peasants' everyday resistance operates from above the autonomy floor
- Alley (*Very Bad People*) — the aid reproduction cycle (P3) maps to
  Alley's documentation of how accountability mechanisms are captured
- Fisher (*Capitalist Realism*) — the Commensurability Destruction (P1) is
  the historical mechanism that produced the condition Fisher describes:
  a world where everything is commensurable and alternatives are
  unimaginable
