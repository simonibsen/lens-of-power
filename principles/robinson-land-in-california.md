# Principles: Land in California

SOURCE: W.W. Robinson, *Land in California* (University of California Press, 1948)

PERSPECTIVE: Position 5 (historian) — Robinson is a title-company historian and popular writer on California land history. He writes for a general audience in an accessible, narrative style. His sympathies lie with the California rancheros — he documents in detail the hardship the Land Commission process inflicted on them. He is more sympathetic to the rancheros than his contemporary Paul W. Gates, who emphasised rancho-holder fraud and speculation.

CENTRAL THESIS: California's land history is a sequential dispossession in which each regime — Spanish, Mexican, American — treated the previous regime's land arrangements as raw material for a new power structure. At each transition the new sovereign claimed legitimacy through the delegitimation of the previous property system, while the structural beneficiaries at each stage were not the displaced population but the professional intermediaries (lawyers, surveyors, lenders) who administered the transition.

## IC-5 disclosure

The LLM's training data likely overrepresents:
- **The romantic narrative of the California missions** as benign institutions, and the triumphalist narrative of American westward expansion as progress. Robinson himself participates in both framings — the missions as an "idealistic adventure" and American settlement as civilisational advance.
- **Legal and institutional accounts** of the land system — the written record of laws, commissions, court cases, and Congressional acts — which Robinson meticulously documents. The perspective of those *processed* by these systems is reconstructed from that institutional record.
- **English-language sources**: Robinson works from American legal archives, the Bancroft Library, and published histories. The Spanish-language archive is accessed through translations.

Critiques likely underrepresented:
- **Indigenous perspectives on dispossession**: Robinson treats indigenous land use as background, not as the foundational act of the entire sequence. The indigenous population's loss is narrated in a single chapter and framed as a natural precursor to colonisation rather than as the constitutive violence that enabled everything that followed. IC-5 requires naming this: the written record Robinson relies on was produced by the displacing regimes, not by the displaced.
- **The experience of ranchero families during adjudication**: Robinson is sympathetic but works from institutional records — court transcripts, patent files, legal briefs. The lived experience of the seventeen-year average wait (financial ruin, squatter invasion, forced sales to speculators) is documented statistically but not from the ranchero families' own voices.
- **Mexican and Spanish legal traditions on their own terms**: These are filtered through American legal interpretation (the Jones and Halleck reports, Land Commission proceedings). The framework within which Mexican grants were assessed was the American legal system — the Mexican system's own coherence is not given independent standing.

Positional lens: Robinson writes from the position of a California title-company historian. His professional context — the title insurance industry — gives him deep knowledge of chains of title, surveys, and legal instruments, but also a structural orientation toward the American property system as the legitimate endpoint of the sequence. The book assumes that clear, marketable title is the goal of the story; the question of whether the property regime itself is the right frame is not asked.

## Principles

### P1: Sequential Sovereignty — Each Regime Constitutes Legitimacy Through Delegitimation of the Previous

PRINCIPLE: When sovereignty over land changes hands, the new sovereign does not merely inherit the previous property system — it *reconstitutes* the property system by subjecting all prior titles to its own adjudicatory framework. Each regime treats the previous regime's land arrangements as raw material to be processed through its own legal machinery. The legitimacy of the new order is constituted through the implicit delegitimation of the old. The sequence does not preserve rights; it replaces them with rights of a new kind, issued by a new authority.

LAYERS: Legal & Regulatory (primary), Institutional, Thought & Narrative, Physical & Coercive

MECHANISM: Robinson documents four regime transitions in California, each following the same structural pattern: (1) **Indigenous → Spanish**: The Laws of the Indies recognised Indians as "theoretical owners of the territory they needed" but "the civilising process to which they were subjected greatly reduced their land needs." The Crown asserted absolute title; indigenous occupancy was subordinated to a European legal category that permitted its erasure. (2) **Spanish → Mexican**: Mexican independence converted Spanish concessions into claims requiring regranting under Mexican law. "Many holders [took] the precaution to have them renewed under Mexican law" — precaution implying that renewal was not guaranteed. (3) **Mexican → American**: The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo promised "full and complete protection of all property rights of Mexicans." The Act of 1851 then required every landowner to prove their title before a commission and courts, "in spite of Articles VIII and IX of the Treaty." (4) **Ranchero → Settler**: Confirmed rancho titles were subjected to squatter pressure, surveyor disputes, and further litigation that transferred land from original owners to American settlers, speculators, and their lawyers.

Each transition was framed as legitimate within the new regime's own legal categories. Each destroyed or degraded the rights of the previous occupants while claiming to honour them.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Robinson's entire narrative structure demonstrates the pattern. The Spanish claim over indigenous land was exercised through the Laws of the Indies. The Mexican claim was exercised through the colonisation laws of 1824 and 1828. The American claim was exercised through the Act of 1851 and its judicial apparatus. At each transition, the incoming sovereign acknowledged the previous rights in principle and undermined them in procedure.

GENERALISES TO: Colonial succession generally (French-British-independent regimes in Africa); the Soviet-to-private transition in post-Soviet states (Guriev-Rachinsky: "selective reform trap"); any regime change where the new sovereign claims to respect prior property rights while subjecting them to an adjudicatory process controlled by the new sovereign's institutions.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends the Constitutive Violence pattern — the constitutive act is not a single founding moment but a *sequence* of founding moments, each constituting its legitimacy through the delegitimation of the previous. Extends Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity — but in Robinson's case, the *structural* element that persists across transitions is not a single power group but the *mechanism itself*: each regime uses the land system as the primary instrument of state-building. Connects to Axiom 4 (consistent mechanisms, varying manifestations) — the same mechanism (sovereign reconstitution of property) operates across four successive regimes.

IC-1 FLAG: Axiom 7 — The Act of 1851 stated its purpose as "settling" land claims. Its function was subjecting existing property holders to a ruinously expensive process that transferred their land to lawyers, lenders, and settlers. The gap between stated purpose and function is documented by Robinson and is consistent with the pattern. However, IC-2 requires the null case: Congress may have genuinely intended to protect Mexican rights while establishing a necessary legal framework for American governance, and the ruinous consequences may have been an unintended product of institutional complexity rather than design. Robinson himself is ambivalent — he notes the commission was "hard-working and fair-minded" while documenting its devastating effects.

STATUS: observed (the four transitions are documented); inferred (the structural mechanism — that each transition constitutes legitimacy through delegitimation)

### P2: The Burden of Proof Reversal as Dispossession Mechanism

PRINCIPLE: When a conquering power requires existing landowners to prove their own titles before a tribunal controlled by the conqueror — rather than presuming their titles valid until challenged — the burden of proof itself becomes a mechanism of dispossession. The requirement to prove what was previously taken for granted transfers power from the landholder to the adjudicating institution, regardless of the institution's stated fairness.

LAYERS: Legal & Regulatory (primary), Economic, Institutional

MECHANISM: The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) specifically promised "full and complete protection of all property rights of Mexicans." The Act of 1851 then required "each and every person claiming lands in California by virtue of any right or title derived from the Spanish or Mexican government" to present their claim with "documentary evidence and testimony of witnesses." The Benton alternative — a simple recorder system that would have presumed titles valid unless fraud was suspected — was rejected in favour of the Gwin bill, which required affirmative proof. The structural consequence: (1) Landowners had to hire American lawyers — many could not afford them and conveyed "undivided interests" to their attorneys as payment. (2) Documentary evidence was scattered — "records prior to 1839 seemed to be missing," archives were in disarray. (3) Witnesses had to be assembled and transported to San Francisco. (4) The process was extended — five years for the commission, then automatic appeal to the District Court, then possible Supreme Court appeal. The burden of proof reversal transformed a treaty guarantee of protection into a process of attrition.

Robinson notes: "Seventeen years was the average length of time that the California landowner had to wait for his patent after filing his petition." During those seventeen years: "rancho land was not salable, squatters came in like locusts, and the owners' funds and resources went to lawyers and lenders. Most claimants were bankrupted in the process of getting clear titles."

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: The contrast between the Jones report (which found the grants "mostly perfect titles" and recommended a simple confirmation process) and the Gwin bill (which imposed a full adjudicatory process). Senator Benton "foresaw many of the hardships that landowners were later to suffer under the Gwin measure." The 604 claims finally confirmed out of 813 filed — the great majority were confirmed, meaning the burden of proof destroyed landowners who held valid titles.

GENERALISES TO: Any legal process where the requirement to prove a pre-existing right becomes the mechanism by which that right is destroyed. Immigration adjudication where the burden falls on the applicant. Regulatory compliance requirements that bankrupt small operators while large ones absorb the cost. Credentialing processes that exclude practitioners who cannot afford the process of proving competence they already possess.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends Inversion of Stated Purpose — the Act of 1851 inverted the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo's promise of protection into a mechanism of dispossession. Connects to the Capture Pipeline — the adjudicatory infrastructure (commission, courts, surveyors) was not captured from outside but was built to serve the interests of the new sovereign from inception.

STATUS: observed

### P3: Procedural Attrition — Delay as Structural Dispossession

PRINCIPLE: When a legal process takes so long that the cost of participating destroys the claimant before a decision is reached, the process itself functions as confiscation — regardless of the eventual outcome. The delay need not be deliberate; structural delay produces structural dispossession whether or not anyone intended it.

LAYERS: Legal & Regulatory (primary), Economic, Institutional

MECHANISM: Robinson documents the seventeen-year average wait for patent. During this period: (1) **Land was unsaleable** — without clear title, rancheros could not sell even portions of their holdings, which were often far larger than they could use. (2) **Squatters invaded** — the unsettled state of title attracted squatters who knew the legal system could not quickly resolve their eviction. (3) **Legal costs accumulated** — lawyers' fees, travel to San Francisco for hearings, witness expenses, survey costs. (4) **Lenders charged usurious rates** — claimants mortgaged their land to pay legal costs, then lost the land to foreclosure. (5) **Government appeals extended delay** — even after the commission confirmed a claim, the government attorney automatically appealed, adding years to the process. (6) **Surveys were slow and disputed** — the Surveyor General's office was a bottleneck, and survey disputes added further years. The result: "Most claimants were bankrupted in the process of getting clear titles. Some chose to sell out to speculators and sharpers."

The critical structural feature: the great majority of claims were eventually confirmed. The process did not deny the validity of Mexican titles — it destroyed the holders of valid titles through the cost of proving what should not have needed proving.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Robinson's documentation of the Rancho San Pascual case study, where the chain of title shows the original Mexican grantee's interests passing through multiple American hands during the adjudication period. The general pattern: rancheros entering the process as landowners and emerging — if they survived the process at all — as debtors or former landowners.

GENERALISES TO: Immigration asylum processes where years of waiting destroy applicants' lives regardless of outcome. Patent litigation where the cost of defending valid patents bankrupts small inventors. Regulatory approval processes where delay transfers market advantage to incumbents. Criminal justice systems where pre-trial detention punishes the accused before conviction. The principle: *procedural delay is substantively indistinguishable from denial when the cost of delay exceeds the value of the right being adjudicated*.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends the Compliance Gradient — procedural attrition operates in the space between formal compliance (the process exists, the claimant can participate) and substantive denial (the process destroys the claimant). Connects to Manufactured Scarcity — the unsaleability of land during adjudication manufactured scarcity in the land market, benefiting those (squatters, speculators, railroad companies) who could wait out the process.

STATUS: observed

### P4: The Professional Extraction Class — Lawyers and Lenders as Structural Beneficiaries

PRINCIPLE: When a complex legal process mediates the transfer of property between regimes, a professional class of intermediaries — lawyers, surveyors, lenders, speculators — extracts wealth from both the process and its participants. This class benefits structurally from the complexity and duration of the process: the more complex and prolonged the adjudication, the greater the extraction. The intermediaries' interest in prolonged adjudication is structural, not necessarily conscious.

LAYERS: Economic (primary), Legal & Regulatory, Institutional

MECHANISM: Robinson documents three distinct extraction channels: (1) **Lawyers**: Claimants "went to Yankee lawyers for help" and "sometimes mortgaged their lands and their futures or conveyed 'undivided interests' to their attorneys." The leading law firms — "Halleck, Peachy and Billings; Clarke, Taylor and Beckh; Jones, Tompkins and Strode; and Crosby and Rose" — handled hundreds of claims. (2) **The revolving door**: Both Halleck (who wrote the sceptical report emphasising title defects) and Jones (who wrote the favourable report) subsequently practised as attorneys for land claimants. Halleck's firm handled "many of the land suits." Robinson notes this without comment, but the structural implication is that the men who assessed the land system and shaped legislation then profited from the adjudicatory apparatus they had helped create. (3) **Lenders**: Rancheros mortgaged land to pay legal costs. When claims were delayed, interest accumulated. Foreclosure transferred land from rancheros to creditors. Robinson notes that "the latter [lawyers], who benefited most from long-drawn-out litigation, point out that, in the United States, courts — and not recorders or registrars — have the final word."

The structural point: the professional class did not cause the complexity of the system (though Halleck's sceptical report contributed to it). But the professional class *benefited* from complexity, and the system's structure ensured that the cost of adjudication fell on the claimants while the fees flowed to the intermediaries.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Robinson's explicit observation that "lawyers" are the answer to "who benefited" from the adjudication process. The documented revolving door of Halleck and Jones. The pattern of ranchero families losing land not to rejected claims but to the cost of proving valid claims.

GENERALISES TO: The Wealth Defense Industry (Winters) — the same structural dynamic operates in reverse: a professional class that defends concentrated wealth benefits from the complexity of tax and regulatory systems. Patent trolls who benefit from the cost of litigation regardless of outcome. The financial intermediaries who extracted wealth during the 2008 mortgage crisis while both lenders and borrowers lost. Immigration lawyers who profit from system complexity regardless of client outcomes.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends the Wealth Defense Industry pattern — Robinson documents the inverse: a *Wealth Extraction Industry* that profits from the transfer of property rather than its defence. The mechanism is identical (professional intermediaries benefit from legal complexity) but the direction is reversed (extraction from the dispossessed rather than defence of the concentrated). Connects to the Division of Learning — the professional class possessed specialised knowledge (American law, English language, court procedure) that claimants lacked, creating an asymmetry of knowledge that enabled extraction.

STATUS: observed

### P5: Squatterism as Political Movement — The Democratisation of Dispossession

PRINCIPLE: Squatting on land held under uncertain title is not merely individual opportunism — it becomes a political movement when the squatters are numerous enough to constitute a voting bloc. The political system then accommodates squatter interests through legislation, judicial appointments, and executive action, creating a democratic mandate for dispossession. The legitimation narrative — that land monopolists obstruct the progress of civilisation — inverts the legal and moral situation while providing democratic cover for expropriation.

LAYERS: Thought & Narrative (primary), Legal & Regulatory, Institutional, Physical & Coercive

MECHANISM: Robinson documents the political trajectory of squatterism in California: (1) **Armed occupation** — squatters settling on ranchero land, building fences, establishing possession through force. "Squatting implements consisted of blankets and firearms." (2) **Political organisation** — squatters holding public meetings, raising money, hiring lawyers. Robinson: "every man had a sacred right to a homestead and a sacred duty to defend it." (3) **Electoral power** — "So large was the squatter population in California that politicians began bidding for their vote." Governor Bigler called squatters "bona-fide settlers" and advocated legislation on their behalf. (4) **Legislative capture** — in 1856 the legislature passed a law declaring all lands in the state public "until the legal title should be shown to have passed to private parties." (5) **Judicial capture** — "There were squatter governors, squatter legislators, squatter courts, and squatter judges." (6) **Narrative legitimation** — the rancheros were reframed as "monopolists who, like the Indians, were obstructing the path of progress of civilisation."

The structural feature: squatterism was not merely tolerated — it was *democratised*. The democratic process provided legitimation for dispossession that physical force alone could not. The squatters' claim was morally underwritten by the same manifest destiny narrative that had justified the conquest itself.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: The Sacramento squatter riots of 1850 (mayor killed, sheriff killed). Governor Bigler's 1854 message. The 1856 law presuming all land public. The twenty-year squatting industry in San Francisco. The phrase "squatter governors, squatter legislators, squatter courts, and squatter judges."

GENERALISES TO: Land reform movements that combine genuine grievance with expropriation of specific populations. Populist movements that democratise the dispossession of minorities. Settler-colonial movements generally — where the democratic will of the settler population provides legitimation for indigenous displacement. The principle: *democratic majorities can provide a legitimation apparatus for dispossession that is more durable than autocratic decree because it carries the moral authority of popular sovereignty*.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence — Robinson documents the inverse: not oligarchy coexisting with democracy, but democratic majorities coexisting with and enabling expropriation of a propertied minority. Connects to Ideological Judo — the squatters invoked American democratic values (free land, homestead rights, opposition to monopoly) against the rancheros, turning the dominant ideology into a weapon of dispossession. Connects to Constitutive Violence — the squatter movement was a continuation of the constitutive violence of conquest, democratised and legalised.

IC-1 FLAG: Axiom 5 — The squatter movement complicates "power concentrates unless actively counteracted." In this case, concentrated landholding (the ranchos) was *dispersed* by popular pressure — but the dispersal was itself a form of dispossession directed at a specific ethnic group (Californios) by a majority (American settlers). Concentration was counteracted, but the counteraction was ethnically targeted expropriation. The axiom's directive ("at whose expense?") captures this, but the case shows that dispersal of concentrated property can itself be an exercise of power when directed by a demographic majority against a minority.

STATUS: observed

### P6: The Regime-Transition Land Rush — Secularisation, Conquest, and the Predictable Scramble

PRINCIPLE: At each regime transition, the dissolution of the previous property regime produces a land rush in which the connected and the powerful acquire the best land before the new legal framework is established. The interregnum between regimes — when the old rules no longer apply and the new rules do not yet exist — is the period of maximum extraction by those positioned to act.

LAYERS: Economic (primary), Institutional, Legal & Regulatory

MECHANISM: Robinson documents three land rushes, each triggered by regime transition: (1) **Secularisation (1834)**: "Secularisation in 1834 was the signal for a land rush in California and a shifting of population." Mission-controlled land was carved into ranchos: "between 1834 and 1842 more than 300 ranches were granted to Mexican citizens, and largely carved out of mission-held land." "Influential citizens were given preference, of course, for land was the gift of government." The Indians, theoretically the beneficiaries of secularisation, received nothing: "demoralization quickly set in, with the Indians — unprepared to take care of themselves — scurrying away to the towns or to the ranchos." (2) **American conquest (1846-1850s)**: Before the Land Commission could establish which land was private and which public, "settlers were pouring into California." Squatters "began early to organize into armed bands to get what they wanted." The gold rush intensified the scramble. (3) **Post-adjudication (1860s-1880s)**: As ranchos were confirmed and broken up, speculators, railroad companies, and settlers scrambled for the newly available land.

The structural feature: each transition creates a window in which the rules are unsettled, and those who can act fastest and most aggressively — the connected, the violent, the legally sophisticated — acquire the most land.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: The 300+ rancho grants in the eight years following secularisation. The squatter invasion before the Land Commission convened. Governor Pio Pico's last-minute grants as American conquest became inevitable. The scramble for "excluded" land whenever a survey found ranchero boundaries smaller than claimed.

GENERALISES TO: Post-Soviet privatisation (the "loans for shares" programme). Post-colonial land grabs. Deregulation windows where incumbents capture newly available resources before the new regulatory framework is established. The principle: *the interregnum between property regimes is the moment of maximum extraction, and those who benefit most are those positioned at the intersection of the old and new systems*.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Connects to the Peacetime Ratchet — each land rush concentrated ownership rather than distributing it. Connects to the Relay Class — at each transition, a relay class (rancheros replacing missionaries, American settlers replacing rancheros) stepped into the structural position of the displaced group. Extends Constitutive Violence — each land rush was a micro-constitutive moment, establishing the property distribution that the subsequent legal order would naturalise.

STATUS: observed

### P7: The Absent Founding Dispossession — Indigenous Erasure as Structural Precondition

PRINCIPLE: Robinson's narrative begins with indigenous land use and then moves past it — treating indigenous dispossession not as the foundational act of the entire sequence but as a natural precursor to "civilisation." This treatment is itself significant: the erasure of the foundational dispossession from the analytical frame is a precondition for treating the subsequent sequence (missions, ranchos, American settlement) as the "real" history of land in California.

LAYERS: Thought & Narrative (primary), Legal & Regulatory, Physical & Coercive

MECHANISM: Robinson devotes Chapter I and part of Chapter II to indigenous peoples, then Chapter III's opening frames mission visits as tourism: the visitor "is in a mood of happy benevolence, satisfied with his excursion into the past and content with a good world." Indigenous land use is described as "possessory claims doomed to fade quickly before conquering white races." The Laws of the Indies recognised Indians as "theoretical owners" but the "civilising process" reduced their "land needs." When the Land Commission was established, "Indians, quite unaware of what was happening, did nothing about the lands they were occupying." Throughout the book, indigenous displacement is narrated in the passive voice — things happen *to* Indians, but the agents and mechanisms are not foregrounded.

The structural consequence: by treating indigenous dispossession as background, Robinson naturalises the entire property sequence that follows. If the first dispossession is natural (the inevitable fading of possessory claims "before conquering white races"), then the subsequent regime transitions are merely adjustments within a legitimate property system. The foundational violence disappears from the analytical frame, and the reader encounters the rancheros' dispossession as the first *real* injustice.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Robinson's language — "possessory claims doomed to fade quickly"; "the civilising process greatly reduced their land needs"; the tourist framing of the mission chapter. The structural absence: no chapter is devoted to how indigenous land was taken; it is simply *gone* by the time the ranchero story begins. The Land Commission was instructed to report on "those held by civilized Indians" — the categorisation ("civilized") reveals the framework within which indigenous claims were processed.

GENERALISES TO: Any historical narrative that begins its account of property at the moment of the narrator's civilisational tradition's arrival, treating prior occupancy as prehistory rather than as a property regime that was destroyed. Australian land law's "terra nullius" doctrine. American property law's treatment of indigenous title as a "right of occupancy" subordinate to European sovereignty. The principle: *the foundational dispossession is the one that cannot be named as dispossession within the property regime it established, because the regime's own legitimacy depends on treating that act as the origin of property rather than as its destruction*.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends Constitutive Violence — the foundational constitutive violence is the one that is *invisible within the frame it constituted*. This is Axiom 3 applied to historiography: the contingency (indigenous land could have been recognised as property) is invisible because the framework within which "property" is defined is the framework established by the conquest. Extends Axiom 8 (absence is significant) — the absence of indigenous dispossession from the analytical frame is not an oversight but a structural feature of the frame itself.

IC-1 FLAG: Axiom 3 (hegemonic naturalisation) — Robinson's treatment of indigenous dispossession is a specific instance of the fifth mode. The mechanism is visible (Robinson describes it). Its contingency is in principle statable (other legal systems recognised indigenous title). But the framework within which the history is told — a framework of property, title, and legal succession — is itself a product of the dispossession. The dispossession is not concealed; it is prior to the analytical categories the book uses. Robinson cannot see it as a structural injustice because his categories (title, patent, chain of ownership) begin after the foundational act.

IC-5 FLAG: The LLM's training data inherits this framing. Legal and historical training data overwhelmingly treats European property regimes as the starting point for land history. Indigenous land systems are classified as "customary tenure" or "occupancy rights" — subordinate categories within the European framework. This extraction names Robinson's positional limitation but must also name the LLM's: the analysis above is conducted within property-law categories that are themselves products of the dispossession being analysed.

STATUS: observed (Robinson's treatment of indigenous peoples); inferred (the structural function of this treatment as a precondition for the legitimacy of the subsequent sequence)

## Patterns identified

### Existing patterns confirmed
- **Constitutive Violence** (ESTABLISHED): Each regime transition is a constitutive act — sovereignty is established through the assertion of a new property framework over the old, backed by military force (Spanish conquest, American conquest) or legal coercion (Act of 1851)
- **Inversion of Stated Purpose** (ESTABLISHED): The Act of 1851, which stated its purpose as "settling" land claims and implementing treaty guarantees, functioned as a mechanism of dispossession (P2, P3)
- **Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity** (ESTABLISHED): The structure of extraction (land concentrated in few hands, surplus population displaced) persisted across all four regime transitions while the legal form of title changed completely (P1)
- **Appearance as Structural Terrain** (ESTABLISHED): The Land Commission and courts presented the appearance of fair adjudication while the process itself destroyed the claimants (P3)
- **The Wealth Defense Industry** (SUPPORTED): Robinson documents the inverse — a Wealth *Extraction* Industry of lawyers and lenders who profited from adjudication complexity (P4)
- **Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence** (ESTABLISHED): Squatterism shows the inverse — democratic majorities coexisting with and enabling expropriation of a propertied minority (P5)
- **The Relay Class** (PRELIMINARY): At each regime transition, a new class steps into the structural position of the displaced: rancheros replacing mission fathers, settlers replacing rancheros (P6)
- **Manufactured Scarcity** (ESTABLISHED): The unsaleability of land during the seventeen-year adjudication process manufactured artificial scarcity in the land market (P3)
- **The Compliance Gradient** (SUPPORTED): The ranchero response ranged from full compliance with the adjudication process to selling out to speculators to abandoning claims entirely
- **The Middle Stratum Trap** (ESTABLISHED): Rancheros occupied a middle stratum — powerful relative to indigenous populations but vulnerable to the American legal and political system
- **Ideological Judo** (SUPPORTED): Squatters invoked American democratic values against rancheros; the rancheros' own property claims were turned against them through the requirement to prove title (P5)
- **Peacetime Ratchet** (ESTABLISHED): Each land rush following regime transition concentrated ownership among the connected and the legally sophisticated (P6)
- **Division of Learning** (ESTABLISHED): The knowledge asymmetry between American lawyers/courts and Spanish-speaking rancheros enabled extraction (P4)

### IC-1 flags
- **Axiom 3 (hegemonic naturalisation)**: Robinson's treatment of indigenous dispossession is an instance of the fifth mode — the framework within which the history is told is itself a product of the founding dispossession (P7)
- **Axiom 5 (power concentrates unless counteracted)**: Squatterism complicates this — concentrated property was dispersed, but through ethnically targeted democratic expropriation (P5)
- **Axiom 7 (stated vs. function)**: The Act of 1851 is a strong case — stated purpose of settling claims; function of destroying claimants through procedural attrition. But the null case (IC-2) requires acknowledging that Congress may not have intended this outcome (P2, P3)
- **Axiom 8 (absence as significant)**: The absence of indigenous dispossession from the analytical frame is not merely significant but *constitutive* — the property framework depends on that absence (P7)

- **Graeber P2** (debt as moral instrument): Ranchero debt to lawyers and lenders was structured as legitimate commercial obligation, but the debt was produced by the adjudicatory system itself — the moral obligation to pay was a product of the dispossession mechanism

- **Braverman P1** (indeterminacy): The indeterminacy of Mexican land titles under American law — the gap between what the titles *were* under Mexican law and what they *became* under American adjudication — is the conceptual space in which the professional extraction class operated
- **García Hernández P1** (historical contingency of legal categories): The Act of 1851's treatment of Mexican titles as presumptively invalid parallels the historical contingency of immigration crime — in both cases, a legal category (invalid title, illegal immigrant) is constructed by the new regime and applied retroactively to a population that was previously legal
- **Scott P7** (ideological judo): The squatters' use of American democratic ideology against the rancheros is ideological judo — holding the new regime to its stated values (free land, opposition to monopoly) in order to dispossess the previous occupants
