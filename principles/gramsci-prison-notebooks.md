## IC-5 disclosure

My training data contains extensive coverage of Gramsci because his
concepts (hegemony, organic intellectuals, civil society, war of
position) are foundational across political theory, sociology, cultural
studies, international relations, and education. The *Prison Notebooks*
are among the most cited texts in twentieth-century social science.
This saturation means my reconstruction of Gramsci's core concepts is
reliable, but it also means I have absorbed decades of secondary
interpretation layered over the primary text. The secondary literature
overwhelmingly reads Gramsci through a class-struggle lens; the
extraction below deliberately generalises his mechanisms to any
hegemonic formation operating at civilisational scale, which is closer
to the structural logic of the text but may diverge from the dominant
academic reception.

Gramsci wrote in prison under censorship, in fragmentary notebooks,
using coded language ("philosophy of praxis" for Marxism, indirect
references to avoid confiscation). The Hoare and Nowell Smith edition
is a thematic selection, not a complete translation. The editorial
arrangement imposes a coherence on material that was originally
provisional and unfinished. This extraction treats the text as a
source of structural mechanisms, not as a finished theoretical system.

**On the generalisation from Marxism**: Gramsci was a Marxist
intellectual — a founding member and leader of the Italian Communist
Party, imprisoned by Mussolini's fascist government, writing explicitly
within and for the Marxist revolutionary tradition. His concepts were
developed to analyse how the bourgeoisie maintains class dominance and
how the proletariat might contest it. The class-struggle vocabulary is
not incidental; it is the analytical framework through which he
developed every concept extracted below.

This extraction generalises his mechanisms beyond the class-struggle
context to any hegemonic formation operating at civilisational scale.
This generalisation is analytically productive — hegemony, common
sense, organic intellectuals, passive revolution, and the war of
position describe structural mechanisms that operate in contexts far
beyond the bourgeoisie-proletariat relationship — but it is not
neutral. It is itself an instance of what Gramsci's own concept of
passive revolution describes: the dominant intellectual tradition
absorbing what is useful from a revolutionary thinker while discarding
the revolutionary intent. Gramsci did not write these concepts so that
liberal analysts could study "hegemony in general." He wrote them so
that the working class could understand and dismantle a specific
hegemonic arrangement.

The framework should use these tools honestly — they illuminate the
mechanisms of hegemonic power regardless of the analyst's political
commitments — but it should not pretend that the generalisation is
innocent. Every extraction selects and transforms. This one selects
structural mechanisms and transforms them from instruments of
revolutionary analysis into instruments of structural diagnosis. That
transformation serves the framework's purposes. Whether it serves
Gramsci's is a question the framework cannot answer from within its
own categories.

### SYNTHESIS SUMMARY

**Novel contribution**: Gramsci provides the most complete theorisation
in the corpus of how hegemonic systems construct and maintain themselves
as the unquestioned background of social life. Where Orwell models total
domination through coercion and surveillance, Gramsci explains how
domination operates primarily through consent — and why consent-based
domination is more durable. Where Ellul describes propaganda as a total
environment, Gramsci explains the institutional infrastructure that
produces and reproduces that environment across generations. Where Scott
documents resistance from below, Gramsci theorises the system from above
that resistance operates against. The distinctive contribution is the
concept of hegemony as a civilisational project — not merely ideological
dominance but the production of an entire world-view, embedded in
institutions, reproduced through a specialised personnel system, and
experienced by the population as the nature of things rather than as a
position.

**Confirmatory contribution**: Provides the theoretical foundation for
multiple existing patterns: Appearance as Structural Terrain (the gap
between the system's presentation as universal interest and its operation
as dominant-group interest is the terrain hegemony depends on),
Anticipatory Obedience (organic intellectuals who advance the hegemonic
programme without explicit instruction), Layered Redundancy of Control
(civil society institutions as overlapping mechanisms of hegemonic
reproduction), Formal Transformation/Structural Continuity (passive
revolution as the mechanism), Middle Stratum Trap (assimilation of
subaltern intellectuals), Capture Pipeline (absorption of oppositional
personnel), Double Performance (hegemonic group performs universal
interest while pursuing corporate interest).

**Limitations**: Gramsci wrote in prison under censorship, in fragments,
with limited access to sources. His examples are predominantly Italian
and European. The Hoare and Nowell Smith edition is a selection — other
arrangements and translations produce different emphases. The text was
written within a Marxist framework that takes class as the primary axis
of analysis; the generalisation to any hegemonic formation (religious,
imperial, technocratic, civilisational) is an analytical extension, not
Gramsci's own stated scope. His theory of counter-hegemony assumes the
possibility of an alternative formation capable of building its own
hegemonic apparatus — an assumption that may not hold in all historical
conditions.

## Principles

### P1: The Dual Architecture of Dominance — Consent + Coercion

**PRINCIPLE**: Any durable hegemonic system operates simultaneously
through two mechanisms: the manufacturing of consent via civil society
institutions (schools, media, churches, professional associations,
cultural norms) and the maintenance of coercive capacity via the State.
Coercion exists primarily as a reserve for when consent fails. The
ratio between the two is the diagnostic indicator of a hegemonic
system's health or crisis. A system that must increasingly rely on
coercion is one whose consent apparatus is failing.

LAYERS: All layers (Thought & Narrative and Institutional primary)
MECHANISM: Gramsci distinguishes between "political society" (the State
apparatus: government, courts, police, military) and "civil society"
(the ensemble of private organisations: schools, churches, media,
professional bodies, cultural institutions). Hegemony is exercised
through civil society — it is the intellectual, moral, and cultural
leadership that makes a dominant formation's world-view appear as the
universal interest. Political society provides the "armour of coercion"
that activates when consent fractures. In a healthy hegemony, the
coercive apparatus is rarely visible because the consent apparatus
makes its deployment unnecessary. In crisis, the ratio inverts: coercion
becomes primary, consent collapses, and the system's vulnerability is
exposed. The diagnostic question is always: what is the current ratio
between consent and coercion, and in which direction is it moving?
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci defines the State in its "integral" sense as
"political society + civil society, that is hegemony protected by the
armour of coercion" (p. 263). He argues that in advanced formations, the
State is "only an outer ditch, behind which there stood a powerful system
of fortresses and earthworks" — the civil society institutions that
constitute the real infrastructure of dominance (p. 238). The distinction
between "domination" (coercion, direct force) and "intellectual and moral
leadership" (consent, hegemony) runs through his notes on intellectuals
(pp. 12, 57-58) and his analysis of the Italian Risorgimento (pp.
55-60, 104-106). Gramsci argues that the "normal exercise of hegemony"
in parliamentary regimes is "characterised by the combination of force
and consent, which balance each other reciprocally, without force
predominating excessively over consent" (pp. 80n, 258-260).
GENERALISES TO: Any formation where the dominant arrangement is
maintained primarily through the population's acceptance of its
legitimacy rather than through continuous coercion — religious
civilisations, imperial administrative systems, market democracies,
technocratic governance orders. The consent/coercion ratio is a
universal diagnostic applicable wherever a formation claims legitimacy.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Confirms Axiom 1 (power operates across layers
simultaneously — hegemony is by definition multi-layer); confirms Axiom
9 (compliance manufactured without explicit coercion — consent is the
primary mechanism); provides the theoretical architecture for Layered
Redundancy of Control (the redundancy is not accidental but structural:
civil society institutions are the distributed infrastructure of
consent).
STATUS: observed

### P2: Common Sense as Invisible Infrastructure

**PRINCIPLE**: The deepest achievement of any hegemonic system is the
production of "common sense" — the sedimented, unexamined assumptions
through which the population interprets reality. This is not ideology
(which can be debated) but the background ontology that determines what
counts as a reasonable question, a realistic proposal, a thinkable
alternative. Common sense is the accumulated residue of prior hegemonic
arrangements, transmitted across generations through institutional
practice rather than explicit instruction. It is experienced not as a
position but as the nature of things.

LAYERS: Thought & Narrative (primary), Institutional
MECHANISM: Gramsci distinguishes between "philosophy" (systematic,
coherent world-views held consciously) and "common sense" (the
"philosophy of non-philosophers" — the fragmentary, contradictory,
sedimented collection of assumptions absorbed from the social
environment). Common sense is not a single formation's ideology but
the geological residue of multiple prior hegemonies layered over
centuries. It contains contradictory elements — traces of medieval
religion alongside Enlightenment rationalism alongside folk wisdom —
but its contradictions are not experienced as contradictions because
they are never examined together. The population operates within common
sense without reflecting on it. It defines the limits of the thinkable.
Challenging hegemony at the level of explicit argument is insufficient
if the background ontology remains intact: the audience evaluates new
arguments through the common-sense framework the hegemony has produced.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci defines common sense as "the conception of
the world which is uncritically absorbed by the various social and
cultural environments in which the moral individuality of the average
man is developed" (p. 419). It is "not a single unique conception,
identical in time and space" but rather "the 'folklore' of philosophy"
— fragmentary, incoherent, the "diffuse, unco-ordinated features of a
generic form of thought common to a particular period and a particular
popular environment" (pp. 419, 325-326). Gramsci's entire project of
developing "good sense" — the critical kernel within common sense —
depends on the recognition that common sense is the terrain of hegemonic
struggle, not merely its symptom (pp. 325-334). His analysis of religion
as the primary historical contributor to common sense (pp. 326, 328,
419-420) illustrates how prior hegemonic formations deposit residues that
persist for centuries after the formation itself has been superseded.
GENERALISES TO: Any formation where the population's background
assumptions about what is normal, natural, or inevitable were produced
by a prior arrangement of power and continue to constrain what is
thinkable. The mechanism operates in market societies (where the
assumption that market organisation is natural is common sense), in
post-colonial societies (where colonial categories persist as common
sense), and in any setting where inherited assumptions function as
invisible constraints on political imagination.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends Axiom 3 beyond its four existing modes.
Common sense is a fifth mechanism: not hidden apparatus, not visible
mechanism with hidden contingency, not prohibitive cost, not degraded
perception — but the production of a background ontology that makes
contingency unthinkable. The subject does not see the arrangement as
natural because the alternative has been concealed or because cost is
prohibitive — but because the category "alternative" does not arise
within the common-sense framework. This is the deepest form of
invisible power in the corpus.
STATUS: observed

### P3: The Organic Intellectual Personnel System

**PRINCIPLE**: Hegemony is maintained not by ideas floating free but by
the personnel who carry, elaborate, transmit, and enforce those ideas.
Every dominant formation generates its own organic intellectuals — the
stratum of organisers, administrators, technicians, educators,
journalists, and cultural workers who give the formation its coherence.
Simultaneously, it must assimilate or neutralise the traditional
intellectuals inherited from prior formations. The quality and density
of this personnel system determines hegemonic resilience. Challenging
hegemony requires building an alternative personnel system, not merely
proposing alternative ideas.

LAYERS: Institutional (primary), Thought & Narrative
MECHANISM: Gramsci redefines "intellectual" to include everyone whose
function is organisational, connective, or directive — not merely those
who produce high culture. "Every social group, coming into existence on
the original terrain of an essential function in the world of economic
production, creates together with itself, organically, one or more
strata of intellectuals which give it homogeneity and an awareness of
its own function" (p. 5). These organic intellectuals are not external
commentators but functional operators of the formation: the industrial
technician, the political organiser, the cultural manager, the educator
who transmits the formation's world-view. Simultaneously, every new
formation must deal with "traditional" intellectuals — the personnel
inherited from prior formations (the clergy, the classical humanists,
the old administrative class) who regard themselves as autonomous and
above social forces. The dominant formation assimilates these traditional
intellectuals into its own apparatus while allowing them to maintain
the appearance of autonomy. The critical insight for understanding
hegemonic durability is that many organic intellectuals are unaware of
the hegemonic function they perform. They experience themselves as
professionals, educators, journalists, or administrators doing their
jobs — not as agents of a dominant formation. This is not conspiracy but
structural position: the formation selects, trains, promotes, and
rewards personnel who organically elaborate its world-view.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci's extended analysis of intellectuals (pp.
5-23) establishes the organic/traditional distinction. He traces how
the European bourgeoisie created its own organic intellectuals (the
economist, the industrial technician, the political organiser) while
assimilating the traditional intellectuals of the feudal order (the
clergy, the classical scholar). His analysis of the Italian school
system (pp. 26-43) shows how educational institutions function as the
primary mechanism for producing and selecting intellectuals. His notes
on journalism (pp. 382-386) describe how media functions as an
intellectual apparatus, "a veritable school for the adults" (p. 382)
that reproduces common sense daily. His insistence that "all men are
intellectuals... but not all men have in society the function of
intellectuals" (p. 9) establishes that the intellectual stratum is a
functional category, not a cognitive one.
GENERALISES TO: Any formation that maintains itself through a
specialised stratum of personnel who organise, elaborate, and transmit
its world-view — whether these are the clergy of a religious
civilisation, the administrative class of an imperial system, the
professional-managerial class of a market democracy, or the
technocratic elite of a digital governance order. The mechanism
explains why challenging a formation by proposing alternative ideas
fails when the personnel system that carries, evaluates, and
distributes ideas remains intact. Ideas without a personnel
infrastructure are structurally impotent.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Confirms Anticipatory Obedience (organic
intellectuals advance the hegemonic programme without explicit
instruction — they anticipate because they have been organically
formed by the formation); extends the pattern by specifying the
production mechanism (it is not merely psychological but
institutional: selection, training, promotion, reward).
STATUS: observed

### P4: Passive Revolution — The Absorption of Challenges

**PRINCIPLE**: Dominant formations with institutional depth do not defeat
challenges by repressing them. They metabolise them. Passive revolution
incorporates just enough of an oppositional programme to neutralise its
threat while preserving the fundamental power arrangement. This operates
through "molecular changes" — gradual adjustments, co-optation of
oppositional personnel, partial adoption of oppositional rhetoric, and
strategic concessions that redefine the terms of debate without altering
structural relations. The diagnostic question: does apparent change
represent genuine structural transformation or absorption designed to
prevent it?

LAYERS: Institutional, Thought & Narrative, Economic
MECHANISM: Gramsci develops the concept of passive revolution from the
Italian Risorgimento, where the Piedmontese monarchy unified Italy "from
above" by absorbing the democratic-republican programme of Mazzini and
Garibaldi without implementing its substantive content. The form of
national unification was achieved; the structural transformation
(agrarian reform, democratic participation, economic redistribution) was
prevented. The mechanism operates through "molecular" processes:
individual leaders co-opted, specific demands partially met, oppositional
rhetoric adopted by the dominant formation, the terms of debate shifted
so that what was once a radical demand becomes a moderate reform within
the existing framework. Passive revolution is not stasis — the system
genuinely changes — but the changes are calibrated to preserve the
fundamental arrangement of power. The dominant formation demonstrates
flexibility precisely to prevent structural transformation.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci's analysis of the Risorgimento (pp. 44-120)
is the primary case. The Moderate Party "led" the Action Party by
absorbing its cadres and programme: "The Moderates continued to lead
the Action Party even after 1870 and 1876, and so-called 'transformism'
was only the parliamentary expression of this action of intellectual,
moral and political hegemony" (p. 58). The concept of "revolution-
restoration" or "revolution without a revolution" (pp. 108-114) captures
the mechanism: the revolution occurs in form but is neutralised in
content. Gramsci extends this analysis to Fascism itself as a passive
revolution — an absorption of mass demands through a statist framework
that preserved the fundamental economic order (pp. 119-120). The concept
of "molecular changes" that "progressively modify the pre-existing
composition of forces, and hence become the matrix of new changes" (p.
109) describes the gradualist mechanism through which absorption operates.
GENERALISES TO: Any hegemonic system's response to challenge — the
adoption of environmental rhetoric without structural change, the
incorporation of diversity language without redistribution of power,
the absorption of populist demands into technocratic management. The
diagnostic applies wherever a system appears to change in response to
pressure: is the change structural (altering power relations) or
absorptive (preserving them under new vocabulary)?
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Provides the theoretical mechanism for Formal
Transformation, Structural Continuity. The pattern documents the
phenomenon; Gramsci theorises the mechanism: passive revolution is
how structural continuity is achieved through formal transformation.
Also extends Capture Pipeline beyond institutional capture to the
capture of entire oppositional programmes.
STATUS: observed

### P5: The Asymmetry of Institutional Response Time

**PRINCIPLE**: In moments of hegemonic crisis, the outcome is determined
not by the justice of the challenge but by the speed differential
between the dominant formation's capacity to reorganise and the
challenger's capacity to organise. The dominant formation possesses
trained cadres, institutional memory, organisational infrastructure, and
analytical resources that allow it to reabsorb control faster than
subordinate groups can accumulate coordination. This asymmetry is a deep
mechanism of hegemonic durability — it explains why crises that seem to
threaten the system reliably resolve in the system's favour.

LAYERS: Institutional, Thought & Narrative, Economic
MECHANISM: Gramsci's analysis of the "conjunctural" versus "organic"
distinction in political crises (pp. 177-185) identifies the asymmetry.
The dominant formation, even in crisis, retains its organisational
infrastructure: the state apparatus, the party system, the media
apparatus, the educational system, the professional associations, the
trained administrative cadre. Subordinate groups in crisis must build
coordination from scratch — they lack the institutional memory, the
trained personnel, the communication infrastructure, and the analytical
resources that the dominant formation possesses as a structural
inheritance. The crisis may be real — the dominant formation may
genuinely lose legitimacy, face economic disruption, see its political
representatives discredited — but the subordinate groups must convert
spontaneous rejection into organised alternative faster than the
dominant formation can reorganise. History documents that the dominant
formation almost always wins this race, because it begins with an
enormous structural advantage in organisational capacity.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci's analysis of how the Italian ruling class
survived the "organic crisis" of 1919-1920 — the Biennio Rosso, when
factory councils and mass strikes threatened the existing order — and
reconstituted itself through Fascism illustrates the mechanism. The
working class had spontaneous energy but lacked the organisational
infrastructure to convert it into a new hegemony; the ruling class,
though politically discredited, retained control of the state apparatus,
the military, the judiciary, and the economic infrastructure (pp.
210-211). Gramsci's concept of "interregnum" — "the crisis consists
precisely in the fact that the old is dying and the new cannot be born;
in this interregnum a great variety of morbid symptoms appear" (p.
276) — captures the structural moment when the asymmetry determines
outcomes. His analysis of Caesar and Napoleon (pp. 219-223) as
"Caesarist" solutions to crises where neither the progressive nor
the reactionary force can prevail describes the system's capacity to
reorganise through authoritarian mediation when normal hegemonic
mechanisms fail.
GENERALISES TO: Any hegemonic crisis where the existing formation faces
challenge — financial crises that produce mass discontent but resolve in
favour of existing financial institutions, democratic crises that
produce protest movements but resolve through absorption or
authoritarian consolidation, technological disruptions that appear to
distribute power but are captured by existing concentrations. The
mechanism is structural, not contingent: organisational infrastructure
is a form of capital that accumulates over generations and cannot be
improvised in a crisis.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends the Peacetime Ratchet by specifying what
happens when the ratchet appears to break — even in crisis, the
structural advantage of accumulated institutional infrastructure
favours the existing formation. Extends Institutional Abdication by
providing the counter-case: institutions do not always abdicate; they
may reorganise and reassert control precisely when challenged.
STATUS: observed

### P6: Civil Society as Deep Defence — The Fortresses-and-Earthworks Principle

**PRINCIPLE**: In advanced formations, the State is not the primary seat
of power. It is an "outer ditch" behind which stands a powerful system
of "fortresses and earthworks" — the dense network of civil society
institutions that collectively constitute the hegemonic apparatus. A
crisis that shakes the government leaves this deep infrastructure
intact. Hegemonic systems in complex societies cannot be transformed by
capturing the State alone (war of manoeuvre). They require the slow,
patient, molecular transformation of civil society itself (war of
position) — a generational project.

LAYERS: Institutional (primary), Thought & Narrative, Legal & Regulatory
MECHANISM: Gramsci distinguishes between "war of manoeuvre" (the direct
frontal assault on the state — insurrection, revolution, regime change)
and "war of position" (the slow, systematic construction of alternative
hegemonic institutions within civil society). In societies with a
rudimentary civil society, war of manoeuvre can succeed — seizing the
state captures the primary mechanism of control. But in societies with
a developed civil society — dense networks of schools, churches, media,
professional associations, cultural institutions, voluntary
organisations — the state is merely the visible political apparatus.
The real infrastructure of hegemony is distributed across civil society.
Capturing or disrupting the state leaves this infrastructure intact,
and it will regenerate the hegemonic order. This is why advanced
hegemonic formations survive regime changes, revolutions, and political
crises: the deep infrastructure of consent reproduces itself through
institutions that no single political event can reach.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci's military metaphor is explicit: "In Russia
the State was everything, civil society was primordial and gelatinous;
in the West, there was a proper relation between State and civil society,
and when the State trembled a sturdy structure of civil society was at
once revealed. The State was only an outer ditch, behind which there
stood a powerful system of fortresses and earthworks" (p. 238). His
argument for the war of position as "the only viable possibility"
(pp. 238-239, 242-243) in advanced societies is a direct consequence
of this analysis. His discussion of the relationship between political
and civil society (pp. 12, 206-209, 235, 243) consistently returns to
the density of civil society as the determining factor in hegemonic
durability. His critique of Trotsky's theory of permanent revolution
(pp. 236-238) rests on the argument that what worked in Russia (war
of manoeuvre against a state with no civil society behind it) cannot
work in the West (where civil society constitutes the deep defence).
GENERALISES TO: Any complex society where institutional infrastructure
extends beyond the state — including contemporary market democracies
where the hegemonic apparatus includes corporate media, university
systems, think tanks, professional associations, digital platforms,
and the diffuse infrastructure of cultural production. The principle
explains why political revolutions in advanced societies (changes of
government, even constitutional transformations) routinely fail to
produce the structural changes they promise: the deep institutional
infrastructure of civil society reproduces the prior arrangement.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Provides the theoretical foundation for Layered
Redundancy of Control. The pattern describes overlapping mechanisms;
Gramsci theorises why the redundancy exists (civil society as deep
defence) and why it is so effective (distributed across institutions
that no single intervention can reach simultaneously). Also confirms
Axiom 2 (layers reinforce each other — the civil society institutions
each independently reproduce the hegemonic framework, creating
structural redundancy).
STATUS: observed

### P7: The Three-Level Analysis of Force Relations

**PRINCIPLE**: Any adequate assessment of a hegemonic situation must
distinguish three levels: (a) the objective relation of social forces,
rooted in production; (b) the relation of political forces — the degree
of homogeneity, self-awareness, and organisation of various groups; and
(c) the relation of military/coercive forces. The critical transition —
the hegemonic moment — occurs at level (b), when one group's interests
transcend their narrow base and become genuinely coordinated with the
interests of subordinate groups. The dominant group must make real
concessions to maintain the coalition — this is why mature hegemonic
systems serve real interests of subordinate groups while
disproportionately serving the dominant group.

LAYERS: All layers
MECHANISM: Level (a) is the structural base — the distribution of
productive capacity and material resources that constrains what is
politically possible. Level (b) is where hegemony is constructed or
contested. Gramsci identifies three sub-phases within the political
level: (i) the "economic-corporate" phase, where a group recognises
its shared professional or trade interests but not its broader
political identity; (ii) the "class solidarity" phase, where the group
recognises shared interests as a class but only within the existing
institutional framework; and (iii) the "hegemonic" phase, where the
group's interests are presented as — and to some degree genuinely
become — the interests of all subordinate groups. This third phase is
the hegemonic moment: the dominant group achieves leadership not merely
through imposition but through a genuine (if asymmetric) coordination
of interests. The coalition requires real concessions — the dominant
group must actually serve some interests of the subordinate groups to
maintain their consent. This is why mature hegemonic systems are not
simply exploitative — they deliver real benefits to the subordinate
population while disproportionately benefiting the dominant group.
Level (c) — the military-coercive — is decisive only when the
political level fails to produce a hegemonic resolution.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci's analysis of the "relation of forces"
(pp. 180-183) is the primary text. The three levels and the sub-phases
of level (b) are laid out systematically. The hegemonic moment is
described as the phase where "previously germinated ideologies...
come into confrontation and conflict, until only one of them, or at
least a single combination of them, tends to prevail, to gain the
upper hand, to propagate itself throughout society — bringing about
not only a unison of economic and political aims, but also
intellectual and moral unity... thus creating the hegemony of a
fundamental social group over a series of subordinate groups" (p.
181-182). The requirement of real concessions is explicit: the
dominant group's interests "prevail, but only up to a certain point,
i.e. stopping short of narrowly corporate economic interest" (p. 182).
GENERALISES TO: Any assessment of power in a complex society. The
three-level analysis provides a diagnostic framework: at what level is
the current configuration primarily operating? Is the dominant
formation at the hegemonic phase (genuine coordination of interests)
or has it regressed to the economic-corporate phase (narrow
self-interest without broader legitimacy)? The framework applies to
any hegemonic arrangement — the assessment of whether a formation has
achieved genuine hegemony or merely dominance is a diagnostic question
applicable in any context.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Complicates Axiom 5 (power concentrates unless
actively counteracted) by specifying that durable concentration
requires genuine concession. The directive "who is accumulating power
here, and at whose expense?" may miss cases where power concentrates
while simultaneously delivering real benefits to subordinate groups.
The expense is not necessarily zero-sum; the asymmetry is
disproportionate rather than absolute.
STATUS: observed

### P8: The Generational Timescale of Hegemonic Construction

**PRINCIPLE**: Hegemony is built across generations through the school
system, through the elaboration and reproduction of intellectual strata,
through the slow sedimentation of "common sense," through institutional
training of cadres. Permanent collective wills are formed through long
molecular processes, not sudden explosions. Hegemonic shifts that appear
sudden are invariably the surface manifestation of molecular processes
underway for decades. The appropriate unit of analysis for hegemonic
change is the generation, not the electoral cycle or the news cycle.

LAYERS: Institutional (primary), Thought & Narrative
MECHANISM: Hegemonic systems reproduce themselves through institutions
that operate on generational timescales. The school system produces
intellectuals across twenty-year cycles. Common sense sediments across
centuries. Organisational capacity accumulates through institutional
memory passed from one generation of cadres to the next. A hegemonic
crisis may appear sudden — a revolution, a collapse, a realignment —
but Gramsci insists that such events are always the surface expression
of "organic" processes that have been developing over long periods. The
"organic" can only be distinguished from the "conjunctural" by observing
processes across generational spans. Conversely, building counter-
hegemony is a generational project: it requires creating educational
institutions, training intellectuals, developing an alternative common
sense, building organisational infrastructure — none of which can be
accomplished within a political cycle. This is the temporal asymmetry
of hegemonic politics: the existing formation operates through
institutions built across generations; the challenger must build
equivalent institutions from scratch.
EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Gramsci's analysis of the school system (pp. 26-43)
treats education as the primary mechanism of generational reproduction:
"the school is the instrument through which intellectuals of various
levels are elaborated" (p. 10). His concept of the "national-popular
collective will" emphasises the temporal depth of hegemonic formation:
"the decisive element in every situation is the permanently organised
and long-prepared force which can be put into the field when it is
judged that a situation is favourable" — and these forces "can only
be formed during a long process, through a whole series of ideological,
political, and organisational intermediaries" (p. 185). His notes on
the formation of intellectuals (pp. 10-11, 17-23) emphasise the
multi-generational process by which a social group elaborates its own
intellectual stratum. His analysis of the Catholic Church's centuries-
long investment in its intellectual apparatus (pp. 7, 332-334) provides
the paradigmatic example of generational hegemonic construction.
GENERALISES TO: Any hegemonic system where institutional reproduction
operates across generational timescales — the multi-century
construction of liberal-democratic common sense, the generational
reproduction of technocratic governance norms, the decades-long
construction of the neoliberal policy consensus through think tanks,
university departments, and professional training programmes. The
principle explains why political victories that do not build
institutional infrastructure are reversed within a generation, and
why institutional investments that appear inconsequential in the short
term can be transformative across decades.
FRAMEWORK STATUS: Extends Axiom 4 (mechanisms are consistent;
manifestations vary) by specifying the timescale at which consistency
becomes visible. Patterns that appear novel within a single political
cycle may be variations of mechanisms visible only at generational
timescales.
STATUS: observed

## Key patterns corroborated

### Appearance as Structural Terrain
CORROBORATION: Hegemony operates precisely in the gap between the
system's presentation (as representing the universal interest) and its
operation (as disproportionately serving the dominant group). "Common
sense" is the terrain — the background of unexamined assumptions that
makes the gap invisible. The hegemonic group presents its corporate
interest as the general interest, and this presentation is not merely
propaganda but the operating environment in which hegemonic power
functions. When the gap becomes visible — when the claim to universality
is exposed as particular — the hegemonic formation enters crisis.
NOTE: Gramsci provides the deepest theoretical grounding for this
pattern in the corpus. The gap is not incidental to hegemony; it is
constitutive.

### Formal Transformation, Structural Continuity
CORROBORATION: Passive revolution is the mechanism. The Risorgimento
unified Italy in form while preventing structural transformation in
content. The Moderate Party absorbed the Action Party's programme,
personnel, and rhetoric while preserving the fundamental power
arrangement. "Transformism" — the parliamentary absorption of
oppositional leaders — is the institutional expression of the pattern.
Gramsci explicitly theorises how systems change form to preserve
structure: "revolution without a revolution" (pp. 108-114).
NOTE: Primary-source theorisation of the mechanism. Extends the pattern
from empirical observation to structural theory.

### Institutional Abdication
CORROBORATION: Gramsci's concept of "crisis of hegemony" or "crisis of
authority" describes the moment when "social classes become detached from
their traditional parties" and institutions cease to represent their
constituency (p. 210). The existing institutional representatives "are
recognised as no longer expressing the class or fraction of a class"
and the crisis manifests as a vacuum that opens space for "the field of
'charismatic' solutions" (pp. 210-211). This is institutional abdication
theorised from the perspective of the hegemonic system: the institutions
fail not because they are captured but because they lose their organic
connection to the forces they represent.
NOTE: Extends the pattern by providing the systemic context — abdication
occurs when the hegemonic formation can no longer maintain the consent
apparatus.

### Anticipatory Obedience
CORROBORATION: The organic intellectual system produces individuals who
advance the hegemonic programme without explicit instruction. They are
not commanded to reproduce the dominant world-view; they do so because
their formation — educational, professional, cultural — has made that
world-view their own. They anticipate and elaborate the hegemonic
programme because they have been organically produced by it. This is
anticipatory obedience at the personnel level: the system does not need
to instruct its intellectuals because it has formed them.
NOTE: Gramsci provides the production mechanism for anticipatory
obedience. Snyder documents the phenomenon; Gramsci explains how the
personnel who exhibit it are produced.

### Middle Stratum Trap
CORROBORATION: Organic intellectuals of subordinate groups are
systematically assimilated by the dominant formation. The subaltern
group's most capable organisers, thinkers, and leaders are absorbed
into the dominant intellectual apparatus — through education, career
advancement, co-optation, or the simple fact that the dominant
formation controls the institutions through which intellectual work
is recognised, funded, and distributed. This prevents the subaltern
from developing autonomous intellectual leadership. The subaltern
group is "always subject to the activity of ruling groups, even when
it rebels and rises up" (p. 55) because it lacks the intellectual
infrastructure for autonomous organisation.
NOTE: Extends the pattern from economic middle stratum to intellectual
middle stratum — the mechanism of trap is assimilation of potential
leaders, not merely marginal privilege.

### Layered Redundancy of Control
CORROBORATION: Civil society as deep defence is the theoretical
foundation for layered redundancy. Schools, churches, media,
professional associations, cultural institutions each independently
reproduce the hegemonic framework. The redundancy is structural: no
single institution is sufficient, but the ensemble is resilient because
disruption of any one institution leaves all others intact. The "war
of position" doctrine is a direct consequence — hegemony cannot be
challenged by attacking any single node because the other nodes
compensate.
NOTE: Gramsci provides the most complete theorisation of why redundancy
exists in hegemonic systems. It is not designed but emergent from the
distributed character of civil society.

### Capture Pipeline
CORROBORATION: Passive revolution operates as a capture mechanism for
entire oppositional programmes. The assimilation of traditional
intellectuals by successive dominant formations (pp. 7-8, 10-12) is a
personnel capture pipeline. "Transformism" — the absorption of
individual oppositional leaders into the dominant parliamentary bloc
(pp. 58-59) — is capture at the political level. The mechanism is
systematic: the dominant formation identifies which elements of the
opposition can be absorbed without threatening fundamental arrangements,
and incorporates them while isolating or marginalising the elements
that cannot.
NOTE: Extends the pattern from institutional capture (Whitehouse,
Project 2025) to hegemonic capture — the absorption of challenges at
the civilisational scale.

### Double Performance
CORROBORATION: The hegemonic group performs universal interest while
pursuing corporate interest. The subordinate group performs consent
while maintaining what Scott would call a hidden transcript. Gramsci's
analysis of the Moderate Party's performance of national unity while
pursuing Piedmontese/conservative interests (pp. 55-60) is a direct
case. His concept of "intellectual and moral leadership" as distinct
from "domination" captures the performance dimension — the hegemonic
group must genuinely lead, not merely dominate, which requires
performing the role of universal representative.
NOTE: Gramsci theorises the structural necessity of the dominant
group's performance. Scott documents the subordinate group's
performance. Together they complete the pattern.

## IC-1 flags

### Axiom 3 extension: common sense as a fifth mode of invisible power

Gramsci's "common sense" is the most complete theorisation in the corpus
of how contingency becomes invisible — not through concealment, not
through visible mechanism with hidden contingency, not through
prohibitive cost, and not through degraded perception, but through the
production of a background ontology that makes the current arrangement
appear as the nature of things. Common sense is not a failure of
perception (the subject cannot see) or a failure of capacity (the
subject sees but cannot act) or a failure of epistemic processing (the
subject's reasoning is compromised). It is the prior condition: the
framework through which perception, capacity, and reasoning operate
has been constituted by the hegemonic formation. The question "could
things be otherwise?" does not arise — not because it is suppressed
but because common sense does not generate it. This extends Axiom 3
beyond its four existing modes. The four modes assume a subject who
encounters the mechanism and either cannot see it, cannot see its
contingency, cannot afford to act, or cannot process what is seen.
Gramsci's common sense describes the condition where the mechanism is
not encountered as a mechanism at all — it is the background against
which all mechanisms are evaluated. This is the deepest stratum of
invisible power.

### Axiom 5 complication: durable concentration requires genuine concession

Gramsci complicates the axiom by documenting how hegemonic systems must
make real concessions to subordinate groups to maintain consent. The
hegemonic moment — when one group's interests become genuinely
coordinated with the interests of subordinate groups — requires that
the dominant group "not only... unify the economic and political aims,
but also intellectual and moral unity" (p. 182). This is not merely
performance: the dominant group must actually serve some interests of
subordinate groups to sustain the coalition. Power concentrates, but
durable concentration requires genuine (not merely apparent)
coordination with subordinate interests. The axiom's directive ("who
is accumulating power here, and at whose expense?") may miss cases
where the expense is distributed across society while the accumulation
benefits the dominant group disproportionately but not exclusively.
Mature hegemonic systems are not zero-sum — they deliver real benefits
while concentrating power — and this is precisely what makes them
durable. The directive might be refined: "who benefits
disproportionately, and what real concessions sustain the arrangement?"

### Axiom 10 complication: understanding requires institutional organisation to resist

Gramsci's war of position is the most sustained argument in the corpus
that understanding CAN resist — but only when it is organised through
institutional capacity (the alternative intellectual personnel system)
and sustained across generational timescales. Individual understanding
— the worker who sees the mechanism (Terkel), the tenant who names the
exploitation (Desmond), the philosopher who analyses the system — is
structurally impotent without an institutional infrastructure to carry,
elaborate, distribute, and sustain that understanding across time. This
specifies the conditions under which Axiom 10 holds and when it fails.
Understanding resists when: (a) it is carried by a trained intellectual
stratum, (b) that stratum is embedded in institutional infrastructure
(schools, media, organisations, parties), (c) that infrastructure
operates on generational timescales, and (d) the understanding has been
translated from individual insight into "good sense" — a systematic,
coherent world-view capable of displacing common sense. Without all
four conditions, understanding is the Terkel/Desmond pattern:
structurally complete but operationally inert. Gramsci's entire
political project — building counter-hegemonic institutions — is the
practical programme for converting understanding into resistance.
