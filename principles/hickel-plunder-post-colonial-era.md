# Principles: Plunder in the Post-Colonial Era

SOURCE: Jason Hickel, Dylan Sullivan & Huzaifa Zoomkawala, "Plunder in the
Post-Colonial Era: Quantifying Drain from the Global South Through Unequal
Exchange, 1960–2018," *New Political Economy* 26(6): 1030–1047 (2021).

PERSPECTIVE: Position 5 (academic — economic anthropologist, political
economist, and independent scholar; LSE, University of Sydney, Karachi).
Peer-reviewed quantitative analysis within world-systems / unequal-exchange
theoretical tradition (Emmanuel, Amin, Wallerstein, Frank, Patnaik). The
authors are open about their political-economic commitments — Hickel
publishes extensively on global inequality, degrowth, and post-development
in both academic and public-facing venues.

CENTRAL THESIS: Drain from the global South to the global North through
unequal exchange remains a significant feature of the post-colonial world
economy. In 2017, the North appropriated commodities worth $2.2 trillion
from the South (Northern prices); over the period 1960–2017 the cumulative
drain totalled $62 trillion, or $152 trillion when accounting for
forgone growth. The intensity of exploitation (price distortion factor)
doubled during the structural adjustment period of the 1980s–1990s.
Appropriation is maintained through monopoly power deployed via
international institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO), trade architecture
(TRIPS, patent monopoly), labor immobility (militarized borders), and
historical interventions against governments that attempted to raise
Southern wages and prices. The mechanism is hidden in the price structure
of the world economy.

- **Contemporary debt-mechanics expertise** — specific institutional

The framework should treat this extraction as the analytical core of a

extractions to ground adequately. Hickel et al. (2021) are not the
foundational text of the tradition — they are its contemporary
quantitative front. The genealogy (Emmanuel 1972, Amin 1976, Frank 1967,
Wallerstein 1974, Galeano 1971, Rodney 1972, Patnaik & Patnaik 2017)
remains unextracted.

Positional lens: The authors write from elite Anglophone academic
institutions (LSE, University of Sydney) but explicitly position
themselves within a tradition developed largely by global-south
intellectuals. The paper's evidentiary base is empirical (PWT, IMF DOTS,
ILO wage data, OECD ICE data) — the conclusions are not philosophical
claims but quantitative findings about specific price ratios over specific
time periods. The political-economic interpretation of those findings
(imperialism, plunder, structural extraction) is theoretical, but the
underlying empirical findings are independently verifiable from public
datasets.

## Principles

### P1: Unequal Exchange as Hidden Value Transfer

PRINCIPLE: Systematic price asymmetries between core (global North) and
periphery (global South) produce uncompensated transfer of labor and
resources through ordinary international trade. The mechanism is hidden
in the price structure of the world economy rather than operating
through visible appropriation — when commodities valued at Northern
prices are exchanged for Southern-valued commodities, the South must
export many more units of value to receive one unit back. The transfer
is structurally invisible to ordinary trade accounting because it
operates through the price relationship rather than the volume of
exchange.

LAYERS: Economic (primary), Thought & Narrative

MECHANISM: The Exchange Rate Deviation Index (ERDI) measures how much
higher a country's prices would need to be to reach parity with the US
price level. ERDI in core countries is close to 1; in periphery
countries it ranges from 2 to 4 (i.e., prices are 2-4 times lower).
This price differential means that for every unit of embodied labor or
resources the South imports from the North, it must export multiple
units to pay for it. Hickel et al. calculate the resulting drain
formally: T = d*X - X, where T is value transferred, X is exports from
periphery to core, and d is the price-distortion factor (the ratio of
the periphery's ERDI to the core's ERDI). In 2017 the drain amounted to
$2.2 trillion (Northern prices); cumulative drain 1960-2017 was $62
trillion in constant 2011 dollars, or $152 trillion accounting for the
growth foregone. The drain represents up to 7% of Northern GDP and 9%
of Southern GDP at peak. The transfer is invisible to participants
because no explicit appropriation occurs — the prices are simply what
they are, and the exchange is voluntary. The structural relationship
that makes those prices what they are is not visible in any single
transaction.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Penn World Table 9.1 GDPe data, IMF Direction of
Trade Statistics, OECD Import Content of Exports figures. Köhler's
formula for value transfer (Köhler 1998, 2003) extended with annual
estimates 1960-2017. Triangulated across two methodologies — exchange-
rate differentials (primary) and wage-rate differentials (Cope 2019).
Both methods produce the same qualitative finding; magnitudes differ.
Specific country findings: China lost $19tn cumulative (102% of GDP);
Mexico $2.8tn (120% of GDP); Vietnam $1.2tn (188% of GDP); the US
gained $18.3tn (103% of GDP).

GENERALIZES TO: Any system in which price relationships rather than
explicit appropriation produce systematic transfer of value. The
mechanism applies inside national economies (rural-urban wage
differentials maintained by labor immobility) and across firms (supply
chain monopsony — Apple's iPad price $1,077 below labor-and-ecological
value per Clelland 2014). The structural feature is that extraction
operates through the price system, making it invisible to ordinary
economic accounting and to the participants.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: provides the strongest quantitative anchor in the
framework for Axiom 3 (invisible mechanism) in the Economic layer —
the mechanism is invisible not because it is concealed but because
prices appear as natural facts; extends the Appearance as Structural
Terrain pattern to a quantitative form (the price structure is the
appearance; the structural relationship is the function); strongly
corroborates Manufactured Scarcity as Control (the South's fiscal
foreclosure is produced by this drain, not by domestic factors)

STATUS: observed (the quantitative findings, peer-reviewed); inferred
(the interpretation as imperial extraction — operates within a
theoretical tradition that has critics)

### P2: Monopoly Power as Price-Maintenance Architecture

PRINCIPLE: The price differentials that produce unequal exchange are
not natural — they are produced and maintained through specific
deployments of geopolitical, commercial, legal, and institutional
monopoly power. Without the institutional architecture that maintains
the price differential, the differential would collapse and the
transfer would stop. The architecture is therefore load-bearing for
the extraction.

LAYERS: Economic, Legal & Regulatory, Institutional, Thought & Narrative

MECHANISM: Five specific mechanisms documented in the paper:
(1) **Patent monopoly under TRIPS** — Northern firms control 97% of
patents (Chang 2008); the TRIPS agreement under the WTO enforces this
monopoly globally, enabling rent extraction well in excess of
free-market rates. (2) **IMF/World Bank voting** — G8 holds a majority
share of votes in both institutions, allowing them to set the rules of
international finance and impose conditionalities. (3) **WTO bargaining
asymmetry** — bargaining power is determined by market size, enabling
high-income countries to set trade rules in their interests.
(4) **Structural adjustment** — IMF/World Bank conditionalities have
cut public-sector wages and employment, rolled back labor rights,
curtailed unions, and removed tariffs/subsidies, preventing infant-
industry protection. (5) **Corporate monopsony** — a small number of
Northern firms have grown so large that their revenues exceed the GDP
of most sovereign countries (Vitali et al. 2011), giving them the
ability to set final prices and depress input costs across supply
chains (Suwandi 2019). Together these mechanisms compound: each
reinforces the others, and dismantling any one alone would not collapse
the price differential.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: 97% Northern patent control figure (Chang 2008,
p. 141); WTO/IMF/World Bank governance structure; structural-adjustment
history (Khor 1995; Petras and Veltmeyer 2002); corporate concentration
(Vitali et al. 2011; Anderson and Cavanagh 2000); Apple supply-chain
monopsony case (Clelland 2014); Foxconn labor discipline (Ngai and Chan
2012).

GENERALIZES TO: Any market-mediated extraction relationship whose
appearance as voluntary exchange depends on institutional and legal
architecture that prevents the supposed market mechanism from
functioning. The principle: when an extraction is named "free trade,"
look for the legal, institutional, and physical-coercive infrastructure
that maintains the conditions of the exchange.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: corroborates the Wealth Defense Industry pattern at
international scale (IMF/World Bank/WTO/TRIPS as global wealth-defense
architecture); corroborates the Capture Pipeline pattern (TRIPS as
Northern firm preferences enforced through WTO institutional process);
provides cross-layer integration unusual in the framework — the price
differential (Economic) is shown to be maintained by Legal & Regulatory
(TRIPS), Institutional (IMF/World Bank/WTO), and indirectly by
Physical & Coercive layer mechanisms (Principle P3)

STATUS: observed

### P3: Border Enforcement as Wage Differential Maintenance

PRINCIPLE: Militarized international borders prevent labor mobility
from South to North, preventing international wage convergence and
maintaining the wage differential that produces unequal exchange.
Northern wages are approximately five times Southern wages; in a
labor-mobile world this gap would close through migration. The
physical-coercive border infrastructure is therefore a load-bearing
component of the economic extraction system. The wage differential
cannot be understood as an economic phenomenon abstracted from the
physical-coercive structure that maintains labor immobility.

LAYERS: Physical & Coercive, Economic

MECHANISM: Cope (2019, p. 80) documents that Southern wages average
one-fifth of Northern wages. Migration would, in principle, equalize
these wages — workers would move from low-wage to high-wage labor
markets until the marginal product of labor equalized. This is the
standard neoclassical prediction. The prediction does not occur because
international borders prevent the migration. Hickel et al. state this
directly: "Low wages are ultimately maintained through militarised
borders, which preclude easy migration from South to North, and thus
prevent international wage convergence." The physical-coercive
apparatus of border enforcement is therefore not an exogenous political
question but a constitutive component of the wage differential. The
five-fold wage gap is produced jointly by the economic relationships
inside the labor market and by the physical-coercive structure that
prevents the labor market from clearing internationally.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Wage differential figures (Cope 2019). The paper's
own statement on militarized borders as wage-defense mechanism.
Convergent evidence in the framework's existing corpus: De León (*The
Land of Open Graves*, 2015) on Prevention Through Deterrence as
structural violence at the US-Mexico border; García Hernández
(*Migrating to Prison*, 2019) on the crimmigration architecture.

GENERALIZES TO: Any system in which the economic layer's apparent
voluntariness depends on physical-coercive infrastructure that prevents
exit from the disadvantaged position. The principle: when a labor or
price differential persists across geographic regions, examine the
physical-coercive infrastructure that maintains the regional
separation. The mechanism applies at smaller scales (gated communities
maintaining residential separation, prison labor maintaining wage
suppression, internal-passport systems historically) and at larger
scales (the global border regime). What appears as an economic
phenomenon is often economic-physical-coercive joint production.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: provides a strong cross-layer integration finding —
the Economic and Physical & Coercive layers are documented as jointly
producing the wage differential; extends Axiom 1 (layers operate
simultaneously) with empirical-economic evidence; corroborates the
Constitutive Violence pattern (the wage differential is constituted
through border violence, not merely correlated with it); strongly
complements De León and García Hernández principles on border
violence by naming the economic function of the border regime

STATUS: observed (the wage differential and border enforcement);
inferred (the causal claim that one maintains the other — defensible
on standard neoclassical reasoning + empirical wage data + observed
migration pressure)

### P4: Structural Adjustment as Intensifier of Exploitation

PRINCIPLE: IMF and World Bank structural adjustment programmes (SAPs)
from the 1980s onward systematically cheapened Southern labor and
resources through policy intervention — cuts to public-sector wages,
rollback of labor rights, curtailment of unions, removal of tariffs
and subsidies, and forced dependence on foreign capital. The empirical
signature is that the price-distortion factor doubled from 1.4 to 2.8
during the structural adjustment period (1980s-1990s) while trade
volume also rose — what the paper calls "intensive growth" in unequal
exchange: not just more trade but more extraction per unit of trade.
The rate of extraction is not natural but produced by specific policy
intervention.

LAYERS: Legal & Regulatory, Economic, Institutional, Thought & Narrative

MECHANISM: SAPs imposed three structural changes that cheapened
Southern labor and resources: (1) **Direct wage suppression** —
public-sector wage cuts and labor-rights rollback reduced wage floors
across the economy. (2) **Subsidy removal** — removal of subsidies on
food, fuel, and basic services forced households into deeper market
dependence at lower real wages. (3) **Trade liberalization** — removal
of tariffs and infant-industry protections prevented the development
of domestic industries that would have competed with Northern firms
and raised Southern wages. (4) **Foreign-investment dependence** —
the resulting fiscal foreclosure made Southern governments compete
with one another to offer cheaper wages and resources to attract
foreign investment, a race-to-the-bottom that further depressed prices.
The empirical result is the doubling of the price-distortion factor:
during the 1960s and 1970s, the average price-distortion factor was
1.54; during the 1980s-1990s it rose to 2.8. The intensity of
extraction roughly doubled through specific policy intervention.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Figure 3 of the paper (Price Distortion Factor,
1960-2017) shows the doubling between 1980 and 2000. SAP literature
cited (Khor 1995; Petras and Veltmeyer 2002). Empirical claim:
"during the 1980s, the market value of the South's exports actually
fell, even though the quantity of commodities exported (measured at
Northern prices) was rising. In 1993, Southern exports had a lower
market price than they did in 1980, even though the value the North
was receiving increased significantly."

GENERALIZES TO: Any policy intervention that intensifies an extractive
relationship by systematically cheapening one side of the exchange.
The principle: extraction intensifies through specific policy choices,
not merely through the accumulation of normal economic activity. The
mechanism applies historically (Enclosure Acts intensifying labor
extraction in early modern England) and contemporaneously (right-to-
work laws, austerity programs in developed economies). The structural
adjustment period provides the cleanest documented case at global
scale.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: corroborates Capture Pipeline (IMF/World Bank
conditionalities as Northern preferences enforced through institutional
process); corroborates Manufactured Scarcity as Control (fiscal
scarcity produced by SAPs forecloses development options);
corroborates Anticipatory Obedience at sovereign scale (Southern
governments racing to offer cheaper conditions before any explicit
demand); empirically grounds the 1980s-1990s as the period during
which the contemporary global extraction architecture intensified

STATUS: observed (the price-distortion factor data and the policy
correlation); inferred (the causal claim that SAPs specifically
produced the doubling — defensible but not formally established by
this paper's methodology)

### P5: Methodological Nationalism as Ideological Cover for Structural Extraction

PRINCIPLE: The dominant assumption in development economics — that
national economic performance reflects internal domestic conditions
(institutions, governance, culture, corruption) rather than structural
relations between countries — erases the 500-year history of
inequitable relationships that have defined the global economy. The
analytical frame is itself a feature of the extractive system: by
analyzing each country in isolation, structural extraction becomes
invisible and "development" interventions are misdirected to domestic
policy. The framework that explains poverty in terms of domestic
factors is the ideological cover for the structural relationship that
actually produces it.

LAYERS: Thought & Narrative (primary), Economic, Institutional

MECHANISM: The paper opens by naming this directly: "The dominant
assumption in the field of international development holds that the
economic performance of nations is due primarily to their internal,
domestic conditions. High-income countries have achieved economic
success because of good governance, strong institutions and free
markets. Lower-income countries have failed to develop because they
lack these things, or because they suffer from corruption, red tape
and inefficiency." Citations: Sachs 2005, Collier 2007, Rostow 1960,
Moyo 2009, Calderisi 2006 — all canonical development-orthodoxy texts.
The frame produces specific operational consequences: development
interventions focus on "fixing domestic policy in global South
countries, with the assistance of aid from donor governments." This
misdirects effort and resources: the structural extraction continues
while interventions address its symptoms in domestic policy. The
methodological frame is the ideological cover that maintains the
extractive system.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: The paper's opening framing. The 500-year history
of inequitable relationships the paper documents. The contrast between
the development orthodoxy's predicted outcomes and the empirical
findings of unequal exchange ($62tn cumulative drain).

GENERALIZES TO: Any analytical frame that locates the cause of a
structural problem in the unit of analysis whose behavior is
constrained by the structural relationship. The principle: examine
whether the analytical frame itself functions as ideological cover by
foreclosing the structural-relational explanation. The mechanism
applies in domestic settings (poverty explained by individual choices
rather than structural conditions), institutional settings (organization
failure explained by individual incompetence rather than systemic
constraint), and international settings (national underdevelopment
explained by domestic governance rather than global extraction).

FRAMEWORK STATUS: provides the strongest case in the corpus of an
analytical frame functioning as ideological cover — the development
orthodoxy is named explicitly, its operational consequences are
specified, and the empirical alternative is quantified;
strongly corroborates Appearance as Structural Terrain pattern (the
frame is the appearance; the structural relationship is the function);
extends the Moral Infrastructure of Economic Power pattern (the
methodological frame places moral blame on the impoverished for
conditions produced by structural extraction); IC-7 relevant —
methodological nationalism is itself a maintaining force of the
hegemonic order

STATUS: observed (the existence and dominance of the frame); inferred
(its specific function as ideological cover for the extractive
relationship — though strongly supported by the quantitative findings)

### P6: Wage-Productivity Inversion

PRINCIPLE: Southern workers in export industries are often equally or
more productive than Northern counterparts, yet Southern wages are
approximately one-fifth of Northern wages. This refutes the
productivity-determines-wages account of global wage differentials.
Wages are determined by monopsony power, labor control regimes, and
the political-physical infrastructure that prevents labor mobility —
not by productivity. The "high wages reflect high productivity"
account functions as moral cover for an extraction relationship.

LAYERS: Economic, Thought & Narrative, Physical & Coercive

MECHANISM: The paper engages the Balassa-Samuelson defense of price
differentials (that prices are higher in high-income countries because
productivity is higher there) and produces empirical counter-evidence.
Baerresen (1971) found that Mexican metal workers, electronics workers,
and seamstresses produced 10-40% more output per hour than their US
counterparts in 1971; given the subsequent offshoring of plants and
equipment from US to Mexico, the productivity advantage has likely
increased. Foxconn workers in China are documented as highly efficient
through repressive labor discipline that "would fall foul of labour
law in the North" (Ngai and Chan 2012). The iPad's market price is
$1,077 below its true labor-and-ecological value (Clelland 2014). The
wage differential therefore reflects monopsony power and labor control,
not productivity. The "high productivity → high wages" account
inverts the actual causation: productive labor is appropriated through
monopoly power that prevents the productivity gains from being
captured by the producing workers.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Baerresen 1971 productivity study; Foxconn labor
discipline (Ngai and Chan 2012); Clelland 2014 on Apple supply chain
monopsony; the paper's own engagement with Subasat 2013 and Balassa-
Samuelson.

GENERALIZES TO: Any wage differential maintained alongside equal or
higher productivity of the lower-paid workers. The principle: when a
wage differential is justified by productivity claims, check the
empirical productivity data — the productivity claim often inverts
the actual relationship. The mechanism applies historically (slave
labor was highly productive; the wage was zero) and contemporaneously
(immigrant labor in agriculture; offshored manufacturing; gig-economy
work). The wage differential reflects power, not productivity.

FRAMEWORK STATUS: corroborates Moral Infrastructure of Economic
Power (the productivity-wage account functions morally — productive
people deserve higher wages — to justify a structural relationship);
corroborates Appearance as Structural Terrain (the productivity
narrative is the appearance; monopsony power is the function);
challenges meritocratic-myth principles already in the corpus
(Piketty's Meritocratic Myth at international scale)

STATUS: observed

### P7: Government Removal as Wage-Defense Mechanism

PRINCIPLE: When Southern governments attempt to improve wages and
resource prices, Western powers have historically intervened to remove
them from power. The wage and price differentials are not produced by
domestic conditions but actively defended through covert and overt
power exercise when threatened. This is the physical-coercive backstop
of the economic extraction architecture — the mechanism that activates
when the institutional and legal mechanisms (Principle P2) and the
border-enforcement mechanism (Principle P3) prove insufficient.

LAYERS: Physical & Coercive, Institutional, Economic

MECHANISM: The paper cites three canonical cases: Republic of Congo
(Lumumba, 1960); Indonesia (Sukarno, 1965); Chile (Allende, 1973).
The structural commonality: in each case, a Southern government
attempted to redirect resources from foreign extraction to domestic
benefit (nationalize resources, raise wages, restructure trade
relationships). In each case, a US-supported coup or assassination
removed the government and reversed the policy direction. The
historical frequency of such interventions during the 1960s-1980s is
the physical-coercive evidence that the extraction relationship is
actively defended, not naturally maintained. The intervention pattern
specifies when the extractive system uses overt force: when
non-coercive mechanisms (price structure, institutional rules, border
enforcement) prove insufficient to maintain the wage and price
differentials.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: Hickel (2017, ch. 4) cited for the historical
intervention cases; broader Cold War interventionism literature
implied. The paper does not reproduce extensive evidence on each case
(the citation does the work) but the cases are well-documented in the
broader scholarship (Westad on the global Cold War; Kinzer on US
interventions; Stone & Kuznick).

GENERALIZES TO: Any structurally maintained inequality whose continued
operation depends on the availability of force when challenged. The
principle: when an extractive relationship persists across changes in
the leadership of the extracted population, examine the history of
interventions that maintained the relationship during attempted
exits. The mechanism applies internationally (the 1960s-1980s
intervention pattern) and intra-nationally (state repression of labor
movements, civil rights movements, environmental movements that
threaten established extractive relationships).

FRAMEWORK STATUS: corroborates Constitutive Violence pattern at
international scale (the wage differential is constituted through
historical violence, not merely correlated with the legal-institutional
architecture); provides the cross-layer mechanism for how the
Economic layer is defended by the Physical & Coercive layer when
challenged; complements Browne's Boomerang Thesis (methods developed
at periphery migrate to center) by documenting the more standard
direction (force deployed from center to periphery to maintain
extraction)

STATUS: observed (the historical interventions); inferred (the
specific causal claim that the interventions functioned to defend
the wage and price differentials — defensible from the documentary
record but a theoretical interpretation)

### P8: The Aid-Drain Asymmetry

PRINCIPLE: The South receives approximately $100 billion per year in
Official Development Assistance (ODA) while losing approximately $1.4
trillion per year through unequal exchange, $486 billion through
profit repatriation, and $1.1 trillion through illicit financial
flows. For every $1 the South receives in aid, $14-30 is extracted.
The aid framework, which positions the North as benefactor and the
South as recipient, inverts the actual direction of net value flow.
The aid relationship is not a transfer from rich to poor; it is a
small back-transfer of a larger extraction.

LAYERS: Thought & Narrative, Economic, Institutional

MECHANISM: The paper presents the aid-drain ratio explicitly: "over
the past few years the South has lost around $1.4 trillion per year in
unequal exchange, while receiving around $100 billion per year in aid.
In other words, for every $1 the South receives in aid it loses $14
through unequal exchange (or $30, if we include losses due to profit
repatriation and illicit financial outflows)." The aid framework is
therefore a moral inversion: the recipient is structurally the donor
(in aggregate value flow), and the donor is structurally the
recipient. The framework operates as ideological cover: by positioning
the North as benefactor, it forecloses the structural-relational
account in which the net flow is from South to North. The aid
literature (Sachs 2005; Moyo 2009 on aid effectiveness) operates
within this inverted frame and is therefore systematically
mis-positioned relative to the structural relationship.

EVIDENCE FROM WORK: The aid-drain calculation explicitly stated;
Griffiths 2014 on profit repatriation; Kar and Spanjers 2015 on
illicit financial flows; the paper's discussion of ODA framing.

GENERALIZES TO: Any relationship in which a small visible transfer
in one direction obscures a larger structural transfer in the
opposite direction. The principle: when a moral relationship is
named ("donor"/"recipient", "patron"/"client", "investor"/"investee"),
quantify the full bidirectional flow before accepting the named
positions. The mechanism applies in employment (employer "provides"
job while extracting surplus), in finance (creditor "provides" credit
while extracting interest from indebted populations), and in
philanthropy (foundation "provides" grant while the foundation's

## New patterns identified

### Hidden Transfer Architecture (PRELIMINARY candidate — not created)

A pattern about value extraction through price structures rather than
visible appropriation. The mechanism: an exchange relationship in which
the price differential itself is the appropriation mechanism, such that
no individual transaction can be identified as extractive but the
aggregate of transactions transfers systematic value. May be a
specialized variant of Appearance as Structural Terrain (the price is
the appearance; the structural relationship producing the price is the
function) or may warrant separation. Deferred pending further
corroboration from related extractions (Patnaik & Patnaik, Cope, the
broader unequal-exchange tradition).

### Labor Immobility as Price-Defense Architecture (PRELIMINARY candidate — not created)

A pattern about the joint operation of the Economic and Physical &
Coercive layers in which physical-coercive infrastructure (borders,
internal passport systems, gated zones) maintains the labor immobility
that underlies price differentials in the Economic layer. Empirically
grounded in the wage-differential / border-enforcement relationship the
paper documents at global scale. Could extend down to smaller scales
(prison labor, gated employment) but framework convention is not to
promote a pattern from one extraction. Deferred pending corroboration.
