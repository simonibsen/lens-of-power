# Instrument: Positional Lens Diagnostic

> Role: Use this instrument to identify which position in a power relationship
> a source speaks from, what that position makes visible and invisible, and
> which positions are absent from the material being analyzed. The same
> mechanism of power looks fundamentally different depending on where you
> stand in the relationship.
>
> Derived from: Synthesis of Machiavelli (*The Prince*), Scott (*Weapons
> of the Weak*), and Fanon (*The Wretched of the Earth*) — three works
> that describe the same structures of power from ruler, everyday governed,
> and revolutionary governed positions respectively.

SOURCE: Cross-extraction synthesis (principles/machiavelli-the-prince.md,
principles/scott-weapons-of-the-weak.md,
principles/fanon-wretched-of-the-earth.md)

TAXONOMY LAYERS: All layers (this instrument concerns perspective, not a
specific layer)

ANALYTICAL USE: During ANALYZE steps 2 (SOURCE), 3 (LOCATE), and
6 (ABSENT)

---

## Positions

### Position 1: The Ruler / Operator

The perspective of those who exercise power, design systems, or administer
institutions.

- **Name**: Ruler / Operator
- **Description**: Speaks from the position of directing or managing a power
  relationship. Concerned with acquisition, maintenance, efficiency, and
  legitimacy of control. Treats the subjected population as an object to
  be managed.
- **Detection**: Language of strategy, management, optimization, governance,
  "stakeholder management." Treats the system's stability as the primary
  value. Discusses "how to" exercise authority rather than "whether"
  authority is justified. Policy language, administrative perspective,
  executive framing.
- **What this position makes visible**: The mechanics of control — how
  systems are designed, what trade-offs rulers face, how legitimacy is
  constructed, what strategies succeed or fail. The internal logic of
  power.
- **What this position makes invisible**: The experience of being subjected
  to those mechanics. The hidden transcript of the governed. The moral
  legitimacy question. The costs borne by those at the receiving end.
  Whether the system should exist at all.
- **Canonical example**: Machiavelli advises the prince to appear merciful
  while being prepared to act otherwise. The mechanism of appearance
  management is fully visible. What the peasant thinks about the
  performance is not considered.
- **Power function**: Sources speaking from this position naturalize the
  existence of the power relationship itself. The question "should this
  authority exist?" is outside the frame. Analysis from this position
  accepts the system and asks how to operate it.

### Position 2: The Everyday Governed

The perspective of those who live within a power structure, navigate it
daily, and resist it through non-confrontational means.

- **Name**: Everyday Governed
- **Description**: Speaks from the position of experiencing and navigating
  power without directly confronting it. Concerned with survival, dignity,
  minimizing extraction, and preserving autonomy within constraints.
  Operates through partial compliance, evasion, gossip, foot-dragging,
  and the hidden transcript.
- **Detection**: Language of coping, navigation, workaround, complaint.
  Ethnographic or first-person accounts of living within systems. Focus
  on the gap between official rules and actual practice. Attention to
  what people do vs. what they say they do. Informal networks, gossip,
  moral economy.
- **What this position makes visible**: How power is actually experienced
  rather than how it is designed. The gap between the system's claims and
  its effects. The hidden transcript — what people actually think vs.
  what they perform. The spectrum of compliance. The moral economy that
  the governed use to judge the powerful.
- **What this position makes invisible**: The system's internal logic and
  strategic calculations. The possibility of structural transformation
  (everyday resistance navigates the system; it does not imagine
  replacing it). The experience of those even further below — the
  most excluded, who lack even the capacity for everyday resistance.
- **Canonical example**: Scott's Sedaka villagers see through the wealthy
  farmers' performance of generosity and critique it through gossip. The
  mechanism of extraction is visible. The possibility of fundamentally
  restructuring land ownership is outside the frame.
- **Power function**: Sources speaking from this position reveal the
  texture of domination as lived experience. They challenge the ruler's
  narrative but may also naturalize the existence of the power structure
  itself — resistance is about negotiating better terms, not abolishing
  the arrangement.

### Position 3: The Revolutionary / Structural Critic

The perspective of those who seek to fundamentally transform or abolish
a power structure rather than navigate within it.

- **Name**: Revolutionary / Structural Critic
- **Description**: Speaks from the position of contesting the legitimacy
  of the power structure itself. Concerned with structural transformation,
  liberation, and the construction of alternatives. Treats the system as
  a totality to be replaced, not a set of terms to be negotiated.
- **Detection**: Language of liberation, structural change, systemic
  critique, revolution, decolonization, abolition. Analysis that treats
  the system itself as the problem rather than particular actors within
  it. Refusal to accept the frame offered by the system. Attention to
  constitutive violence and foundational illegitimacy.
- **What this position makes visible**: The totality of the system — how
  all layers operate together as a unified structure of domination. The
  constitutive violence underlying apparently neutral institutions. The
  impossibility of reform within a system constituted by coercion. The
  psychological dimensions of domination — internalization, alienation,
  the colonized mind.
- **What this position makes invisible**: The texture of everyday
  navigation within the system. The agency and creativity of the
  governed in their daily resistance. Internal differentiation within
  the subjected population. The practical difficulties of transformation.
  The possibility that some elements of the existing system serve genuine
  functions. Post-liberation risks (though Fanon is unusual in
  addressing these directly).
- **Canonical example**: Fanon argues that colonial violence is
  constitutive and can only be met with counter-violence. The total
  structure of domination is visible. The peasant's everyday strategies
  for navigating that structure — which Scott documents — are largely
  absent.
- **Power function**: Sources speaking from this position denaturalize
  the power structure entirely, making its contingency visible. But they
  may also overstate the system's coherence (treating it as a totality
  when it may contain contradictions and spaces for agency) and understate
  the governed population's existing capacity for resistance short of
  revolution.

### Position 4: The Intermediary / Relay

The perspective of those positioned between the dominant and the dominated,
mediating the relationship for their own benefit.

- **Name**: Intermediary / Relay
- **Description**: Speaks from the position of occupying a middle role —
  translating, administering, brokering — between the top and bottom of
  a power structure. Concerned with maintaining the position's value to
  both sides while extracting a share. May speak the language of either
  side depending on audience.
- **Detection**: Language of mediation, brokering, professional expertise,
  technical management. Claims to represent the interests of those below
  while operating within the framework set by those above. "Bridge"
  rhetoric. Development language, diversity language, reform language
  that proposes changing who occupies positions rather than what the
  positions do.
- **What this position makes visible**: The practical mechanics of how
  power is transmitted and translated through institutions. The
  complexity of administration. The tensions between different levels
  of a hierarchy.
- **What this position makes invisible**: The intermediary's own
  extractive role. The possibility that the intermediary function itself
  is part of the structure of domination. The experience of those at
  the bottom, which the intermediary claims to represent but may not
  share.
- **Canonical example**: Fanon's national bourgeoisie positions itself
  as representative of the liberated nation while functioning as a
  relay for continued external extraction. Its language is liberation;
  its function is intermediation.
- **Power function**: Sources speaking from this position often present
  themselves as neutral translators or advocates for the powerless. The
  instrument's value is in detecting when the intermediary's own
  interests diverge from those they claim to represent.

### Position 5: The External Observer / Analyst

The perspective of those studying a power relationship from outside it.

- **Name**: External Observer / Analyst
- **Description**: Speaks from a position of analytical distance. Academic
  researchers, journalists, international observers, consultants. Not
  directly subject to the power relationship being described but shaped
  by their own institutional position.
- **Detection**: Third-person analysis, theoretical frameworks, comparative
  language, methodological justification. Claims to objectivity or
  neutrality. Institutional affiliation (university, think tank, media
  outlet, international body).
- **What this position makes visible**: Structural patterns, comparisons
  across cases, theoretical insights not available to those embedded in
  the relationship.
- **What this position makes invisible**: The lived experience of the
  relationship. The observer's own institutional incentives. The ways
  the observer's framework shapes what they can see (IC-5). The power
  relationship between the observer and the observed — especially when
  the observer is from an institution embedded in the same structures
  being studied.
- **Canonical example**: Scott is an external observer studying Sedaka.
  His analytical distance enables structural insight. His institutional
  position (Yale professor) means his account is mediated by the same
  academic structures the framework's IC-5 identifies as sources of
  bias.
- **Power function**: External observers produce the "official" knowledge
  about power relationships. Their institutional position determines
  which accounts are published, cited, and incorporated into training
  data. The observer's perspective disproportionately shapes the
  historical record (Axiom 8 — what is absent).

## How to apply this instrument

### During ANALYZE Step 2 (SOURCE):

1. Identify which position the source speaks from using the five
   categories above. Most sources map clearly to one position; some
   straddle two.
2. Name the position explicitly: "This source speaks from Position [N]:
   [name]."
3. Using the "what this position makes visible / invisible" fields,
   identify what the source can and cannot see from its position.
4. Ask: "What would this look like from each of the other positions?"
   You need not answer for all four alternatives, but consider at least
   the most structurally opposed position (e.g., if the source is
   Position 1, consider Position 2 or 3).

### During ANALYZE Step 3 (LOCATE):

1. Note the source's positional bias in the LAYERS ACTIVE assessment.
   A source speaking from Position 1 may describe all layers as they
   are designed; a source from Position 2 may describe how those layers
   are experienced in practice. The same layer may appear differently
   depending on position.
2. Cross-reference with the Double Performance pattern: if the source
   describes a public transcript, ask whether both sides of the
   performance are represented or only one.

### During ANALYZE Step 6 (ABSENT):

1. List which positions are absent from the material.
2. For each absent position, note what it would likely reveal if present,
   using the "what this position makes visible" fields.
3. Apply IC-5: the LLM's training data overrepresents Positions 1 and 5
   (rulers/operators and academic observers) and underrepresents
   Positions 2 and 3 (everyday governed and revolutionary). Position 4
   (intermediary) is well-represented but often without the
   self-awareness that would make its intermediary function visible.

### Compound application:

The highest-value use of this instrument is when multiple sources on the
same topic speak from different positions. When this occurs:

1. Map each source to its position
2. Identify where their accounts agree (structural confirmation)
3. Identify where their accounts diverge (positional bias)
4. Ask what remains invisible from ALL available positions — this is the
   deepest absence

## Limitations

This instrument categorizes perspectives schematically. In practice:

- Individuals may occupy multiple positions simultaneously (a middle
  manager is both governed and governing)
- Positions are not fixed — the same person may shift positions depending
  on context
- The five categories are not exhaustive — they are derived from three
  source works and may need expansion as the framework encounters new
  perspectives
- The instrument identifies positional bias but does not resolve it —
  knowing where a source stands does not tell you what the view from
  elsewhere would reveal; it only tells you to look
