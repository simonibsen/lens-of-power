## Narrative

The Center for Renewing America publishes a policy brief arguing that "the Great Replacement" is "not a theory, but a reality" — that US immigration policy is deliberately designed to replace American citizens with non-citizens for electoral advantage. The brief is authored by an organisation founded by Russ Vought, former Director of the Office of Management and Budget in the first Trump administration and a central figure in Project 2025. The brief operates simultaneously as an act of narrative construction and as a policy proposal, performing the mechanism Axiom 6 describes: language precedes and enables institutional action.

The brief's central rhetorical move is what it calls the "celebration parallax" — the argument that progressives celebrate demographic change while condemning anyone who names it as a political strategy. The brief assembles quotes from Biden, Durbin, Clark, and Murphy referencing demographic trends, then frames these observations as evidence of a deliberate replacement programme. The analytical operation is specific: the brief collapses the distinction between observing that the electorate is becoming more diverse (a demographic fact) and claiming that this diversity is being engineered as an electoral weapon (a conspiracy claim). The collapse is performed through juxtaposition — by placing demographic observations from Democratic politicians alongside the policy brief's own framing, the brief converts description into confession. The politicians are made to testify against themselves, not because of what they said but because of the interpretive frame the brief imposes on their words.

The brief performs a second structural operation: it separates the Great Replacement from its white-nationalist origins by simultaneously acknowledging and disavowing them. The brief names Renaud Camus as the originator, acknowledges the association with mass-casualty violence (Christchurch, Buffalo), labels the white-nationalist version as racist — and then immediately reclaims the structural claim. The "legitimate" version replaces race with citizenship: the concern is not white displacement but the replacement of "individuals tethered to the American founding with individuals who are not." This substitution does not eliminate the racial dimension — it encodes it in a new vocabulary. "Tethered to the American founding" and "from faraway countries" perform the same classificatory work as racial categories while operating in a register that is formally race-neutral. This is the mechanism García Hernández documents in the Racial Architecture of Immigration Law: formal transformation of racial categories with structural continuity of racial function.

The brief's policy section connects the narrative to enforcement architecture. It advocates restricting immigration, excluding non-citizens from census apportionment, opposing amnesty, and establishing the "highest possible bar" for citizenship. These policy recommendations operationalise the narrative: if immigration is an attack on the republic, then enforcement is defence. The brief thereby converts a demographic observation into a threat assessment, and a threat assessment into a policy mandate. The causal chain — narrative → threat designation → enforcement policy — is the same sequence the framework documented in the Texas terrorism prosecution: the antifa designation preceded and enabled the prosecution, just as the replacement narrative precedes and enables the enforcement architecture.

The brief connects immigration policy to Marxism and critical theory, arguing that progressive immigration advocates seek to erode citizenship as a category in service of socialist transformation. This connection is not supported by evidence in the brief — it is asserted through juxtaposition of quotes from academic figures (some taken from contexts decades old) with current immigration policy positions. The function of the Marxism connection is to activate the Perpetual Threat pattern: immigration is not merely a policy disagreement but an existential threat to the constitutional order, which justifies permanent mobilisation and extraordinary enforcement measures.

The brief contains an internal tension it does not resolve. It acknowledges that Camus himself was a "left-wing socialist" and "gay activist," that demographic change operates through "law of large numbers" rather than coordinated conspiracy, and that the replacement happens through policy rather than organised plot. These concessions undermine the brief's own framing — if replacement is the product of policy rather than conspiracy, then it is addressable through policy debate, not through the existential-threat frame the brief constructs. The brief acknowledges the non-conspiratorial explanation and then proceeds as though it did not.

If this process succeeds — and the brief's parent organisation (CRA) has contributed personnel and policy language to the current administration — the following consequences are testable:

## Circumventions

**Transparency / Naming**: The brief has been named as replacement-theory advocacy by media and civil-rights organisations (ADL, SPLC, numerous outlets). The naming has not prevented the brief's arguments from migrating into policy discourse. The brief itself anticipates and absorbs the naming — it pre-emptively labels critics as participants in the "celebration parallax," converting the act of naming the conspiracy theory into evidence for the conspiracy theory. This is a documented failure mode: naming is captured and converted into fuel for the named mechanism (cf. Stanley's undermining propaganda; Arendt's ideology as closed logical system).

**Legal Challenge**: The census apportionment question is litigable — the Fourteenth Amendment specifies "whole number of persons" for apportionment, and excluding non-citizens would require either a constitutional amendment or a Supreme Court willing to reinterpret "persons." The framework's Whitehouse extraction documents the judicial capture pipeline that may shape appellate outcomes.

**Electoral / Democratic**: The brief's policy recommendations (restricting immigration, excluding non-citizens from apportionment) are electorally contestable. However, if the apportionment exclusion succeeds, it structurally reduces the electoral representation of the populations who would oppose it — the policy locks in its own electoral advantage.

## Null case

**Initial null case**: The brief raises a legitimate policy concern — that immigration policy should prioritise citizens' interests and that demographic change has electoral implications. These are mainstream policy positions held by many democracies. The brief's disavowal of white nationalism is genuine. The Marxism connection is standard conservative rhetoric, not evidence of conspiratorial thinking. The policy recommendations (restrict immigration, enforce law, value citizenship) are within the normal range of democratic debate.

**Assessment**: The null case is partially sustained for the policy recommendations in isolation — restricting immigration and enforcing existing law are positions within democratic debate. The null case is not sustained for the brief's analytical apparatus: the "celebration parallax" device, the Marxism connection, the existential-threat framing, and the reproduction of the replacement claim after disavowing its violent manifestations are not conventional policy argument. They are a specific technique for converting a conspiracy theory into a policy mandate by separating its racial content from its structural claim and re-encoding the classification in formally neutral language.

The brief's own concessions — that replacement operates through policy, not conspiracy; that demographic change follows "law of large numbers"; that Camus was a left-wing figure — undermine the threat frame the brief constructs around them. A conventional policy argument would build on these concessions (immigration is a policy question; demographic change is a structural trend; let us debate the policy). The brief acknowledges them and then proceeds as though the republic faces an existential threat from immigration. The gap between the concessions and the conclusions is the structural finding.

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM for the policy recommendations in isolation; LOW for the analytical apparatus (celebration parallax, Marxism connection, threat framing, racial re-encoding). The brief is not a conventional policy argument; it is a technique for normalising a conspiracy-theory frame within policy-legitimate institutions.
