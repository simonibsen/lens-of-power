## Narrative

A North Carolina congressional primary between incumbent Valerie Foushee and challenger Nida Allam has become an early test of what the article calls "datacenter politics." The race centers on a proposed 190-acre datacenter near Apex and the $1.6 million spent by Jobs and Democracy, a Super PAC whose sole disclosed donor is Anthropic, the AI company that produces Claude. The PAC spent the money in the final two weeks of the campaign, reversing a funding disadvantage. As of the article's publication, the race was within one percentage point and likely headed for a recount.

The PAC's stated mission is to support leaders who protect "kids, families, workers, and our national security from the risks of unregulated AI." Its sole funder is an AI company. The candidate it backs opposes a federal datacenter moratorium — the strongest available regulatory intervention — in favor of deferring decisions to local authorities. Allam, who supports a 10-year national moratorium alongside Senator Bernie Sanders, characterizes the proposition that an industry-funded group would support regulation of that industry as "laughable." The PAC's name, stated mission, and structural function create a configuration where the stated purpose (AI regulation) and the structural effect (preventing the strongest regulatory option) diverge.

The article documents a second structural alignment: Foushee was appointed to co-chair a new bipartisan AI commission. The same Super PAC has funded ads supporting another commission co-chair, New Jersey representative Josh Gottheimer. The body designed to develop AI regulation is thus populated by members whose campaigns are financed by an AI company, through the formal processes of campaign finance and congressional appointment.

The Apex datacenter proposal involves resource consumption at a scale that alters the local environment: up to 1 million gallons of water per day (one-fifth of the town's average daily use), 300 megawatts of electricity (three times the combined consumption of Apex and nearby New Hill), and 100 diesel backup generators. The developer, Maryland-based Natelli Investments, has not disclosed which companies will use the facility. Allam identifies the distributional structure: "it's going to be our working-class neighbors that suffer the most." Foushee states she shares constituents' concerns but argues local leaders should make the decision, framing her position as deference to democratic self-governance.

The article notes a pattern in Foushee's campaign financing. In 2022, she accepted funding from the pro-Israel lobby and a Super PAC linked to Sam Bankman-Fried, who was subsequently convicted of fraud. The funding source changes each cycle; the structural relationship — candidate funded by the industry she is positioned to regulate — persists.
