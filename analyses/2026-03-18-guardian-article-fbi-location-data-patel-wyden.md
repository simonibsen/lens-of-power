## Hegemonic context

The article treats warranted surveillance as acceptable and warrantless surveillance as a violation — it does not ask whether the surveillance infrastructure itself (warranted or not) represents a structural concentration of information power. The assumption that "fixing the loophole" through legislation would resolve the problem is shared by all actors (Wyden, Davidson, Patel's framing of legal compliance). ## Narrative

FBI Director Kash Patel confirmed under oath at the Senate intelligence committee worldwide threats hearing that the FBI purchases location data on Americans from commercial data brokers. Patel characterised the purchases as "commercially available information that's consistent with the constitution and the laws under the Electronic Communications Privacy Act," stating they have "led to some valuable intelligence." Senator Wyden, who had established in 2023 testimony from Patel's predecessor Christopher Wray that the FBI was not then purchasing location data, identified the practice as "an outrageous end run around the fourth amendment."

The mechanism through which the FBI acquires location data without a warrant is a commercial workaround. The Supreme Court ruled in *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) that government acquisition of location data from telecom companies requires a warrant. Data brokers, however, aggregate and sell the same information — derived from advertising data, app permissions, and device signals — without judicial authorisation. The FBI purchases from the broker rather than compelling production from the telecom company. The legal distinction (purchase vs. compulsion) is the gap through which the surveillance operates. The information obtained is functionally identical; the legal pathway is different.

Patel's framing — citing the Electronic Communications Privacy Act (1986) as the legal basis — illustrates a specific mechanism. ECPA was enacted before the commercial internet, before smartphones, before the data broker industry. Its stated purpose was privacy protection. Its current structural function, as invoked by Patel, is to provide the legal authority for warrantless data purchases that circumvent the warrant requirement the Fourth Amendment imposes. The law designed to protect privacy is cited as the basis for acquiring private data without judicial oversight.

The bipartisan character of the opposition is structurally significant for what it reveals about institutional failure. Wyden (Democrat) and Davidson (Republican) both describe the practice as a Fourth Amendment violation. Davidson's bill mirrors Wyden's Senate measure. This bipartisan alignment on surveillance reform has existed for over a decade — Wyden has raised these concerns since at least 2013 — without producing legislation. The structural question is why bipartisan agreement does not produce legislative outcomes. The framework identifies this as consistent with the pattern in which democratic institutions handle surface debate while substantive arrangements operate undisturbed: the surveillance infrastructure persists not because it is covertly defended but because the legislative conditions for action (committee priorities, floor time, executive cooperation, industry lobbying) never align.

Wyden's reference to artificial intelligence marks the forward-looking dimension. Location data combined with AI analysis produces qualitative rather than merely quantitative surveillance — not just tracking where individuals are but predicting movements, mapping associations, and identifying patterns. The framework's prior DHS surveillance procurement analysis documented AI-enabled surveillance tools being developed through government contracts. The combination of commercial location data acquisition + AI processing capacity + an institutional culture that views legal constraints as obstacles (documented in the CHMR analysis just completed) constitutes a surveillance infrastructure that the Fourth Amendment's framers could not have anticipated and that the Fourth Amendment's current interpretation does not constrain.

## So What

The data broker loophole is not a bug — it is a structural feature of the commercial data economy that government agencies exploit. Closing the loophole through legislation (Wyden-Davidson bill) would address the specific mechanism but not the underlying condition: that commercial data collection creates a permanent surveillance infrastructure available to any purchaser, government or otherwise.

Watch items:
- Whether the Government Surveillance Reform Act advances through committee or remains stalled (the decade-long pattern of bipartisan agreement without legislative action is itself the finding)
- Whether the FBI's data purchases are quantified — volume, scope, targeting criteria, retention periods
- Whether the AI integration Wyden flags produces documented cases of predictive location surveillance
- Whether the practice extends beyond the FBI to other agencies (DHS, as documented in the prior surveillance procurement analysis, is a likely candidate)
- Whether the Patel admission produces any operational change at the FBI or merely enters the public record without consequence

## Circumventions

### Structural

**Transparency / Naming**: Wyden's questioning produced an on-the-record, under-oath admission of a practice that had previously operated in ambiguity. Davidson's social media amplification extended the naming. Observed outcome: the practice is now documented in congressional testimony. Whether naming produces legislative action is the structural test — the decade-long precedent suggests it may not. Failure mode: the admission enters the record alongside dozens of prior surveillance disclosures that produced documentation without producing constraint.

**Legal Challenge**: The Wyden-Davidson bill (Government Surveillance Reform Act) is a direct legislative response. The *Carpenter* decision provides the constitutional framework. Observed outcome: the bill has not passed despite bipartisan support. Failure mode: legislative stalling through committee process, industry lobbying, and executive resistance.

## Null case

The non-power explanation: the FBI's data purchases are narrowly targeted at specific investigations involving national security or serious crime, represent a reasonable adaptation to the modern information environment, and have produced genuine intelligence value as Patel claims. The data broker market exists regardless of government participation, and the FBI is simply accessing commercially available information.

**Plausibility: LOW-MEDIUM.** The null case has some force for individual purchases — some may indeed support legitimate investigations. However, it does not explain why the FBI uses a commercial workaround rather than seeking warrants — if the purchases are legitimate and targeted, the warrant process would authorise them. The workaround's structural purpose is to avoid the constraint. The null case also does not explain the decade of bipartisan agreement without legislative action, or the shift from Wray's 2023 testimony (not purchasing) to Patel's 2026 admission (purchasing). The configuration — commercial workaround + AI capability + institutional culture documented in CHMR analysis that views legal constraints as obstacles — is not explained by individual case-level legitimacy.
