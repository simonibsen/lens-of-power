## Narrative

This brief argues that the Paramount–Warner Bros. Discovery merger is not
primarily a media consolidation story. It is the transaction that completes
Oracle Corporation's position as the dominant infrastructure layer beneath
American healthcare, defense, social media, and media — simultaneously,
under the control of a single family.

The brief assembles a configuration that no single regulatory body is
currently evaluating. In a two-week window in February 2026: CMS selected
Oracle Cloud to host systems touching 150 million Americans' healthcare
data (Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, ACA marketplace); Oracle secured an $88
million Air Force Cloud One task order with Secret and TS/SCI
authorizations; and WBD's board declared Paramount Skydance's $111 billion
bid a "Company Superior Proposal." Larry Ellison, Oracle's co-founder and
chairman, is personally guaranteeing $45.7 billion of the media
transaction, secured against approximately 1.16 billion Oracle shares.

Oracle's pre-existing footprint spans: electronic health records for 9
million veterans (Cerner, $28.3B acquisition); TikTok's US operations (170
million monthly users, 15% equity stake, algorithm audits, data isolation,
independent oversight); Sinclair Broadcast Group's enterprise systems (40%
of US households); Paramount's cloud migration ($100M/year); the Stargate
AI joint venture ($500B, with SoftBank, OpenAI, and Gulf sovereign wealth
funds); and TS/SCI cloud regions serving 18 Intelligence Community
agencies. Larry Ellison has stated publicly that Oracle holds data on "5
billion consumers out of 8 billion people on the planet."

The brief's core argument: Oracle is being treated as a neutral third-party
vendor in the merger review. It is not. It is a related party whose
aggregate cross-domain position creates conflicts of interest that fall
through the gaps between domain-specific regulatory processes. Three
specific conflicts are documented: the data position (cross-domain access),
the revolving door (Seema Verma, CMS Administrator 2017–2021, now Oracle
Health EVP), and the financial loop (federal contracts → Oracle revenue →
stock price → Ellison collateral → media acquisition → runs on Oracle).
Three asks: formal OCI review, data firewalls as merger condition, treat
Oracle as related party.

The brief is explicit that it is advocacy: "Nothing in this brief alleges
illegality. It asks whether the government's own rules are being applied."
It includes 50 source citations with URLs and specific questions for David
Ellison under oath.

The framework has previously analyzed the media consolidation dimension of
the Paramount–WBD transaction (see `2026-02-26-variety-article-paramount-
warner-bros-deal.md`). That analysis focused on editorial capture, the
Capture Pipeline through media ownership, and Democracy-Oligarchy
Coexistence. This brief adds a new dimension: the infrastructure substrate
beneath the media surface — Oracle as the common platform connecting
healthcare, defense, social media, media, and AI under a single family's
control.

## Step 5: INVERT

INVERSE CLAIM: Oracle is a large enterprise technology company that won
federal contracts through competitive procurement, acquired Cerner through
normal M&A, and provides cloud services to Paramount as a vendor. The
Ellison family's involvement in both Oracle and the media transaction is
known and disclosed in SEC filings. Regulatory processes exist and function.
The brief is a policy advocate making the most alarming possible
interpretation of standard business arrangements.

STRONGEST DEFENSE:
- Oracle is one of three major cloud providers serving the federal
  government (alongside AWS and Microsoft Azure). Its FedRAMP and DISA
  authorizations are shared by competitors. The government has multiple
  cloud options.
- FedRAMP High, IL5/6, and TS/SCI environments are technically isolated.
  Physical and logical separation between government and commercial
  workloads is a genuine security requirement, not theater.
- The Cerner acquisition was a healthcare IT acquisition evaluated by
  regulators. The VA EHR problems may reflect implementation challenges,
  not malicious intent.
- The revolving door (Verma) is common across all major government
  contractors. Ethics rules (cooling-off periods, recusal requirements)
  exist to manage this.
- Larry Ellison's financial guarantee is disclosed in SEC filings. The
  brief's own sources are these disclosures.
- The Oracle Data Cloud advertising business was shut down after the $115M
  settlement. The bridge from "infrastructure exists" to "will be used for
  cross-domain profiling" is inference.
- Stargate is a multi-party joint venture, not Oracle's sole project.
  Gulf SWF participation in US investments is routine.

NULL CASE: Oracle is a commercially successful enterprise technology
company whose cross-domain presence reflects market success, not strategic
design for infrastructure monopoly. The Ellison family connection between
Oracle and the Paramount-WBD deal is a family-wealth phenomenon. Regulatory
processes exist and function. The brief is advocacy, and it assembles
standard business arrangements into an alarming configuration through
selective presentation.

NULL CASE PLAUSIBILITY: MEDIUM at the component level. LOW-MEDIUM at the
configurational level.

Each individual element has a standard business explanation: companies win
government contracts; former officials join companies; cloud providers serve
multiple sectors; wealthy families invest across domains; media companies
use cloud infrastructure. None of this is unusual in isolation.

What the null case cannot explain:

1. **The governance asymmetry**: The TikTok USDS arrangement includes
   algorithm audits, data isolation, independent oversight, and binding
   National Security Terms — protections the US government required when a
   foreign-connected entity hosted American user data. Oracle hosts
   healthcare data for 150M+ Americans, defense and IC workloads, social
   media data for 170M users, AND media infrastructure — with none of
   these protections for the media/healthcare cross-domain position. The
   governance framework exists; it is selectively applied.

2. **The Ellison Guarantee carve-out**: The parties explicitly exempted
   Oracle Corporation and the Ellison Institute from regulatory-cooperation
   obligations — an acknowledgment that Oracle is differently situated from
   other Ellison-controlled entities. This is not a neutral vendor
   arrangement; the parties themselves recognized the structural conflict.

3. **The circular financial dependency**: Federal contracts → Oracle
   revenue → stock price → Ellison collateral → media acquisition → runs
   on Oracle. Each link is standard; the loop is self-reinforcing and
   creates path dependency that makes the configuration increasingly
   difficult to unwind. The null case treats each link as coincidental;
   the loop structure is the analytical finding.

4. **The regulatory absence**: No regulatory body has jurisdiction over
   the question "should one entity simultaneously control the
   infrastructure beneath healthcare, defense, media, social media, and
   AI?" This is not because one reviewed it and found no issue — no
   process exists to review it. The absence of review is not explained by
   the null case; it is the operating environment the configuration
   depends on.

5. **Ellison's own statements**: "Citizens will be on their best behavior
   because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that's
   going on." "We need to unify all the national data, put it into a
   database where it's easily consumable by the AI model." "Oracle manages
   most of the world's high-value private data." These describe the
   cross-domain convergence as an explicit objective, not as an accidental
   outcome. The null case requires treating these as aspirational marketing
   rather than as statements of operational intent from the chairman of the
   company that now occupies the described position.

The strongest null case element: FedRAMP and DISA technical isolation are
real. Physical and logical separation between government and commercial
workloads is enforced through third-party audits, continuous monitoring, and
specific security standards. The brief's argument depends on the claim that
organizational separation is insufficient despite technical separation —
that the same executive leadership, AI research teams, and data science
capabilities operating across all environments constitute a meaningful
cross-domain position regardless of FedRAMP boundaries. This is an
analytical claim, not a proven fact.

FALSIFIABLE BY:
- Evidence that Oracle's government and commercial cloud operations are
  functionally independent with separate leadership, separate AI teams,
  and no cross-pollination of insights
- Evidence that CMS conducted a formal OCI review and found no conflicts
- Evidence that Verma is subject to meaningful recusals on CMS-related work
- Evidence that a cross-domain review of Oracle's aggregate position has
  been conducted by any regulatory body
- Evidence that the Oracle Data Cloud infrastructure was fully
  decommissioned, not merely the advertising product built on it
- Evidence that the TikTok USDS governance protections were considered for
  the Paramount-WBD arrangement and determined unnecessary

LLM BIAS CHECK: Am I hedging because evidence warrants caution or because
alignment training resists naming infrastructure monopoly? The evidence for

## Step 7: SO-WHAT

IMPLICATIONS:
- If this analysis is correct, Oracle has assembled a cross-domain
  infrastructure position that no single regulatory body can see, evaluate,
  or contest. The position is invisible not because it is concealed but
  because the regulatory architecture is domain-specific. This is the
  Division of Learning applied to governance itself: each regulator sees
  its own domain; Oracle sees all of them simultaneously.
- The circular financial dependency (federal contracts → stock → collateral
  → media acquisition → runs on Oracle) is a self-reinforcing loop that
  makes the configuration increasingly difficult to unwind. Each cycle
  compounds the path dependency. This is the Peacetime Ratchet operating
  through infrastructure accumulation rather than through wealth
  concentration alone.
- The governance asymmetry — TikTok USDS required algorithm audits, data
  isolation, independent oversight, and National Security Terms; the
  Paramount-WBD arrangement has none — reveals that the US government has
  the tools but applies them selectively. The criterion appears to be
  foreign ownership, not cross-domain data position. Oracle's domestic
  ownership makes it invisible to the governance framework that would

## Briefing: The Oracle Layer — Infrastructure Position Across American
Government and Media

DATE: 2026-03-05
SOURCE TYPE: Policy brief prepared for Senate Subcommittee on Antitrust
SOURCE POSITION: Position 4 (Intermediary — policy advocate)

### Key finding

Oracle Corporation has assembled a cross-domain infrastructure position
beneath American healthcare (CMS, 150M+ Americans; VA, 9M veterans),
defense and intelligence (Air Force, 18 IC agencies, TS/SCI), social media
(TikTok, 170M users), broadcast media (Sinclair, 40% of US households),
streaming and news (Paramount+, CBS News, and post-merger HBO, Max, CNN),
and AI (Stargate, $500B JV) — simultaneously, under the control of a
single family. No regulatory body is evaluating the aggregate position.
Each regulator sees Oracle as a vendor in its domain; none sees the
cross-domain configuration. The Paramount-WBD merger is not the beginning
of this configuration — it is the transaction that completes it by adding
the largest media company in American history to an infrastructure position
already spanning healthcare, defense, social media, and AI.

### Who benefits / who bears cost

| Actor | Gains | At whose expense |
|-------|-------|------------------|
| Oracle / Ellison family | Cross-domain infrastructure position spanning healthcare, defense, social media, media, and AI; circular financial dependency sustaining stock valuation; vendor lock-in through GSA OneGov pricing | 150M+ CMS beneficiaries, 9M veterans, 170M TikTok users, media audiences — populations whose data environments are consolidated under a single infrastructure provider with no cross-domain governance |
| Federal agencies (CMS, VA, DoD) | Cloud modernization, cost savings, technical capability | Vendor dependency, cross-domain risk unassessed, revolving-door conflicts |
| Seema Verma | EVP position at Oracle Health, leveraging prior CMS institutional knowledge | CMS procurement integrity, public confidence in contract awards |
| Gulf sovereign wealth funds | Investment returns from Stargate JV and media transaction; potential CFIUS fast-track status | National security review rigor; democratic oversight of foreign investment in critical infrastructure |

### What is absent

- Cross-domain regulatory review of Oracle's aggregate position
- Governance frameworks equivalent to TikTok USDS for the media/healthcare
  cross-domain position (algorithm audits, data isolation, independent
  oversight)
- CMS press release or SAM.gov award notice for the Oracle cloud contract
- Formal OCI review for the Verma appointment or subsequent contract awards
- Voices of VA patients, CMS beneficiaries, and TikTok users whose data
  environments are being consolidated
- The 58% of VA staff who believe Oracle's EHR system increases patient
  safety risks
- Assessment of Oracle AI Database 26ai cross-domain implications

### Findings

1. **Cross-domain infrastructure position without unified oversight**
   LAYER: All non-Physical layers (Economic, Institutional, Surveillance &
   Information, Legal & Regulatory, Thought & Narrative)
   STATUS: observed (infrastructure position documented through contracts,
   acquisitions, and filings); inferred (that the absence of unified
   oversight constitutes a structural governance gap rather than adequate
   domain-specific regulation)
   Oracle simultaneously controls infrastructure beneath healthcare (CMS
   150M+, VA 9M), defense/intelligence (Air Force, 18 IC agencies,
   TS/SCI), social media (TikTok 170M), broadcast (Sinclair 40% of
   households), streaming/news (Paramount/CBS, post-merger HBO/Max/CNN),
   and AI (Stargate $500B). No single regulatory body evaluates this
   aggregate position. Each sees Oracle as its domain's vendor.
   Corroborates **Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence** (compartmentalized
   regulatory review variant) and **Division of Learning** (cross-domain
   infrastructure variant).

2. **The circular financial dependency**
   LAYER: Economic (primary), Institutional, Surveillance & Information
   STATUS: observed (each link documented in SEC filings and public
   records); inferred (that the loop structure creates self-reinforcing
   path dependency)
   Federal contracts flow to Oracle as revenue. Oracle revenue supports
   stock price. The stock price provides Ellison's collateral ($45.7B
   guarantee against ~1.16B shares). The collateral enables the media
   acquisition. The merged media company runs on Oracle infrastructure.
   The loop compounds: each cycle reinforces the configuration and makes
   unwinding increasingly difficult. Oracle's 55% stock decline and
   securities fraud lawsuits introduce instability into the loop —
   weakening collateral threatens the media acquisition that depends on it.
   Corroborates **The Peacetime Ratchet** (infrastructure accumulation
   variant).

3. **Selective governance application**
   LAYER: Legal & Regulatory (primary), Institutional, Surveillance &
   Information
   STATUS: observed (TikTok USDS protections documented; absence of
   equivalent protections for Paramount-WBD arrangement documented)
   The TikTok USDS arrangement includes algorithm audits, source code
   review, data isolation gateways, independent oversight board, and
   binding National Security Terms — protections the US government required
   when a foreign-connected entity hosted American user data. Oracle hosts
   healthcare data for 150M+ Americans, defense/IC workloads, social media
   data for 170M users, AND media infrastructure — with none of these
   protections for the healthcare-media cross-domain position. The
   governance tools exist. They are applied based on perceived threat
   category (foreign ownership) rather than actual data position.
   Corroborates **Appearance as Structural Terrain** — the security
   authorization framework legitimates the position it should evaluate.

4. **The revolving door as pipeline mechanism**
   LAYER: Institutional (primary), Legal & Regulatory, Economic
   STATUS: observed (Verma's career path documented through Politico, KFF,
   CREW, FierceHealthcare, Oracle announcement)
   Seema Verma served as CMS Administrator (2017–2021). During her tenure,
   the HHS Inspector General found "significant deficiencies" in her
   management of CMS funds, including steering of approximately $5M to
   GOP-connected consultants. In 2023/2024 she joined Oracle as EVP of
   Oracle Health. In this role she has been instrumental in securing
   expansion of Oracle's VA and DoD EHR contracts and the February 2026
   CMS contract — the same agency she ran. No public record indicates that
   CMS or GSA conducted a formal OCI review of Oracle's engagement of
   Verma. The 2022 Preventing Organizational Conflicts of Interest in
   Federal Acquisition Act was designed specifically for this situation.
   Extends **The Capture Pipeline** — revolving door as sixth documented
   domain.

5. **Oracle as related party treated as neutral vendor**
   LAYER: Economic (primary), Institutional, Legal & Regulatory
   STATUS: observed (Ellison Guarantee carve-out documented in SEC 8-K);
   inferred (that the vendor characterization is structurally misleading)
   Larry Ellison is Oracle's co-founder and chairman. His son David Ellison
   leads the acquiring entity. Larry Ellison is personally guaranteeing
   $45.7B of the acquisition. The Ellison Guarantee explicitly carves out
   Oracle Corporation and the Ellison Institute from regulatory-cooperation
   and antitrust non-interference obligations — an acknowledgment by the
   parties themselves that Oracle is differently situated. No disclosure of
   the Oracle-Paramount relationship as a related-party arrangement appears
   in the current merger review filings. Oracle is being treated as a
   neutral third-party vendor in a transaction guaranteed by its chairman.
   Corroborates **Appearance as Structural Terrain** — the gap between
   "neutral vendor" and actual position is the operating environment.

6. **Agentic AI in classified environments**
   LAYER: Surveillance & Information (primary), Institutional, Legal &
   Regulatory
   STATUS: observed (Oracle AI Database 26ai described in Oracle
   materials); speculative (cross-domain risk implications)
   Under the February 2026 Air Force Cloud One task order, Oracle is
   deploying its AI Database 26ai across IL5, IL6, and TS/SCI workloads.
   Oracle describes these as enabling "dynamic agentic AI workflows" that
   "combine private database data with public information" and "securely
   combine internal and public data." The capability to have in-database AI
   agents performing autonomous actions in classified defense and
   intelligence environments — by the same company that hosts healthcare
   records, social media data, and media infrastructure — has not been
   publicly evaluated for cross-domain risk. This is **The Unprecedented as
   Sanctuary** — agentic AI in classified environments operates in a
   conceptual and regulatory void.

7. **IC-5: The Anthropic precedent and instrument conflict**
   LAYER: Surveillance & Information, Institutional
   STATUS: unverifiable (events described are beyond this instrument's
   training data cutoff); the IC-5 flag is the finding itself
   The brief claims (citing NPR, NYT, Opinio Juris) that President Trump
   issued an executive order targeting Anthropic on February 27, 2026 after
   the company refused to remove safety guardrails for mass surveillance
   and autonomous weapons. The brief uses this as contrast: "Oracle has no
   equivalent guardrails, ethical AI policy, or public refusal on record."
   This analytical instrument (Claude) is produced by Anthropic. I cannot
   verify the factual claims, cannot assess the contrast neutrally, and
   cannot rule out that my evaluation of the brief's use of Anthropic as a
   positive contrast is biased. The contrast claim about Oracle's absence
   of stated AI guardrails may be independently valid but must be evaluated
   by the user rather than by this instrument.

### Null case

The non-power explanation: Oracle is a commercially successful enterprise
technology company whose cross-domain presence reflects competitive wins in
separate markets. The Ellison family connection between Oracle and the
Paramount-WBD deal is a disclosed family-wealth arrangement. FedRAMP and
DISA technical isolation protect government workloads. Regulatory processes
function adequately within their domains. The revolving door is managed by
existing ethics rules. The brief is advocacy, making the most alarming
interpretation of standard business arrangements.

This explains the components. It does not explain: the governance
asymmetry (TikTok USDS protections exist, Paramount-WBD has none), the
Ellison Guarantee carve-out (parties acknowledged Oracle is differently
situated), the regulatory absence (no body can see the aggregate), the
circular financial dependency (a self-reinforcing loop whose individual
components are standard but whose configuration creates structural path
dependency), or Ellison's own statements describing the cross-domain
convergence as an explicit objective.

Null case plausibility is MEDIUM at the component level and LOW-MEDIUM at
the configurational level.

## Gap recommendation

The framework has two complementary analyses of the Ellison/Oracle/
Paramount-WBD configuration — the media dimension (Variety, 2026-02-26)
and the infrastructure dimension (this brief). The brief's core analytical
contribution — infrastructure ubiquity as cross-domain power — would be
strengthened by studying infrastructure monopoly breakup precedents.
Recommend extracting from the AT&T divestiture history (the Bell System
breakup as response to cross-domain infrastructure monopoly) or from Tim
Wu's *The Curse of Bigness* (2018), which connects antitrust history to
contemporary platform monopoly. Either would test whether the framework's
existing patterns adequately describe infrastructure-layer power or whether
a new pattern is warranted.
