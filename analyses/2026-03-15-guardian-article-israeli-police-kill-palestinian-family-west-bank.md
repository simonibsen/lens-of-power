## Narrative

Israeli border police, operating as an undercover unit in a car with Palestinian licence plates, shot and killed four members of the Bani Odeh family near Tamoun in the occupied West Bank late Saturday 15 March 2026. Mohammed (5), Othman (7, blind and with special needs), their mother Waad (35), and father Ali (37) were all shot in the head and face as they returned from a Ramadan shopping trip to Nablus to buy Eid clothes. Two brothers survived: Khaled (11) reported that after the shooting, Israeli forces dragged him from the wreckage, beat him, and said "we killed dogs." Israeli forces prevented ambulances from reaching the scene and towed the family car away.

The Israeli police statement said forces opened fire when they "perceived an immediate threat" after the vehicle "accelerated." Asked what threat was posed by four young children and their unarmed parents, or whether the shooting violated rules of engagement, police and military declined to comment. The military confirmed border police killed the family and referred all questions to the police.

The killing occurred hours after Israeli settlers shot and killed Amir Moatasem Odeh (28) in Qusra and stabbed his father. Over the preceding two weeks, Israeli settlers had shot six civilians dead during invasions of Palestinian olive groves, villages, and grazing land. The Guardian notes that since the last homicide indictment of Israeli forces for a West Bank killing in 2019, Israeli forces have killed more than 1,400 people, including 320+ children and 30+ women (UN figures).

## Null case

**Initial null case** (stated before analysis): Israeli forces were conducting a legitimate counter-terrorism operation ("arrest wanted suspects believed to be involved in terrorist activity"). The vehicle accelerated toward the forces, creating a perceived threat. Forces responded within their rules of engagement. The incident is under review. Individual tragic outcomes do not demonstrate systemic intent.

**Post-analysis assessment**: The null case at the individual-incident level — that forces perceived a threat and responded — is the official account. It cannot be verified or falsified from the available evidence because Israeli forces towed the family car (potential forensic evidence) and declined to specify what threat was posed by four young children and unarmed parents. The null case is structurally weakened by the longitudinal context: 1,400+ killings since the last homicide indictment (2019), <1% indictment rate across two decades, identical "perceived threat" / "vehicle accelerated" framing across multiple incidents, and a systematic pattern of preventing ambulance access and removing evidence (towing the car). At the configurational level, the null case — that each of 1,400+ killings is an individual incident requiring individual assessment — becomes implausible when the institutional architecture that produces impunity is itself the finding.

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM for the individual incident in isolation (threat perception is not verifiable from available evidence); LOW at the systemic level (the impunity structure is documented across two decades of longitudinal data and is not explained by individual incident assessments).
