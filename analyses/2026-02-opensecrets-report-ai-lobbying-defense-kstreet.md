## Narrative

OpenSecrets documents the transformation of AI from a niche lobbying topic into a central pillar of corporate influence in Washington. In 2025, 774 organizations lobbied on AI issues with over 3,500 reports — increases of 423% and 505% from 2020. The surge occurred across the economy: AI-active firms in health and finance/insurance/real estate sectors reported $503 million in combined lobbying expenditures in 2025 alone.

The defence sector presents a specific pattern. Legacy contractors — Lockheed Martin ($15.7 million annual lobbying), Huntington Ingalls, Honeywell, Boeing — are not lobbying on AI as a standalone issue. They are bundling AI into existing weapons system appropriations: Lockheed groups AI with hypersonic missiles, munitions, and the F-35 program. Huntington Ingalls ties AI to unmanned surface vehicles and the Columbia Class submarine. Boeing bundles AI policy with unmanned aerial vehicles. The AI label is being attached to the existing defence procurement pipeline, not creating a separate one.

Simultaneously, AI-native defence firms — Scale AI ($1.2 million), Shield AI ($1.4 million), Applied Intuition ($550,000 to $910,000 in one year), Torch.ai, Accrete AI, Rebellion Defense — are using lobbying to establish themselves within the defence ecosystem, targeting Armed Services and Appropriations committees with proposals for autonomous systems, battlefield mapping, and AI-enabled surveillance.

The civil society counterweight is present but dramatically outscaled. The Leadership Conference on Civil & Human Rights reported $1.8 million in expenditures. The Disability Rights Education & Defense Fund reported $50,000. These organisations lobby on biometric surveillance, algorithmic discrimination, deepfakes, and election integrity. The article notes the "contrast in scale is stark" but does not editorially characterise this asymmetry.

A single sentence may be the article's most significant observation: the AI lobbying surge was "driven in part by companies branding existing analytics, automation and targeting systems as artificial intelligence." If the growth partly reflects vocabulary capture rather than technological transformation, the lobbying data measures linguistic strategy as much as policy activity.

Both the democratic form (disclosure) and the oligarchic substance (asymmetry) are visible in the same dataset. ## Implications

The OpenSecrets data is most valuable when combined with the existing corpus. Alone, it documents conventional lobbying. Combined with the Data & Society policy analysis and the NC-04 campaign finance data, it provides the third channel of a documented multi-channel influence architecture. The Powell Memo prescribed this architecture in 1971; the AI-defence-industrial complex is executing it in 2025-2026.

The vocabulary capture finding warrants monitoring. If the 423% increase in AI lobbying substantially reflects relabelling rather than genuine technological integration, then the lobbying data itself is partly an artefact of narrative strategy — and AI policy is being shaped by firms whose primary AI innovation is in their lobbying filings rather than their products.

## Null case

**Initial null case**: Industry lobbying on a new technology is entirely conventional. Every major technology has generated a lobbying surge. The defence industry has always lobbied on weapons systems. The asymmetry between industry and civil society is structural to every policy domain. The lobbying disclosure system is working as designed.

**Assessment**: The initial null case is **strong at the component level** — each individual data point is consistent with conventional lobbying behaviour. The null case is **weaker at the configurational level** when the OpenSecrets data is combined with the Data & Society policy analysis and the NC-04 campaign finance data. The configuration — lobbying ($503M in health/FIRE AI alone) + campaign spending (NC-04 PACs) + state investment ($8.9B Intel, no governance) + regulatory bypass (NEPA) + export dependency (full stack) — describes a multi-channel influence architecture operating simultaneously. No single data point proves capture; the configuration is not explained by "normal lobbying."

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM-HIGH at component level (individual lobbying is conventional); MEDIUM at configuration level (multi-channel architecture is not fully explained by the null case).

**IC-5 flag**: Training data likely overrepresents the institutional perspective that lobbying is a legitimate democratic activity ("petitioning the government"). The perspectives of populations affected by AI-enabled weapons and surveillance — who are not in the lobbying data — are almost certainly underrepresented. The article's data-neutral framing may normalise the asymmetry it documents. The article does not ask whether a system where the Disability Rights Education & Defense Fund spends $50,000 and Lockheed spends $15.7 million can produce equitable AI governance.
