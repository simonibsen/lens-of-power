## Narrative

The article examines the domestic Israeli consensus supporting the second war on Iran in under a year. Less than twelve months after Prime Minister Netanyahu declared "a historic victory, which will stand for generations" following a 12-day war on Iran in June, the decision to attack again was met with broad and enthusiastic support from politicians and public. The article identifies a structural absence: few prominent Israelis ask why one "historic victory" requires another war, or whether regime change from the air is realistic.

The article documents what appears to be a military-economic feedback loop sustaining the consensus. Former ambassador Alon Liel describes a population that equates military power with national success: arms sales, economic growth, the Tel Aviv skyline, and survival through two and a half years of war are cited by Israelis as evidence that the strategy works. Netanyahu has presided over both the economic expansion and the military technology boom for much of the last three decades. The war economy does not impose costs on the domestic population — it generates prosperity. This distinguishes the mechanism from standard perpetual-threat analysis, where war imposes costs that the population accepts because the threat appears existential. Here, the population supports war in part because the war is economically positive.

The article identifies Netanyahu's political interests as structurally fused with the military campaign. Columnist Nadav Eyal observes that "Netanyahu probably does not draw any distinction between the strategic aspect and the political aspect, and that is putting things gently." The war enables the prime minister to campaign as a hardline security candidate before elections due by October. It displaces domestic debate about responsibility for the October 7, 2023 attacks. Trump's request for a pre-emptive pardon in Netanyahu's corruption trial may gain force if the war is perceived as a victory. The Legal & Regulatory, Institutional, and Physical & Coercive layers are fused: criminal liability is being resolved through military success.

The article names historical parallels that complicate the "historic victory" narrative: peace treaties with Jordan and Egypt brought lasting stability but are not celebrated by Israel's current leaders; Israel's spy agencies have a "decades-long track record" of targeted killings that "did not destroy the groups these men headed"; Libya, Iraq, and Islamic State illustrate what follows the death or departure of dictators. These parallels are present in the article but absent from the public consensus the article describes.

Analyst Mairav Zonszein identifies three potential circuit-breakers: economic cost to Israelis, mass Israeli casualties, or international intervention. All three are defined by costs to the warmaking population. The costs borne by attacked populations — Iranians, Palestinians, the unnamed Filipina care worker killed by shrapnel in the same strike Tom Yaakov mythologized — do not appear as circuit-breakers. They are the background condition, not the variable.

## Findings

1. **Military-economic feedback loop sustains permanent war consensus**
   LAYER: Economic, Physical & Coercive, Thought & Narrative
   STATUS: inferred
   Israel's hi-tech military sector growth and arms exports create a positive-sum economic relationship with permanent war. Liel's description of Israelis citing "the sales of arms all over the world" and the resilience of the economy through years of war indicates that the domestic population experiences war as economically beneficial, removing the standard cost-based circuit-breaker.

2. **"Historic victory" as non-terminal — the serial war pattern**
   LAYER: Physical & Coercive, Thought & Narrative
   STATUS: observed
   Netanyahu declared "a historic victory, which will stand for generations" in June. Less than a year later, another war is launched. The article explicitly notes that few ask why. This instantiates the Serial Regime Change pattern: each operation is presented as decisive while the structure requires the next operation.

3. **Peace-treaty amnesia as structural**
   LAYER: Thought & Narrative, Institutional
   STATUS: observed
   Zonszein notes that peace treaties with Jordan and Egypt — which "brought lasting stability with countries once considered bitter enemies" — are "rarely celebrated by Israel's current political leaders." The article observes that these treaties' importance was demonstrated when the two countries offered the only route home for stranded Israelis. Non-military security achievement is present in material reality but absent from the narrative.

4. **Strategic-political fusion in Netanyahu's war calculus**
   LAYER: Institutional, Legal & Regulatory, Physical & Coercive
   STATUS: inferred (Eyal's analysis)
   Eyal's observation that Netanyahu does not distinguish strategic from political aspects indicates that the war simultaneously serves national security objectives, electoral positioning, and legal survival (corruption trial pardon). Three distinct layers are fused in a single operation.

5. **Corruption trial resolved through military success**
   LAYER: Legal & Regulatory, Physical & Coercive, Institutional
   STATUS: speculative
   Trump's request for a pre-emptive pardon for Netanyahu "may gather force if the war plays out in a way that the US and Israel can claim as victory." Criminal liability is being positioned for resolution not through legal process but through military achievement. This is a fusion of the Legal & Regulatory layer with the Physical & Coercive layer.

6. **War costs displaced onto invisible populations**
   LAYER: Physical & Coercive, Thought & Narrative
   STATUS: observed (displacement) / inferred (structural function)
   A Filipina care worker was killed by shrapnel while guiding her employer to a shelter. She is identified by nationality, not name. In the same passage, Tom Yaakov mythologizes the damage to his building as "an Israeli story I can tell my children." The war's costs fall on populations who do not appear in the narrative that sustains the war: foreign workers, Iranian civilians, Palestinians. Zonszein's circuit-breakers are defined exclusively by costs to Israelis.

7. **Anticipatory compliance across the political spectrum**
   LAYER: Institutional, Thought & Narrative
   STATUS: observed
   The war received "broad and enthusiastic support from Israeli politicians, including the prime minister's bitter rivals." Fierce domestic debate about October 7 responsibility "was instantly set aside." Political opposition complied with the war consensus before being asked. This is consistent with the Anticipatory Obedience pattern operating at the institutional level.

## Implications

The military-economic feedback loop documented here suggests that the standard assumption — that war imposes costs which eventually trigger democratic course-correction — does not apply when the war economy is positive-sum for the domestic population. The circuit-breaker is external, not internal. This has implications for the Perpetual Threat pattern: where war generates prosperity, the threat frame is reinforced by economic success, not just fear.

The corruption-trial-to-pardon pipeline represents a novel fusion of the Legal & Regulatory and Physical & Coercive layers. Criminal accountability being resolved through military success converts the judicial system from a check on executive power to a dependent variable of military outcomes.

The peace-treaty amnesia is analytically significant because it demonstrates that non-military security achievements can be structurally excluded from the national narrative even when materially present. The treaties' role in evacuating stranded Israelis during the war they enabled is precisely the kind of evidence that should produce strategic questioning but does not.

## Null case

The non-power explanation: public support for the war on Iran is a genuine democratic expression by citizens who face real threats from a regime that sponsored Hamas, Hezbollah, and called for Israel's destruction. Israeli society is traumatized by October 7 and rationally prioritizes military security. The military-economic relationship is a natural consequence of a small country surviving in a hostile environment.

This is partially plausible at the individual level — MEDIUM. The threat from Iran-backed groups is real. Post-October 7 trauma is genuine. Individual support for the war reflects actual security concerns.

At the structural level — LOW. The null case does not explain: (1) why the legacy of one "historic victory" is another war; (2) why peace treaties that produced lasting stability are not celebrated; (3) why decades of targeted killings that did not destroy groups are repeated as though each is decisive; (4) why no mainstream voice asks whether military power is the best path to lasting security; (5) why the corruption trial and electoral calendar align so precisely with military operations. The emotional response is genuine; the structural absence of strategic questioning is better explained by the military-economic feedback loop and the internalized perpetual-threat frame than by rational security calculation alone.

Overall: MEDIUM-LOW. The power explanation holds at the structural level. The null case holds at the individual-emotional level.
