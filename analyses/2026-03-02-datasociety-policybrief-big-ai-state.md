## Narrative

The policy brief documents three mechanisms by which the Trump administration is organizing the US AI industry: derisking data center construction through environmental review bypasses, federal land leasing, and financial assistance; underwriting the export of the "American AI technology stack" through diplomatic and financial tools; and acquiring equity stakes in critical supply chain firms including Intel ($8.9 billion, 9.9% stake) and MP Materials ($400 million, 15% stake, DoD as largest shareholder).

The brief identifies a structural contradiction: the administration's AI policy is presented through a "deregulation" narrative — dismantling environmental protections, blocking state and local AI regulation, rolling back Biden-era guidance. At the same time, the administration is executing what the author calls "massive government projects" using executive orders, agency coordination, public financing, and direct equity investment. The contradiction is not incidental. The "deregulation" framing serves an identifiable function: it prevents the state intervention from being evaluated as state intervention.

The financial architecture documented in the brief distributes risk and reward asymmetrically. Environmental review bypasses (NEPA categorical exclusions) transfer environmental risk to affected communities. Federal land leasing transfers public assets to private use. Government loans, grants, tariff exemptions ($19 billion in 2025 alone), and 100% bonus depreciation socialize capital costs. Equity stakes provide financial backstops to firms deemed strategically important. In each case, the public bears the risk. The brief notes that the government explicitly declined governance mechanisms: no board seats at Intel despite a $8.9 billion investment, and the AI Action Plan rejects what it calls "extraneous policy requirements" — a phrase that encompasses the CHIPS Act's worker protections including childcare mandates.

The export dimension introduces geopolitical dependency creation. The "American AI technology stack" export program requires foreign governments to adopt the "full stack" of US AI infrastructure — hardware, data systems, foundation models, and applications — as a condition of access. The program is coordinated by the Economic Diplomacy Action Group and backed by Ex-Im Bank loans, DFC equity investments, and political risk insurance. The National Security Strategy frames the choice for foreign countries as "whether they want to live in an American-led world of sovereign countries and free economies or in a parallel one." The brief notes the concept of "weaponized interdependence" (Farrell & Newman 2019): US domination of large-scale technology networks permits it to "gather or choke off information, exploit vulnerabilities, and compel policy change."

The underlying business model faces structural sustainability questions. J.P. Morgan estimates that the projected $5 trillion global AI investment requires $650 billion per year in AI product revenue, indefinitely, for a 10% return. Few people are paying for AI; eight in ten companies report no change to their bottom line from adoption. A finance analyst cited in the brief describes the capital structure as "a snake eating its own tail." The state intervention documented in the brief is, in part, an effort to sustain an investment cycle whose commercial fundamentals have not yet been established.

## Step 5: INVERT

INVERSE CLAIM: The Trump administration's AI industrial policy is a legitimate exercise of national security strategy — comparable to historical US industrial policy (space race, DARPA, interstate highways) — where government investment in strategic technologies produces broad public benefit despite initial concentration effects.

STRONGEST DEFENSE:
- China is genuinely competing for AI dominance, and US national security requires maintaining technological advantage. The timeline is real — a presidential term is a constraint.
- Government investment in strategic industries has historical precedent and has produced broad public benefits (DARPA → internet, NASA → satellite, interstate highways → logistics infrastructure).
- Equity stakes are more taxpayer-protective than grants — if the companies succeed, the public participates in the upside. This is arguably more responsible than the original CHIPS Act grant structure.
- The speed required for competitive advantage may genuinely require streamlined environmental review. NEPA processes routinely take 4-7 years for major infrastructure.
- The "full stack" export requirement prevents foreign adversaries from accessing US technology selectively for military applications.
- Business model sustainability concerns are premature — early-stage general-purpose technologies routinely face similar skepticism. Electricity, automobiles, and the internet all faced "where's the revenue?" phases.

NULL CASE: A government genuinely concerned about national security invests in strategic industries using available tools. The absence of board seats reflects practical governance considerations (government bureaucrats are poor corporate directors). NEPA exclusions reflect genuine urgency. Export requirements reflect legitimate security concerns. The "deregulation" framing is standard political messaging, not concealment.

NULL CASE PLAUSIBILITY: MEDIUM — The national security argument has genuine substance, and government investment in strategic industries has precedent. However, five specific features are not explained by the null case:
1. The explicit rejection of governance mechanisms (no board seats, no worker protections) despite multi-billion dollar investments — national security does not require declining governance.
2. The AI Action Plan's characterization of worker protections (childcare mandates) as "extraneous policy requirements" and "sweeping ideological agendas" — this language serves identifiable corporate interests, not national security.
3. The overlap between beneficiary firms/sectors (tech, fossil fuel, real estate, asset management) and administration political support networks.
4. The energy dimension — households bearing disproportionate utility costs while firms receive subsidized energy access — is a distributional choice, not a security necessity.
5. The Sanders/Warren equity proposal requiring "benefit of taxpayers" was rejected — the governance gap is documented as deliberate, not accidental.

The null case holds at the level of individual decisions (any one investment could be justified) but fails at the structural level (the pattern of investments systematically socializes risk while declining governance, benefiting identifiable actors).

FALSIFIABLE BY: Evidence that the governance gap produces public benefit — i.e., that Intel, MP Materials, or other equity investments return value to the public, that NEPA-exempt projects do not produce disproportionate community harm, or that "full stack" export requirements lead to genuine technology transfer rather than dependency.

LLM BIAS CHECK: I am not hedging. The evidence is specific, sourced, and quantified. The brief documents executive orders, financial figures, and policy statements that support the power explanation. The null case is noted as partially plausible (MEDIUM) because the national security dimension is real, not because the evidence is uncertain.

## Briefing: The Big AI State — State-Directed AI Industrial Policy as "Deregulation"

### Key finding

The Trump administration's AI policy contradicts its own "deregulation" framing: it is using executive orders, federal land, public financing, and direct equity investment to organize the AI market and socialize investment risk — while explicitly declining governance mechanisms (no board seats, no worker protections) that would convert public investment into public accountability.

| Actor | Gains | At whose expense |
|-------|-------|------------------|
| AI and tech firms (OpenAI, Intel, Oracle, SoftBank) | Subsidized infrastructure, derisked investment, federal financing, tariff exemptions | Taxpayers bearing equity risk without governance rights |
| Fossil fuel and energy firms | Data center energy demand, weakened NEPA review, expanded coal and nuclear construction | Communities near data centers and power plants, households facing higher utility costs |
| Critical mineral firms (MP Materials, Vulcan, ReElement) | Government equity, offtake agreements at above-market rates, guaranteed demand | Taxpayers; communities near mining operations; foreign supply chain competitors |

### What is absent

- Communities affected by NEPA-exempt construction — environmental justice dimension documented but not centered
- Workers displaced or managed by AI systems deployed through subsidized infrastructure

- Public as stakeholders in $10+ billion of equity investments with no governance rights
- Assessment of what happens when the AI business model ($5T investment / $650B/year revenue needed) fails to sustain itself

### Findings

1. **"Deregulation" as narrative inversion**
   LAYER: Thought & Narrative
   STATUS: observed
   The administration presents its AI policy as deregulation — weakening environmental protections, blocking state/local regulation, rolling back Biden-era guidance. Simultaneously, it executes massive state intervention through executive orders, agency coordination, public financing, and equity investment. The brief documents this contradiction with specific policy citations. The administration's own statements ("every tool at our disposal," AI Action Plan language) confirm the interventionist reality beneath the deregulatory framing.

2. **NEPA categorical exclusions as risk externalization**
   LAYER: Legal & Regulatory, Economic
   STATUS: observed
   Three executive orders (EOs 14318, 14261, 14299) direct agencies to establish categorical exclusions for data center, coal, and nuclear projects, bypassing environmental impact assessment required under NEPA. This removes procedural opportunities for community input and opposition. The brief notes that data center construction disproportionately affects communities of color and that civil rights groups have voiced concerns about "reproducing histories of displacement."

3. **Federal land conversion from public to private use**
   LAYER: Legal & Regulatory, Economic, Institutional
   STATUS: observed
   The administration has ordered agencies to identify and lease federal lands for data centers, coal infrastructure, and nuclear plants. DOE has selected four sites (Idaho National Lab, Oak Ridge, Savannah River, Paducah) for AI data center build-outs. This converts public assets into subsidized private infrastructure without corresponding public governance or benefit-sharing.

4. **The socialized risk architecture**
   LAYER: Economic
   STATUS: observed (mechanisms); inferred (structural function)
   Multiple financial instruments — loans, grants, tariff exemptions ($19B/2025), 100% bonus depreciation, offtake agreements — distribute investment risk to the public while concentrating returns in private firms. The brief's characterization ("socialize the risks of private AI spending to the American public, but without a corresponding mechanism to share the rewards") is supported by the documented absence of governance mechanisms and the rejection of the Sanders/Warren equity-with-public-benefit proposal.

5. **Equity without governance as deliberate structural choice**
   LAYER: Economic, Institutional, Legal & Regulatory
   STATUS: observed (the fact); inferred (the deliberateness)
   The government invested $8.9 billion in Intel for a 9.9% stake with no board seats. The AI Action Plan explicitly rejects "extraneous policy requirements" including CHIPS Act worker protections (childcare mandates), characterizing them as "sweeping ideological agendas." The Sanders/Warren alternative requiring equity "for the benefit of taxpayers" was rejected. The governance gap is not an oversight — it is a documented design choice. The observable effect is public investment that cannot be directed toward public ends.

6. **"Full stack" requirements as technological dependency creation**
   LAYER: Economic, Institutional, Surveillance & Information
   STATUS: observed (the requirement); inferred (the dependency function)
   The AI exports program requires foreign governments to adopt complete US technology packages — hardware, data systems, foundation models, and applications — as a condition of access. The EO empowers EDAG to make exports contingent on "pro-innovation regulatory, data, and infrastructure environments," constraining recipient countries' regulatory autonomy. This is consistent with the concept of "weaponized interdependence" — structural dependency through infrastructure, not just trade.

7. **Defense Production Act repurposing for civilian technology**
   LAYER: Physical & Coercive, Economic, Legal & Regulatory
   STATUS: observed
   The MP Materials deal ($400M, 15% stake, DoD as largest shareholder) was executed under Defense Production Act Title III. The DoD committed to purchase output at double market rate for ten years. Five days later, Apple committed $500M in purchases from MP. The brief notes the administration is "leveraging technology components with 'dual use' applications . . . to its advantage, exploiting policy levers on the defense side to pump private investments on the commercial side." The DPA — designed for wartime production — is being applied to civilian AI supply chains.

8. **The business model sustainability gap**
   LAYER: Economic
   STATUS: observed (the figures); speculative (the implications)
   J.P. Morgan estimates $5T global AI investment needs $650B/year in product revenue for 10% returns. Few users are paying; superuser costs exceed revenue; 80% of adopting companies see no bottom-line change. The state intervention documented in this brief is, in part, an effort to sustain an investment cycle whose commercial fundamentals have not been established. If the business model fails, the government's equity positions, offtake commitments, and NEPA-exempt infrastructure become stranded public obligations.

9. **Energy consumption externalization**
   LAYER: Economic, Legal & Regulatory
   STATUS: observed
   US data centers consumed 183 TWh in 2024 — over 4% of total US electricity consumption, comparable to Pakistan's total. Projected to double to 426 TWh by 2030. The brief cites analysis showing that "ordinary households, not data center operators and their corporate tenants, face disproportionate increases in their utility expenses." Rather than treating energy constraint as a limiting factor, the administration treats it as justification for removing environmental protections and expanding fossil fuel and nuclear infrastructure.

10. **The "derisking" euphemism**
    LAYER: Thought & Narrative, Economic
    STATUS: observed (the language); inferred (the function)
    "Derisking" — a term from development economics applied to encouraging private green investment — is repurposed in this context to describe government absorption of private investment risk. ### Null case

The national security argument is genuine: China is competing for AI dominance, and government investment in strategic industries has historical precedent (DARPA, NASA, highways). Individual policy decisions could each be justified on pragmatic grounds. NEPA processes are genuinely slow (4-7 years for major projects). Equity stakes are arguably more taxpayer-protective than grants. The null case is rated **MEDIUM**: plausible at the level of individual decisions but insufficient at the structural level, where the systematic pattern — socializing risk, declining governance, rejecting worker protections, benefiting identifiable political allies — is not explained by national security logic alone. The governance gap in particular (no board seats despite $8.9B; explicit rejection of worker protections as "ideological agendas") is not predicted by the null case.
