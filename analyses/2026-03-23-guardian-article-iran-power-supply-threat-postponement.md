## Hegemonic context

Both the material and the analyst operate within the **national security state** background — the assumption that the US has the capacity and, by structural implication, the authority to determine whether a sovereign nation's civilian infrastructure survives. The article treats Trump's threat as a question of whether he will follow through, not whether the threat itself constitutes a violation of international humanitarian law (the Fourth Geneva Convention prohibits attacks on civilian objects indispensable to the survival of the civilian population). The framework shares this assumption at the background level while noting that Nasri, an Iranian international lawyer, names the legal dimension the hegemonic frame suppresses. Maintaining forces include media norms that frame threats to civilian infrastructure as "escalation options" rather than as potential war crimes, the classification system that controls what the public knows about operational planning, and the **Perpetual Threat as Perpetual Justification** pattern operating in both directions — Iran's nuclear programme justifies US military posture; the US attack justifies Iran's retaliatory threats.

## Narrative

Trump announced his intention to attack Iran's energy infrastructure — the power supply for 90 million people — then postponed the attack after claiming "productive conversations" with Tehran that Iran promptly denied. The postponement produced temporary relief in Iran while leaving the threat operative. Turkish and Omani diplomatic channels remain active, though whether they produced the postponement is not established.

The structural observation at the centre of this material is that the threat functions as a mechanism of control independent of its execution. The announcement places an entire civilian population in a state of indefinite existential uncertainty — contemplating extended darkness, no water, no gas, no food, no transportation. The postponement does not remove the threat; it extends the uncertainty. Each reprieve reinforces the power relationship: the population experiences gratitude for the absence of destruction that should not have been threatened. The threat-then-postpone cycle operates as leverage without expenditure. This is a specific variant of the framework's Economy of Violence pattern: violence is most effective when its potential is maintained while its execution is deferred.

The article provides the most extensive Position 2 (Iranian civilian) sourcing in the corpus's Iran war cluster. Reformist writer Zeidabadi describes the threat as "the greatest threat posed against our country or any other country in the world throughout history" and appeals to "the people of America or other countries" to "stop this savage being." Masoud Pezeshkian's son writes that "whatever we do sooner or later comes back to haunt us." Former ambassador Baharvand states that "no honour or credibility is added to a super power if it attacks civilian facilities." International lawyer Nasri warns that attacking power plants "would not be a war crime carried out in the chaos of battle, but something premeditated and announced in advance." Each voice articulates the structural situation from inside it: the mechanism is visible, its contingency is visible, but the capacity to prevent it is absent.

A significant internal Iranian political observation emerges from the material: the power supply threat may function as a distraction from the strategic objective of seizing control of the Strait of Hormuz. Former parliament deputy speaker Motahari states this directly: the power plant attack threat was "a deception designed to divert attention from plans to seize islands in the strait." If correct, this connects the threat-as-mechanism analysis to the strait gatekeeper analysis: the visible threat (infrastructure destruction) manages attention while the structural objective (geographic control of energy transit) operates beneath it. The power supply threat is the appearance; Hormuz seizure is the function.

Iran's defence council response extends the strait analysis from the Hormuz chokepoint to the entire Persian Gulf. The council warns that any coastal or island attack would lead to mining "all access routes and communication lines in the Persian Gulf" so that "the entire Persian Gulf will effectively be blocked." The council invokes the 1980s precedent: "more than 1,000 mine clearers failing to clear a limited number of naval mines." The asymmetry is geographic and economic: mining is cheap and fast; clearance is expensive and slow. The statement concludes: "The only way for non-hostile countries to pass through the strait of Hormuz is in coordination with Iran" — restating the permission-based transit regime documented in the Al Jazeera analysis.

The Diego Garcia missile claim — which Iran denies and which NATO Secretary General Rutte acknowledges cannot be confirmed as an Iranian ICBM — functions as a narrative instrument. The unconfirmed claim, if accepted, would establish that Iran's missile programme threatens Europe, expanding the coalition of interests aligned with military action. British minister Steve Reed's statement — "there is no specific assessment that the Iranians are targeting the UK or even could if they wanted to" — contradicts this narrative from within the alliance, paralleling the UK's position in the Geneva talks analysis (Powell's inside knowledge contradicting the institutional narrative).

## So What

The threat-then-postpone cycle produces a specific structural condition: a population held in indefinite suspension between destruction and reprieve. If maintained, this cycle converts a military threat into a permanent governance instrument — the capacity to plunge 90 million people into darkness becomes leverage that does not require execution to produce compliance. The structural question is whether the postponement reflects genuine diplomatic progress or the maintenance of threat-as-leverage.

Watch items:
- Whether the postponement expires and the attack proceeds, or whether the cycle repeats (a second postponement would confirm the threat-as-leverage reading)
- Whether the US attempts Kharg Island or Gulf island seizure during the period when attention is focused on power supply (Motahari's distraction thesis)
- Whether Turkey and Oman produce a formal diplomatic framework or whether their mediation remains informal and deniable
- Whether Iran's mining threat is operationalised beyond the strait to the full Persian Gulf (would transform the regional strategic configuration)
- Whether the Diego Garcia missile claim is independently verified or whether it remains an unconfirmed narrative instrument
- Whether European states respond to the infrastructure threat as a potential war crime (Nasri's legal framing) or as a strategic option (the hegemonic frame)

## Circumventions

### Structural

**Transparency / Naming**: The Iranian voices in this article — Zeidabadi, Nasri, Baharvand, Pezeshkian's son — are performing a naming function: identifying the threat as potential war crime (Nasri), as moral outrage (Baharvand), as civilisational threat (Zeidabadi). This is naming from Position 2 — the governed naming the mechanism being applied to them. Observed outcome: the naming reaches international audiences through the Guardian but has not produced international intervention. Failure mode: the naming is absorbed into the news cycle as "Iranian reaction" rather than treated as a legal and moral claim requiring a response.

**International Pressure**: Turkey and Oman are mediating. The UK has distanced itself from the Diego Garcia claim. These constitute forms of international pressure that have not (yet) produced structural change but may have contributed to the postponement. Observed outcome: the threat is deferred, not withdrawn. The mediators' leverage is limited by the power asymmetry.

**Legal Challenge**: Nasri explicitly frames the threatened attack as premeditated and announced in advance — a stronger legal case than crimes committed in the fog of war. UN human rights experts have previously called for investigation of the Minab strike. Observed outcome: no legal mechanism has been activated. Failure mode: the US is not party to the ICC and holds a UN Security Council veto.

### Hegemonic

The article operates within the assumption that the US has the structural capacity to determine whether Iran's civilian infrastructure survives — and that the relevant question is whether it will exercise that capacity, not whether the capacity itself constitutes a power arrangement. A hegemonic circumvention would require contesting the background assumption that one state may hold another state's civilian population hostage to strategic demands. The Fourth Geneva Convention formally prohibits this, but the prohibition is inoperative against a state with Security Council veto power. The hegemonic arrangement is visible in the structure of the threat itself: it is announced publicly, debated as a "strategic option," and postponed as a "gesture" — all within a frame that treats 90 million people's survival as a policy variable.

## Null case

The non-power explanation: Trump's threat is genuine military signalling designed to bring Iran to negotiations, the postponement reflects real diplomatic progress through Turkish and Omani channels, and the threat-postponement cycle is standard coercive diplomacy rather than a novel mechanism of indefinite population control. The power supply target reflects military logic (degrading Iran's industrial and military capacity) rather than a deliberate choice to threaten civilians.

**Plausibility: MEDIUM at the diplomatic level; LOW at the humanitarian level.** The diplomatic reading has some force — Turkey and Oman are genuinely mediating, and the postponement may reflect their work. However, the null case does not explain: (1) why the target is civilian infrastructure (power supply for 90 million people) rather than military targets; (2) why the threat was announced publicly rather than communicated through diplomatic channels — public announcement produces mass fear, which is a control mechanism, not a negotiating tactic; (3) why Trump claimed conversations that Iran denied — suggesting the diplomatic framing is itself an appearance; (4) the structural parallel to the Geneva talks (diplomatic process announced while military action was being prepared). The humanitarian dimension — treating 90 million people's survival as a policy variable — is not explained by diplomatic logic.
