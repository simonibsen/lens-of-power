## Narrative

Forbidden Stories' investigation into the death of Ukrainian journalist
Viktoriia Roshchyna documents three nested structures: a journalist's
individual trajectory from investigation to detention to death; the
institutional architecture of Russia's informal detention system in
occupied Ukraine; and the information architecture that makes both
possible.

Roshchyna traveled to Russian-occupied Zaporizhzhia in the summer of
2023 to report on what she and her editor called "ghost detainees" — an
estimated 16,000 to 20,000 Ukrainian civilians held in Russia's system
of ad hoc prisons and torture centers. She was compiling a database of
FSB agents and documenting torture facilities. She was captured, likely
identified through surveillance in Enerhodar, and transferred through
at least three detention sites: a police station in Enerhodar used to
"filter" suspected resisters, an informal torture center in Melitopol
known as "the garages," and SIZO-2 in Taganrog — a former juvenile
detention center repurposed for Ukrainian prisoners. Ten former
detainees describe institutionalized torture at Taganrog: beatings,
electric shocks, near-drowning, with interrogators instructed to push
prisoners to the limits of survival.

Roshchyna was the only journalist covering occupied territories. Her
editor states that after her disappearance, "there is no coverage of
what is happening." The investigation documents a closed structural
loop: the occupation creates an information vacuum over the occupied
zone; the journalist who attempts to penetrate that vacuum is absorbed
into the system she was documenting; her disappearance deepens the
vacuum. The system that produces the darkness eliminates those who
bring light, which deepens the darkness.

The forensic record, as documented by Ukrainian prosecutors, shows
signs of torture on the body (abrasions, broken rib, neck injuries,
possible electric shock marks on feet) and evidence consistent with
an attempt to conceal the cause of death: several organs removed —
including parts of the brain, the larynx, and eyeballs — before the
body was returned. An 11-day gap exists between Roshchyna's removal
from her cell (September 8) and the official death date (September
19). No Russian officials responded to requests for comment.

The article's structure is personal — Roshchyna's story as an entry
point — but the investigation's scope is systemic. Forbidden Stories
continued her unfinished work on the ghost detention system, making
this article simultaneously an account of her death and an act of the
same investigation that led to it. The article does not explore the
legal accountability pathway for the documented crimes, the internal
institutional logic of the Russian detention system, or the experiences
of the broader ghost detainee population beyond Roshchyna's trajectory.

The central reinforcement loop: Physical control creates information

CONFIRMS:

CONTRADICTS: None.

EXTENDS:
- Naming as Disruption (A4) receives an extension: the namer's

## Step 5: INVERT

INVERSE CLAIM: Russia detained Roshchyna as a suspected intelligence
operative, not as a journalist. She entered Russian-controlled
territory through irregular means (Poland → Latvia → Russia), carried
no press credentials recognized by Russian authorities, and was
compiling a database of FSB agents — activity indistinguishable from
intelligence collection. Her death was a tragic outcome of
generally poor detention conditions affecting all prisoners, not a
targeted killing. The detention system, while harsh, operates within
the context of an active military conflict where civilian screening
("filtration") is a wartime necessity.

STRONGEST DEFENSE: The strongest version of the Russian position is
structural rather than moral: in an active conflict zone, any state
detains people who cross into its territory through unauthorized
channels and compile databases of its intelligence agents. The
conditions at SIZO-2 affect all detainees, not specifically
Roshchyna. The system is brutal and indiscriminate — which is a
different claim than that it specifically targeted her for her
journalism. One former prisoner suggests exactly this: "They pushed
it too far" — implying her death was an excess of routine violence,
not an assassination.

NULL CASE: Could this be explained without reference to systematic
power dynamics?
- The *ghost detention system*: Not plausibly accidental. An estimated
  16,000-20,000 people held in an informal system with repurposed
  facilities, transfer protocols, and formal notification procedures is
  institutional infrastructure, not chaos.
- The *torture*: Not plausibly incompetence. Ten former detainees
  describe institutionalized practices. Interrogators were instructed
  to push limits. "Reception" beatings are routine.
- *Roshchyna's specific death*: The null case is more plausible here.
  She may have died from accumulated abuse rather than deliberate
  killing. "They pushed it too far" is consistent with routine
  brutality exceeding its intended limits. However, the 11-day gap
  between cell removal and official death date, combined with organ
  removal from the body, is not explained by the routine-excess
  account.
- The *information blackhole*: Structural feature of military
  occupation, not accidental. Every occupation produces information
  control; the question is degree.
- The *organ removal*: Not plausibly routine. Removing brain, larynx,
  and eyeballs from a body before repatriation serves no legitimate
  forensic purpose and is consistent with concealment.

NULL CASE PLAUSIBILITY: LOW for the system as a whole and for the
information architecture. MEDIUM for the specific circumstances of
Roshchyna's death — routine brutality exceeding its limits is
plausible, though the 11-day gap and organ removal resist this
reading.

FALSIFIABLE BY: Evidence that (a) the ghost detention system is not
centrally coordinated but is a spontaneous phenomenon of individual
units acting without institutional structure; (b) Roshchyna's death
was investigated internally by Russian authorities with genuine
accountability outcomes; (c) the organ removal has a legitimate
forensic explanation; (d) the 11-day gap is explained by documented
medical treatment or transfer records.

LLM BIAS CHECK: The evidence for systematic torture and
institutionalized detention is multiply sourced (ten former detainees,
Ukrainian prosecutors, forensic examination, border documents, prison
testimonies). I am not hedging due to alignment training — the
evidence warrants directness. However, I note that my training likely
overrepresents Ukrainian and Western sources on this conflict and may
underrepresent the internal institutional logic of the Russian system
as experienced by its own personnel. This does not change the
analysis of the documented facts but means the institutional
mechanisms may be more complex than the investigation (or my
analysis) captures.

## Briefing: The Infrastructure of Disappearance

DATE: 2026-03-02
SOURCE TYPE: Collaborative investigative report
SOURCE POSITION: Position 5 (external observer / investigative
journalist) with elements of Position 3 (structural critique)

### Key finding

Russia's ghost detention system operates as institutional
infrastructure — with facilities, transfer protocols, and formal
processes — while remaining outside any legal framework. Roshchyna's
absorption into the system she was investigating demonstrates that the
system recognizes investigative journalism as counter-surveillance
and responds by incorporating the observer into the observed system,
deepening the information blackhole that enables the system to operate.

### Who benefits / who bears cost

| Actor | Gains | At whose expense |
|-------|-------|------------------|
| Russian occupation forces | Unaccountable control over civilian population through detention, torture, and deterrence | 16,000-20,000 ghost detainees — Ukrainian civilians held without legal status or process |
| Russian state | Information control over occupied territories — no independent reporting, no external accountability | Roshchyna (life, investigation), other potential journalists (deterrence), occupied population (voicelessness) |
| Forbidden Stories | Institutional validation of continuation model; high-profile investigation | Roshchyna — the model depends on journalists being killed to demonstrate its necessity |

### What is absent

- The 16,000-20,000 ghost detainees' own voices — known by number, not
  heard as people
- The internal institutional logic of the Russian system — how
  personnel experience and justify their roles
- The international legal accountability pathway — what mechanisms
  exist and whether they are functioning
- The occupied civilian population as agents, not only as victims

### Findings

1. **The ghost detention system is institutional infrastructure, not
   ad hoc violence**
   LAYER: Physical & Coercive, Institutional
   STATUS: observed
   Russia's detention of Ukrainian civilians operates through a
   multi-tier system: "filtration" stations at the front line,
   informal torture centers in occupied cities (Melitopol "garages"),
   and formal prisons repurposed from other functions (SIZO-2
   Taganrog, a former juvenile detention center). Transfer protocols
   exist between tiers. Formal processes (death notification, ICRC
   repatriation) operate at the output end. The structure is
   institutional, not chaotic.

2. **The information blackhole is a structural condition of
   occupation, not a byproduct**
   LAYER: Surveillance & Information, Thought & Narrative
   STATUS: inferred
   The occupied zone functions as what the article calls an
   "informational black hole." Roshchyna was the only journalist
   covering occupied territories; after her disappearance, her editor
   confirms "there is no coverage of what is happening." The
   information vacuum is maintained by the same physical control
   that produces the detention system. The observable effect is that
   physical control and information control are mutually reinforcing:
   occupation produces the darkness that prevents accountability for
   what occupation does.

3. **The namer absorbed into the named system**
   LAYER: Surveillance & Information, Physical & Coercive
   STATUS: observed (absorption) / inferred (structural significance)
   Roshchyna was investigating the ghost detention system. She became
   a ghost detainee. This is not coincidental — she was compiling FSB
   databases and investigating torture facilities, activity the system
   recognizes as a structural threat. The response was to eliminate
   the counter-surveillance by absorbing the observer into the system
   being observed. This extends Alley's Naming as Disruption principle:
   when the system cannot absorb the naming through formal responses
   (the Kimberley Process variant), it eliminates the namer directly.

4. **Evidence redaction through organ removal**
   LAYER: Surveillance & Information, Physical & Coercive
   STATUS: observed (organs removed) / inferred (concealment intent)
   Roshchyna's body was returned with several organs removed,
   including parts of the brain, the larynx, and eyeballs. Ukrainian
   prosecutors suggest this was intended to mask cause of death. If
   deliberate, this represents an extension of information control to
   the forensic record — treating the body as a document and redacting
   it. The mislabeling of the body bag as "Unnamed Male" is consistent
   with this pattern: misidentification at every stage of the return.

5. **Continuity with the gulag tradition**
   LAYER: Physical & Coercive, Institutional
   STATUS: inferred
   The article describes SIZO-2 as "reminiscent of the worst Soviet
   gulags," and the described practices — "reception" beatings upon
   arrival, institutionalized torture, extrajudicial detention of
   civilians — are structurally continuous with the gulag system. This
   is not a historical analogy but an institutional lineage: the
   Russian prison system never fully dismantled the institutional
   culture of the gulag. The formal transformation (Soviet → Russian
   Federation) did not produce structural discontinuity.

6. **Forbidden Stories as structural counter-mechanism**
   LAYER: Institutional, Thought & Narrative, Surveillance & Information
   STATUS: observed (model) / speculative (effectiveness)
   Forbidden Stories' model — continuing murdered journalists'
   investigations through collaborative international teams — is a
   structural response to the structural problem of killing the namer.
   The continuation does not prevent the killing but reduces its
   informational effectiveness: the investigation persists even when
   the investigator does not. Whether this produces accountability
   outcomes (beyond publication) or triggers a formal absorption
   response (creating appearances of accountability without substance)
   remains to be observed.

7. **The ghost detention system is not uniquely Russian**
   LAYER: All layers
   STATUS: inferred
   The mechanism — military occupation or "emergency" creating legal
   ambiguity that enables extrajudicial detention — recurs across
   contexts and eras: Soviet gulags, Nazi Night and Fog, Pinochet's
   desaparecidos, US black sites, Israeli administrative detention,
   Chinese detention of Uyghurs. The structural insight is the
   mechanism (occupation → legal ambiguity → extrajudicial detention
   → information control → impunity), not the national attribution.
   This finding exists in tension with the article's framing, which
   is specific to Russia-Ukraine — appropriately so for an
   investigation, but the pattern generalizes.

### Null case

The null case is rejected for the system (LOW plausibility) — an
infrastructure detaining an estimated 16,000-20,000 people across
multiple tiers of facilities with transfer protocols is not accidental.
Institutionalized torture confirmed by ten former detainees is not
incompetence. The information blackhole is a structural feature of
occupation, not a byproduct.

The null case is MEDIUM for the specific circumstances of Roshchyna's
death: routine brutality exceeding its limits ("they pushed it too far")
is plausible and is suggested by a former detainee's testimony. However,
the 11-day gap between cell removal and official death date, combined
with the removal of organs from the body, resists the routine-excess
account.

The distinction may matter less than it appears: whether Roshchyna was
deliberately killed or died from accumulated routine brutality, the
system that produced the outcome is the same. The ghost detention
system's structural function — extrajudicial control of the civilian
population through detention, torture, and deterrence — does not depend
on the intent behind any individual death.
