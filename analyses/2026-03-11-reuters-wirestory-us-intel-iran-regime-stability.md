## Narrative

US intelligence assessments conclude that Iran's government "is not in danger" of collapse and "retains control of the Iranian public" after nearly two weeks of US-Israeli bombardment. The assessments persist despite the killing of Supreme Leader Khamenei on 28 February and "dozens of senior officials" and IRGC commanders. The Assembly of Experts declared Khamenei's son Mojtaba the new supreme leader. Three anonymous sources provided the intelligence findings to Reuters; the ODNI, CIA, and White House either declined to comment or did not respond.

The article documents a three-way divergence between the war's stated purpose, its official walk-back, and the intelligence community's own assessment. Trump "urged Iranians to take over your government" when announcing the operation — a regime change call. Subsequently, "top aides have since denied that the objective was to oust Iran's leadership." Simultaneously, the intelligence community concludes that regime collapse is not achievable under current operations. The stated purpose has shifted at least twice in two weeks, and neither version aligns with what US intelligence assesses as structurally possible. The article describes this as "the biggest U.S. military operation since 2003" — an Iraq comparison the reporters make implicitly through the date reference without stating the parallel.

The article reports a secondary channel: Iranian Kurdish militias based in Iraq "consulted with the U.S. about how and whether to attack Iran's security forces." Abdullah Mohtadi of the Komala Party claims "tens of thousands of young people are ready to take up arms" if given US support. US intelligence separately "cast doubt on the ability of the groups to sustain a fight," assessing them as lacking "firepower and numbers." Trump then "ruled out" having Kurdish groups enter Iran. The sequence — proxy consultation, intelligence scepticism, presidential rejection — is a compressed version of a pattern documented across US regime change operations: the instrumentalisation and subsequent abandonment of ethnic proxy forces.

The intelligence leak itself is structurally significant. Three anonymous sources provided classified assessments to Reuters that contradict the administration's stated position. This represents institutional actors — likely intelligence professionals — using the press to create a public record of divergence between the executive's rhetoric and the intelligence community's analysis. The article notes "the latest report was completed within the last few days," establishing temporal proximity between the assessment and the leak. The function of the leak is to make the divergence visible before the war's outcome forecloses alternative accounts.

## So What

The documented intelligence-executive divergence creates a specific structural prediction: if the war ends without regime change (as intelligence assesses is likely), the administration will need to redefine success retroactively. The framework predicts this based on the Serial Regime Change pattern — prior instances (Iraq, Libya) show post-hoc redefinition of war aims when original objectives are not achieved. The monitor is what language the administration uses when announcing the end of operations.

The Kurdish proxy channel, though currently shelved, establishes a template that could be reactivated. The Kurdish groups have formally requested weapons and armoured vehicles from Washington. If the conventional bombing campaign fails to achieve its objectives (as intelligence predicts), the proxy option returns to the table despite Trump's current rejection. The monitor is whether Kurdish groups receive any form of US material support in the coming weeks.

The intelligence leak creates institutional cover for the intelligence community: if the war's outcome matches their assessment, the public record shows they warned. This functions as an institutional hedge against post-war accountability, paralleling the pre-Iraq intelligence debates that became significant during subsequent investigations.

**Watch items:**
- Language used by the administration when announcing end of operations (tests retroactive redefinition)
- Whether Kurdish groups receive US material support despite Trump's stated rejection
- Whether "soaring oil costs" produce the political pressure the article identifies as constraining Trump
- Whether further intelligence leaks document divergence on war conduct, not just war objectives
- Whether Israel's stated intention to not "allow any remnants of the former government to stay intact" diverges from US willingness to end operations

## Circumventions

**Transparency / Naming**: The intelligence leak to Reuters is an instance of this circumvention type — making hidden information visible through journalism. The mechanism operates through anonymous sources using institutional credibility to create a public record. Outcome range: the leak has established a factual counter-narrative to the administration's shifting rationale. Whether this naming produces policy change (war termination) or is absorbed into the information environment without structural effect is not yet observable. Failure mode risk: the leak may be captured by the news cycle — reported, consumed, and displaced by the next day's developments without altering the war's trajectory. This is the Real-Time Narrative Saturation failure mode applied to investigative disclosure.

**Institutional Reform (inverse)**: The intelligence community is asserting its analytical independence against executive overreach — the inverse of Institutional Abdication. This is a fragile circumvention: it operates through individual professionals choosing to leak rather than through institutional mechanisms (congressional oversight, formal dissent channels). The prior framework analysis of the Senate war powers vote documented institutional abdication at the congressional level; this article documents institutional assertion at the intelligence level. The two institutions are moving in opposite directions on the same conflict.

## Null case

**Initial null case**: This is routine intelligence reporting by competent wire service journalists. The divergence between intelligence assessments and stated war aims is a standard feature of wartime reporting — intelligence communities frequently assess outcomes differently from political leadership, and this gap is newsworthy in itself. The Reuters reporters are doing their job: reporting what sources tell them. No power-dynamics framework is needed to understand this article.

**Assessment**: The null case is partially weakened at the structural level. The article's individual facts are competently reported, and the null case holds for the article as a piece of journalism — it is what it claims to be. But the structural pattern visible across the article's facts — a war launched despite intelligence indicating its stated objectives are unachievable, with the stated purpose shifting twice in two weeks, a proxy channel opened and shelved, and intelligence professionals leaking to create a divergent record — is a configuration that the wire service format reports but does not synthesize. The framework adds the synthesis: this configuration has structural parallels to prior regime change operations (Iraq 2003 being the most direct), and the pattern of intelligence-executive divergence, proxy instrumentalisation, and post-hoc redefinition of war aims is documented across those cases. A reader without the framework could identify the Iraq parallel (the article implicitly invites it), but would not systematically connect it to the broader Serial Regime Change template or identify the intelligence leak as a circumvention mechanism.

**Null case outcome: plausible** (MEDIUM — the article is competent wire reporting; the framework adds structural pattern recognition that the wire format does not provide but that an attentive reader could partially reconstruct).
