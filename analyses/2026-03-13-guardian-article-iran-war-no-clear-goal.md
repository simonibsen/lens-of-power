## Narrative

The article documents a structural gap between US military capability and political purpose in the Iran war. Five named former officials and analysts — from Brookings, AEI, the Washington Institute, the former Office of the Secretary of Defense, and Defense Priorities — converge on a single diagnosis: the military execution of Operation Epic Fury has been competent, but the political direction has been absent or incoherent. Michael Rubin's formulation is the most precise: "We have the targeting, but we don't have a clear goal, and that lies not on the Pentagon planners, but on Donald Trump."

The article traces a sequence of four stated war aims — backing Iranian protesters, eliminating the nuclear programme, destroying the ballistic missile system, and reopening the Strait of Hormuz — each presented as the operative goal without acknowledgment that the previous one has been abandoned. This serial redefinition is not described in the article as a pattern; it is documented as a series of individual policy adjustments. The framework identifies it as structurally continuous with the goal-shifting documented in earlier analyses of this conflict: the stated purpose of the operation has changed at least six times since the January naval buildup, across the Guardian live blog (28 Feb), the Fox News Senate war powers coverage (4 March), the leaked embassy cable (4 March), the Reuters intelligence assessment (11 March), and now this retrospective.

The institutional conditions that produced the planning gap are documented in the article's own account: Trump "gutted the national security council," Rubio "proceeded with major layoffs" at the State Department, and the "closely held decision to launch the strikes meant that official directions had not been handed down to the state department and other key agencies." The article treats these as context for the planning failure. The framework reads them as the primary mechanism: the "deep state" narrative redefined institutional expertise as conspiracy, which legitimised the elimination of the interagency process. The language preceded the institutional hollowing, and the institutional hollowing preceded the strategic incoherence. The consequences the article documents — 175 dead in a school strike from outdated targeting data, no citizen evacuation plan, destruction of the US's own preferred Iranian successors, $11.3 billion in six days — are downstream effects of a causal chain that begins at the Thought & Narrative layer and terminates at the Physical & Coercive layer.

Two details carry structural significance beyond the article's own framing. First, the war forced a reversal of Russian oil sanctions policy — one exercise of power (war) directly undermining another (economic leverage over Russia in a separate conflict). Second, the redeployment of air-defence assets from Asia to the Middle East degrades US military posture against North Korea and China. Jennifer Kavanagh's assessment that "the implications of this are going to last for decades" describes a structural constraint, not a temporary inconvenience: military capacity invested in Iran is capacity unavailable elsewhere. The prior framework analysis of North Korea's nuclear calculus (10 March) identified exactly this structural consequence as a prediction; the current article documents it as an observed fact.

Trump's statement about Iranian security forces — "They literally have people in the streets with machine guns, machine-gunning people down if they want to protest. That's a pretty big hurdle to climb for people that don't have weapons" — is an acknowledgment that the regime change theory requires the Iranian population to accomplish what the US military cannot. The administration's own account of the situation identifies the structural conditions that prevent the outcome its policy requires.

## Null case

**Initial null case** (stated before analysis): The consequences documented — school strike, Strait closure, cost overruns, resource redeployment — are the inherent costs of any major military intervention, regardless of planning quality. The Iraq war had extensive interagency planning and still produced comparable chaos. The expert critique represents the foreign policy establishment defending its institutional relevance.

**Assessment**: The null case is partially sustained at the component level. Individual operational failures (the school strike, the cost ambiguity, the Strait closure) are common features of large-scale military operations. The Iraq comparison cuts both ways — it demonstrates that planning does not prevent chaos. The establishment's critique does carry institutional self-interest: these are the people whose approach (JCPOA, interagency process) was dismantled, and the article gives them a platform to argue for its restoration.

However, the null case does not account for the specific causal sequence: the institutional dismantlement was deliberate and preceded the war, the exclusion of agencies from planning was by design, and the serial goal-shifting maps to a documented pattern across US regime change operations. The null case explains the consequences but not the conditions that produced them.

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM at component level (individual failures are common in war); MEDIUM-LOW at configurational level (the specific sequence — deliberate institutional dismantlement → closed decision circle → serial goal-shifting → cascading consequences — is not explained by war's inherent unpredictability).
