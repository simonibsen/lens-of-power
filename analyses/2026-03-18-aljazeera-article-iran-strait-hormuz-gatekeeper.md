## Hegemonic context

Both the material and the analyst operate within the **national security state** (the assumption that open waterways are a global public good requiring military enforcement and that the US has legitimate authority to project force to maintain them) and **market capitalism** (the assumption that energy markets should function without state interference and that oil price spikes are crises requiring resolution rather than shifts in bargaining power). The article partially challenges the military assumption by documenting the failure of force to restore passage, but treats the economic assumption as given — energy disruption is presented as self-evidently requiring resolution. Maintaining forces include the Carter Doctrine (1980 — US declared the Persian Gulf a vital interest worth military defence), the maritime insurance industry (translates military risk into commercial impossibility, enforcing the military reality through economic mechanisms), and **Perpetual Threat as Perpetual Justification** — the threat to the strait, produced by the US attack, justifies further military action to "reopen" it, in a self-generating cycle.

## Narrative

Iran has emerged as the de facto gatekeeper of the Strait of Hormuz, through which 20 percent of global crude oil and natural gas supplies pass. After the US-Israeli strikes began on 28 February, Iran responded by leveraging its control of the strait's northern shore. Approximately 20 vessel attacks and the threat of further attacks caused insurance companies to withdraw coverage, producing what the article describes as "near paralysis in maritime traffic" — a 97% reduction in traffic according to maritime intelligence firm Windward. Crude prices have risen above $100 a barrel, more than 20% above pre-war levels, with gas prices up more than 40%.

The mechanism through which Iran's leverage operates is economic, not primarily military. The insurance industry, not mines or missiles, enforces the shipping paralysis. A relatively small number of attacks produced prohibitive insurance premiums, which produced commercial impossibility. The physical layer (vessel attacks) operates *through* the economic layer (insurance markets) to achieve what a full naval blockade would require far greater resources to accomplish. This is the framework's **Layered Redundancy of Control** pattern — but deployed by the target of military action rather than by the dominant power.

The most structurally significant development is what Windward identifies as "permission-based transit." India, Pakistan, Turkey, and China have negotiated passage directly with Iran, including a phone call between Indian PM Modi and Iranian President Pezeshkian. The Financial Times reports Italy and France have also reached out. Ships are passing through Iranian territorial waters with Tehran's selective approval. Iran is not merely blocking the strait but administering it — deciding who passes and on what terms. Iranian FM Araghchi stated: "This is up to our military to decide." Pro-government figures frame the strait as a post-war bargaining tool for sanctions relief and economic concessions. The conversion of temporary military leverage into a permanent structural bargaining position is deliberate and stated.

Trump's coalition-building effort has produced the opposite of the intended outcome. He demanded that NATO allies, Japan, Australia, and others provide warships. Japan declined. Australia ruled out ships. The UK refused. Germany stated: "This is not our war." But the countries are not merely refusing to help — they are negotiating with the adversary. India, a country Trump specifically named as obligated to assist, conducted bilateral negotiations with Iran for passage. The structural effect is that the US attack has driven countries toward bilateral relationships with Iran rather than toward a US-led coalition. The expected isolation of the target has instead isolated the attacker.

The article documents Iran's capacity to extend pressure beyond the strait itself. A drone attack on the port of Fujairah — the UAE's only crude export terminal located outside the strait, designed to circumvent exactly this scenario — demonstrates that Iran or its proxies can target the workaround. The Houthis' stated readiness to disrupt Bab al-Mandeb would create a second maritime front. This is redundant pressure across multiple chokepoints, forcing the US to operate across multiple theatres.

The expert quoted at the article's close — Andreas Krieg of King's College London — states that the strait issue "is really not a military one. It's a market issue, and confidence cannot be restored by the military. Confidence can be restored through diplomacy only." This observation, cross-referenced with the Geneva talks analysis, closes a structural circle documentable across four prior analyses: diplomacy was foreclosed by military action (Geneva talks, 2026-03-17); military action produced consequences unsolvable by military means (this article); the only path to resolution is diplomacy (expert assessment); the diplomatic capacity and credibility needed to pursue that diplomacy has been damaged by the war itself.

## So What

The war has produced a structural inversion: Iran's leverage has increased rather than decreased as a result of the US-Israeli attack. The mechanism is geographic (Iran controls the shore) but the enforcement is economic (insurance markets, not physical blockade). This configuration is resistant to military resolution because even degraded Iranian capacity — a few attacks rather than many — is sufficient to keep insurance premiums prohibitive and traffic near zero.

Watch items:
- Whether bilateral passage agreements include political or economic concessions beyond transit (sanctions relief, diplomatic recognition, compensation)
- Whether Iran begins formally charging for passage or linking transit to specific demands
- Whether Houthis execute their stated readiness to disrupt Bab al-Mandeb, opening a second front
- Whether the US attempts Kharg Island seizure and what consequences follow
- Whether insurance markets resume coverage after specific military milestones (would test the null case — if coverage resumes, the military approach is working)
- Whether Russia provides military, intelligence, or diplomatic support to Iran's strait strategy
- Whether the bilateral relationships countries are building with Iran persist after the war, creating a permanent structural realignment

## Circumventions

### Structural

**International Pressure**: Allied refusals to join the strait operation, combined with bilateral negotiations with Iran, constitute a form of international pressure operating against the dominant actor (US) rather than against the target (Iran). Germany's "this is not our war" and India's direct negotiation with Iran are observed instances. Outcome: the US is increasingly isolated diplomatically on the strait question, though it retains overwhelming military capacity.

**Economic Alternative**: Countries negotiating directly with Iran for passage create an alternative economic channel that bypasses US military mediation. This reduces US leverage over energy transit — countries do not need the US to guarantee their access if they can negotiate it bilaterally with Iran. Observed outcome: India, Pakistan, Turkey, China have secured passage; traffic through Iranian territorial waters is increasing. This is the first instance in the corpus where an economic alternative circumvention operates against the dominant power's interests.

### Hegemonic

The article does not contest the background assumption that the strait should be "open" — available to all comers without Iran's consent. The post-WWII energy security architecture, in which the US guarantees waterway access through military force, is treated as the natural order from which the current situation deviates. A hegemonic circumvention would require asking whether that architecture is itself a power arrangement: the US guarantees access to energy that primarily benefits importing states and Gulf monarchies, and in return maintains military infrastructure that projects power throughout the region. Iran's "permission-based transit" implicitly contests this arrangement by asserting geographic sovereignty over access — but neither the article nor the framework has yet developed the vocabulary for this as a hegemonic challenge rather than a military tactic. The question the hegemonic frame would ask: is the "normal" state of the strait (open, US-guaranteed) itself a product of a specific power arrangement, and is Iran's gatekeeper role a disruption of a neutral order or a challenge to a constructed one?

## Null case

The non-power explanation: Iran's strait control is a temporary military contingency that will be resolved as US strikes degrade coastal capability. The "permission-based transit" is an improvised response, not a deliberate strategy. Countries negotiating with Iran are acting pragmatically — they need energy access now and will revert to the US-led framework once the military situation resolves. The coalition failure reflects the normal difficulty of multinational coordination, not structural realignment.

**Plausibility: MEDIUM.** The null case has genuine force for the military dimension — US bunker-busting strikes on antiship sites may degrade Iran's capacity, and the 97% traffic reduction is unsustainable for all parties, including Iran, creating mutual pressure toward resolution. However, the null case does not explain: (1) why countries are negotiating with Iran rather than waiting for US military resolution — this is a choice that creates new bilateral relationships; (2) why Iranian officials are explicitly framing the strait as a *post-war* bargaining tool — deliberate strategic positioning, not improvisation; (3) why the insurance mechanism ensures that even degraded Iranian capacity produces shipping paralysis — a few attacks suffice to keep premiums prohibitive; (4) why the Fujairah attack demonstrates capacity to target workarounds. The convergence with prior analyses — Geneva talks foreclosure, failed coalition (NATO analysis), serial goal-shifting ("no clear goal"), India's bilateral energy diplomacy (Indian refiners) — places this within a structural pattern not fully explained by temporary military contingency.
