## Hegemonic context

This material operates within two overlapping hegemonic backgrounds: **national_security_state** (the assumption that the Department of Homeland Security has legitimate surveillance needs requiring continuous technological investment) and **technological_progress** (the assumption that AI-enhanced surveillance represents an improvement over prior methods and that the private sector is the natural vehicle for developing state surveillance capacity). Both the article and this analysis share these backgrounds: the article's critical frame contests specific programs but does not question whether DHS should exist or whether the state should have surveillance capacity at this scale. The analyst's framework likewise treats surveillance as a layer of power rather than questioning its foundational premises.

**Maintaining forces**: The SBIR program itself (a Congressional mandate since 1982 requiring commercial applicability — structural reproducer that ensures surveillance technologies spread beyond their original state context). The defence-tech venture capital ecosystem (architect — see the AI-fication of K Street analysis). The $165bn DHS funding boost in last year's spending bill (institutional momentum). Feedback mechanisms include the Wealth Defense Industry pattern (contractor lobbying for continued procurement) and Precorporation (each new technology cycle absorbs prior critique — SPOT failed, so AI replaces it, formatted to appear fundamentally different).

## Narrative

Hacked data from the Department of Homeland Security's Office of Industry Partnership, provided to the Guardian by transparency nonprofit Distributed Denial of Secrets, exposes two decades of the **Small Business Innovation Research** (SBIR) pipeline — 1,400+ contracts worth a combined $845 million, and a registry of 6,800+ companies that have bid for the work. The data reveals the full scope of the private sector's appetite for homeland security surveillance contracts and the technologies DHS considered but did not pursue.

Three clusters of contracts awarded on 7 May 2025 — shortly after Trump's inauguration — are structurally significant. The first cluster funds mobile biometric scanning adapters (fingerprint, iris, and facial recognition via phone attachment), with all three contractors promising availability beyond DHS to "interagency stakeholders, non-federal entities, the intelligence community, and international mission partners." The second cluster funds AI analysis of existing airport CCTV to automatically catalogue passengers' physical characteristics at pre-checkpoint zones, including "anthropometric characteristics, clothing articles, shoe types, and visible accessories." The third cluster funds AI platforms to ingest all 911 call data nationally, with one — built by a newly registered firm, Cassius LLC — describing "geospatial heat maps" to "predict incident trends," a form of **predictive policing** (Brennan Center description: "tech-washing" that gives racially biased methods the appearance of objectivity).

The airport surveillance contracts are structurally continuous with DHS's earlier **Screening of Passengers by Observation Techniques** (SPOT) program, which deployed 3,000+ "behavior detection officers" at 176 airports at a cost of over $900 million. A 2013 GAO review found SPOT's ability to identify deceptive behaviour was "the same as or slightly better than chance." A follow-up 2017 GAO report found TSA could not produce valid evidence for 28 of 36 behavioural indicators and that 98% of the supporting sources it cited were invalid — including "news articles, opinion pieces, presentations created by law enforcement entities and industry groups, and screen shots of online medical websites." An ACLU investigation found the program had been used for racial profiling. The program was quietly eliminated in 2016–2017 and its technology successor, the FAST program, was quietly wound down after EPIC disclosures. The new AI contracts do not reference SPOT or FAST. They present the technology as novel.

The SBIR program's Congressional mandate requires that proposals be evaluated in part on their "potential for commercial application." This structural feature transforms state-funded surveillance R&D into a **commercialisation pipeline**: technologies developed with public money for DHS are designed from inception to become self-sustaining commercial products available to police departments, private security firms, foreign governments, and the private sector. Intellisense's airport surveillance proposal explicitly listed "retail analytics, and public space surveillance" as commercial applications. This mandate is not incidental to the program — it is the program's stated design since 1982.

The biometric contracts emerged in a specific operational context: ICE and CBP agents' gathering of visual and biometric data on protesters in Minneapolis, and the Columbus "surge" in which agents harvested biometric data during enforcement operations triggered by rightwing influencers' allegations of childcare fraud. The mobile biometric adapters would make such field-level data harvesting faster and more routine — eliminating the need for fixed infrastructure and allowing any agent with a phone to conduct biometric capture.

## So What

The SBIR pipeline's commercialisation mandate means that surveillance technologies developed for DHS do not remain within DHS. The three biometric contractors' proposals explicitly name distribution to the intelligence community, non-federal entities, and international partners. If the airport AI surveillance contracts progress to Phase II ($1M+ each), the resulting systems — trained on US airport CCTV — will be marketed to retail, transit, and public space operators. The predictive policing platform, if deployed, would centralise 911 data from 5,000+ call centres into a national dataset accessible to federal agencies.

**Watch items** (monitorable):
- Whether the Phase I biometric and airport AI contracts ($100K–175K range) receive Phase II funding ($1M+) within the next 12–18 months
- Whether Cassius LLC's Cimas platform is piloted in any 911 call centres and whether the resulting data is accessible to ICE/CBP
- Whether mobile biometric adapters appear in future ICE enforcement operations, particularly in cities with immigrant populations
- Whether any of the 7 May 2025 airport surveillance technologies are deployed at TSA checkpoints and whether racial disparities in flagging rates are tracked or disclosed
- Whether Congressional oversight hearings are convened on the post-inauguration contract clustering

## Circumventions

### Structural

**Transparency / Naming** — operating in this material. The leak itself is an instance: hacked data, provided through a transparency nonprofit, published by investigative journalism. The EPIC counsel's critique and the Brennan Center's "tech-washing" framing are additional naming acts. Outcome range: SPOT was formally terminated after GAO critique and ACLU profiling findings — but the underlying programme objective persisted and reappeared as AI-branded successors. This matches the documented failure mode of naming: formal termination without structural change (Church Committee: COINTELPRO). The 7 May 2025 contracts represent the structural condition surviving the naming of SPOT.

**Legal Challenge** — partially operating. EPIC's prior disclosures led to the FAST program's wind-down. The ACLU's profiling investigation produced documentation of racial targeting. However, neither produced binding constraints on successor programs. The legal challenge failure mode of "narrowed through interpretation" is relevant: each new technology is formally distinct from the challenged predecessor, requiring new legal challenge from scratch.

**Institutional Reform** — not operating. No institutional restructuring has prevented DHS from restarting behavioural surveillance under new technology labels. The SBIR program's Congressional mandate has not been amended to exclude surveillance commercialisation. The GAO's findings produced no statutory reform.

### Hegemonic

The **national_security_state** background — the assumption that DHS has legitimate surveillance needs requiring a continuous technology pipeline — is not contested in the material or by any actor within it. The EPIC counsel's critique ("dystopian science fiction") contests specific programs but not the institutional premise that the state should have surveillance capacity at this scale. A **counter-hegemonic construction** contesting this background would require alternative institutions that produce alternative "common sense" about the state's relationship to citizens — institutions that treat the surveillance architecture itself as the problem rather than its specific applications. The abolitionist critique of policing (which questions the institutional premise rather than its specific practices) is the closest existing model, but it has not been extended to federal surveillance infrastructure at scale.

The **technological_progress** background — the assumption that AI represents an improvement over prior methods — is also not contested. SPOT failed; the replacement is AI. The structural question (whether automated surveillance of airport passengers has any valid basis) is not asked. A hegemonic rupture in this background would require the AI systems to fail on their own terms — producing demonstrable harm or documented ineffectiveness at a scale that makes the "AI is better" assumption temporarily visible as contingent. The SPOT GAO findings represent a partial rupture that was absorbed through technological succession.

## Null case

**Initial null case** (stated before analysis): DHS is a legitimate security agency procuring technology through a long-standing, bipartisan, Congress-mandated program. The $845M over 20 years is modest relative to DHS's budget. Many contracts never advance past proof-of-concept. The leak reveals routine government procurement, not a surveillance state expansion.

**Post-analysis assessment**: The null case holds at the component level — individual SBIR contracts are routine procurement under established statutory authority. At the configurational level, the null case is weakened by several observations the routine-procurement explanation does not account for: the post-inauguration clustering of biometric and predictive-policing contracts; the commercialisation mandate that structurally ensures technologies spread beyond DHS; the repetition of discredited approaches (SPOT → AI successors) without reference to prior failure findings; the newly registered firm with no contracting history receiving a predictive-policing contract; and the explicit naming of international and intelligence-community distribution in biometric proposals.

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM at component level (individual contracts are routine SBIR). LOW-MEDIUM at configurational level (timing, clustering, commercialisation pipeline, and SPOT repetition form a pattern not explained by routine procurement).
