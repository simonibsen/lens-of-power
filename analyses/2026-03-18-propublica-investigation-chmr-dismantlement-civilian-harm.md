## Hegemonic context

Both the material and the analyst operate within the **national security state** background — the assumption that the US military conducts global operations as a matter of course, and that the question is how those operations are conducted (with or without civilian protections) rather than whether they should occur. The article's reform framework takes US military operations as given and asks for better guardrails within that frame. The CHMR programme itself embodies this assumption: it was designed to reduce harm from operations whose legitimacy it does not question. Maintaining forces include military professional culture (which naturalises global operations as "the mission"), the defence-industrial base (which profits from the strike volume the article documents), the classification system (which controls what the public knows about strike outcomes), and **Perpetual Threat as Perpetual Justification** — the post-9/11 "war on terror" has provided continuous justification for global military operations for over two decades.

## Narrative

The Civilian Harm Mitigation and Response (CHMR) programme, established in 2022 to reduce civilian casualties during US military operations, has been dismantled under Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth. Approximately 90% of the mission's personnel have been eliminated, including reducing Central Command's 10-person team to one. The Civilian Protection Center of Excellence, which cannot be formally closed without congressional approval, continues to exist on paper but has no mission, mandate, or budget. The top military lawyers (JAGs) who provided guidance on compliance with international law have been fired, described by Hegseth as "roadblocks" and "jagoffs." Authorisation levels for lethal force have been lowered, target categories broadened, threat assessments inflated, and inspectors general fired.

The consequences are quantified. US strikes worldwide under Trump's second term surpassed the total from all four years of Biden's presidency before the Iran campaign began. In Somalia, 125 strikes versus 21 under Biden, with fatalities rising from 189 to 359. A strike on a migrant detention centre in Yemen killed 61 African migrants. In the Iran campaign, 1,200+ civilians have been killed including nearly 200 children, according to the Human Rights Activists News Agency. The Minab school strike — 165+ dead, most under 12 — has been attributed to a US Tomahawk missile by Bellingcat through authenticated video and physical fragments. Trump, without providing evidence, told reporters the attack was "done by Iran."

The article documents the mechanism connecting institutional dismantlement to civilian deaths with a specificity the framework's prior analyses could only infer. The sequence is: Hegseth's "lethality" doctrine → CHMR personnel eliminated → JAGs fired → authorisation thresholds lowered → target categories broadened → inspectors general removed → strike volume increases across multiple theatres → mass civilian casualties. Each step removes a layer of the accountability architecture. The result is not that commanders are ordered to kill civilians — it is that the structural conditions that would prevent, detect, investigate, and learn from civilian casualties are systematically removed. Personnel is the mechanism: you do not need to change the policy if you eliminate the people who implement it.

The gap between formal commitments and operational reality is documented with unusual precision. During Senate confirmation hearings, Gen. Caine wrote that commanders "see positive impacts" from CHMR. Undersecretary Colby wrote that reducing civilian harm is "in the national interest." Vice Adm. Cooper committed to integrating the principles "as part of our culture." These formal commitments remain on the record while 90% of the programme's personnel have been eliminated. The confirmation testimony is the appearance; the personnel cuts are the operating reality.

The article documents a specific trajectory of institutional abdication. Adm. Bradley, described by CHMR adviser Bryant as "incredibly supportive" and "a model" for integrating civilian protection, later defended "double-tap" strikes — hitting survivors of a first strike — a practice legal experts say could violate laws of warfare. The shift from model adopter to double-tap defender traces the path of institutional accommodation: the professional who embraced accountability abandoned it when the institutional signals changed. Hegseth's doctrine, the JAG firings, and the CHMR gutting communicated what was expected. Bryant's attempt to save the programme by proposing to rename it the "Center for Precision Warfare" — repackaging civilian protection in language Hegseth would not consider "woke" — illustrates the depth of the narrative constraint: the substance of the programme was less threatening to the new order than its name.

## Circumventions

### Structural

**Transparency / Naming**: This ProPublica investigation is a structural circumvention — it names the dismantlement, quantifies its consequences, and provides documentary evidence (Bryant's briefing notes, Bellingcat authentication, casualty data). Bryant's own public criticism (Washington Post, Boston Globe, TV appearances) is a further instance. Observed outcome: Bryant was placed on leave, his security clearance threatened, and he was eventually forced to resign. The naming produced personal consequences for the namer without producing institutional change. Failure mode: **Real-Time Narrative Saturation** — the ongoing war generates continuous coverage that may overwhelm this specific investigation. The Trump administration's non-response (no comment from DoD or White House) is itself a technique: silence absorbs naming without engaging it.

**Institutional Reform**: The CHMR programme was itself an institutional reform — the product of a decade of advocacy, investigations, and political pressure following the Kunduz hospital strike (2015), the Raqqa campaign, and the Kabul aid worker strike (2021). Its dismantlement documents the fragility of institutional reform as a circumvention: reforms that depend on sustained political commitment are vulnerable to personnel replacement and reinterpretation, as documented in the circumventions framework's failure modes.

**Legal Challenge**: UN human rights experts have called for an investigation into whether the Minab strike violated international law. Amnesty International says the Yemen detention centre strike "qualifies as an indiscriminate attack and should be investigated as a war crime." Observed outcome: no investigation initiated. Failure mode: the US is not party to the International Criminal Court and holds a UN Security Council veto.

### Hegemonic

The article does not contest the background assumption that the US conducts global military operations as a matter of course. The reform framework accepts this assumption and asks for better guardrails — fewer civilian casualties, not fewer operations. A hegemonic circumvention would require asking whether the system that produces global strikes, across multiple theatres, with a volume that surpasses four years in one year, is itself the problem rather than the absence of guardrails within it. McChrystal's "insurgent math" gestures toward this but remains within the frame: the argument against killing civilians is that it creates more enemies, not that killing is wrong. The deepest hegemonic assumption — that the US has legitimate authority to conduct lethal operations in Somalia, Yemen, Syria, and Iran simultaneously — is not contested by the article, the sources, or the analyst.

## Null case

The non-power explanation: the CHMR programme was bureaucratic overhead that did not meaningfully reduce civilian casualties; the Trump administration's approach reflects a legitimate strategic preference for decisive military action; civilian casualties in war are inevitable regardless of institutional safeguards; the increased strike volume reflects genuine threat escalation (Al-Shabaab regrouping, IRGC operations) rather than lowered restraint.

**Plausibility: LOW.** The null case has minimal force. The article documents: (1) specific CHMR mechanisms (no-strike lists, civilian environment mapping, post-strike investigation) that were designed to prevent outcomes like Minab; (2) a quantified step-change in strike volume and fatalities that preceded the Iran campaign and therefore cannot be attributed to a single threat escalation; (3) the school in Minab was marked on maps since 2013 — a no-strike list would have included it; (4) former personnel state the safeguards "almost certainly would've reduced the number of noncombatants harmed"; (5) the dismantlement was institutional policy (Hegseth's lethality doctrine) not threat response. The argument that civilian protections have a "chilling effect" on commanders is stated in the article but does not explain why the institutional response was 90% elimination rather than reform. The null case does not account for the configuration of simultaneous changes (personnel cuts + JAG firings + lowered authorisation + IG removal) that collectively remove the accountability architecture.
