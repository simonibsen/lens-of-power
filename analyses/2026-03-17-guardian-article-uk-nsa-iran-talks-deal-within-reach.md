## Hegemonic context

This material and analysis operate within the **national security state** (the assumption that nuclear proliferation constitutes an existential threat requiring extraordinary state action and that the US has inherent interests in determining Iran's nuclear status). The article challenges the specific decision to attack but not the background assumption that the Non-Proliferation Treaty regime — which codifies asymmetric nuclear rights — is a neutral security arrangement rather than a power structure. Maintaining forces include the defence-industrial base (structural reproducer), the classification system (information asymmetry between state and public), and **Perpetual Threat as Perpetual Justification** (the Iranian nuclear programme has served as justification for sanctions, military posture, and now war for two decades). The **Asymmetry of Institutional Response Time** (Gramsci) operates here: military machinery mobilises faster than diplomatic processes can consolidate gains.

## Narrative

Britain's national security adviser, Jonathan Powell, attended the final US-Iran nuclear talks in Geneva and judged that Iran's offer was significant enough to prevent a rush to war. Powell brought his own technical team from the UK Cabinet Office because the US negotiating team — Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff — did not bring US technical experts. Nuclear experts subsequently described Witkoff's public pronouncements on Iran's nuclear programme as containing basic errors. Two days after the Geneva session ended, and after a date had been scheduled for a further round of technical talks in Vienna on 2 March, the US and Israel launched their military attack on Iran.

Iran's offer included elements that distinguished it from the 2015 JCPOA: a permanent agreement with no sunset clauses, down-blending of 400kg of highly enriched uranium under IAEA supervision inside Iran, a commitment to zero future stockpiling of HEU, and a three-to-five-year pause on domestic enrichment. The Omani mediator regarded the zero-stockpiling commitment as a breakthrough. In the afternoon session, after consulting Trump, the US demanded a ten-year enrichment pause — a gap the UK team considered bridgeable in the scheduled follow-up round. In return, nearly 80% of economic sanctions on Iran would have been lifted, and the US would have been offered participation in Iran's future civil nuclear programme.

The article provides three independent source categories confirming Powell's attendance and assessment: sources close to Powell, a former official briefed by participants, and Gulf diplomats. The UK subsequently regarded the attack as unlawful and premature, having seen no compelling evidence of an imminent Iranian missile threat to Europe or of Iran securing a nuclear weapon. This inside knowledge explains the UK government's reluctance to support the US attack — a reluctance that has placed the UK-US relationship under what the article describes as unprecedented strain, with Trump repeatedly criticising Starmer.

The US negotiating team's composition is structurally significant. Kushner, whose Affinity Partners has solicited over $5 billion from the governments he negotiates with as Middle East envoy (documented in a prior analysis), and Witkoff, whose nuclear pronouncements contained basic errors, were the sole US representatives at a negotiation on nuclear weapons capability. One diplomat with knowledge of the talks described them as "Israeli assets that dragged a president into a war he wants to get out of." Whether or not this characterisation is accurate, the structural observation holds: the US negotiating team was not configured to produce a diplomatic outcome. The institutional function of competent negotiation was either abdicated or redirected.

The two-day gap between the end of the Geneva talks and the military attack — with the Vienna follow-up already scheduled — is the article's most structurally significant disclosure. If the US regarded Iran's offer as insufficient, the institutional response would have been to attend the Vienna talks and press for better terms. Instead, the military option pre-empted the diplomatic one. The pattern is consistent with the framework's prior observation (Day 1 liveblog analysis) that Iran's foreign minister claimed the US "attacked during active negotiations twice in twelve months." The Geneva disclosure provides western institutional corroboration of that claim.

## So What

The diplomatic process was not exhausted; it was terminated. If the UK's assessment is accurate — and it is sourced to a participant with technical capacity the US team lacked — the stated justification for the attack (Iran's nuclear programme) was being addressed through negotiation at the moment force was applied. The gap between stated purpose and structural function widens with each disclosure.

## Null case

The non-power explanation: the US genuinely assessed that Iran's 3-5 year enrichment-pause offer was insufficient, that intelligence indicated an imminent threat justifying pre-emption of the Vienna talks, and that Kushner and Witkoff's lack of technical expertise reflects administrative style rather than structural failure.

**Plausibility: LOW-MEDIUM.** The null case has some force for the enrichment-pause gap (a genuine disagreement between 3 and 10 years). But it is weakened by five structural factors operating simultaneously: (1) the US did not bring a technical team, inconsistent with genuinely seeking a technical resolution; (2) the attack came two days before scheduled follow-up talks, inconsistent with allowing diplomacy to run its course; (3) the UK, with inside access, concluded the attack was unlawful and premature; (4) the Omani mediator judged a deal was within reach; (5) the war's stated objectives have shifted four times (documented in `2026-03-13-guardian-article-iran-war-no-clear-goal.md`). The null case would require all five indicators to be misleading simultaneously.
