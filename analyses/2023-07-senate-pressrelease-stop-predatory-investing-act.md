## Hegemonic context

Both the material and the analyst operate within the **property rights** and **market capitalism** hegemonic backgrounds. The property rights background makes private ownership of housing the natural condition — the press release contests who owns, not whether housing should be treated as an investment asset. The market capitalism background makes tax incentives the natural terrain of intervention — the bill removes a specific tax advantage rather than questioning whether housing markets should be structured to generate returns on capital. The maintaining forces include the real estate industry's lobbying infrastructure (NAR, NMHC, corporate landlord trade groups), the tax code's structural subsidisation of capital over labour, and the financial sector's development of single-family rental securitisation instruments that converted a fragmented market into an institutional asset class.

MAINTAINING FORCES:
  Architects: Financial institutions that developed single-family rental securitisation post-2008; private equity firms (Invitation Homes, American Homes 4 Rent) that built the institutional landlord model
  Structural reproducers: Tax code provisions (interest deductibility, depreciation) that subsidise leveraged acquisition; real estate and financial industry lobbying; Senate committee procedures that allow bills to die without a vote
  Feedback mechanisms: Wealth Defense Industry — real estate and financial lobbying prevents committee action; Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence — democratic process produces the bill; oligarchic mechanism prevents the vote; Passive Revolution — the bill's existence serves as evidence that "the system is working on it" while the structural arrangement persists

## Narrative

Eight Democratic senators introduced the Stop Predatory Investing Act in July 2023, proposing to prohibit investors who own fifty or more single-family rental homes from deducting mortgage interest and depreciation — the two principal tax advantages that make leveraged residential acquisition profitable at scale. The bill operates through the tax code: it does not prohibit institutional ownership but removes the subsidy that makes it financially attractive. The mechanism is precise and the data cited is stark. In Cleveland, 16% of homes were purchased by investors, rising to 70% in one zip code. In Cincinnati, the figure reached 50% in some communities. Two large investors owned more than 12,000 homes across three Ohio markets. One company purchased 29 homes on a single street.

The press release describes a functioning democratic process. Senators identified a documented problem — institutional investors purchasing single-family homes at scale, converting them from ownership to rental, and driving up prices. The Senate Banking Committee had held hearings on the issue in 2021 and 2022. A coalition of housing advocacy organisations supported the bill. The mechanism proposed — removing tax advantages rather than imposing prohibitions — is a moderate, market-compatible intervention. This is what representative democracy is supposed to do: identify a problem, build a coalition, propose a solution, advance legislation.

The bill never received a committee vote. S.2224 died in the 118th Congress without committee action. Reintroduced as S.969 in the 119th Congress, it has again received no committee vote. The democratic process produced the bill; the institutional process prevented the vote. This is the structural terrain the analysis must examine — not whether the legislators acted in good faith (they appear to have done so) but whether the system within which they acted is configured to permit the outcome their bill seeks.

The most analytically significant feature of the press release is what it does not contain. Academic research — An (2023), the Georgia Tech study — documents that institutional investors are twice as likely to purchase in Black neighbourhoods, that Black families lost $4 billion in home equity over the period studied, that Black homeownership dropped 4.2 percentage points while white homeownership was unaffected, and that investor purchases in Black neighbourhoods correlate with declining property values, increased crime, and reduced political participation. The racial targeting dimension is entirely absent from the bill and the press release. The senators name Cleveland zip codes and Ohio markets but do not name the racial distribution of the harm. This is a significant Axiom 8 signal — the omission of race from a bill addressing a phenomenon that academic research documents as racially structured may reflect political calculation (naming race alienates swing voters), genuine blind spot, or the hegemonic background in which housing is understood through markets and investment rather than through racial political economy. All three explanations may be simultaneously operative.

## So What

The analysis documents a structural configuration in which the democratic process functions at the proposal stage but is blocked at the decision stage. The bill exists; the vote does not. This is not dysfunction — it is the Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence pattern operating as described: democratic institutions handle the visible work of governance (hearings, bill introductions, coalition building) while oligarchic mechanisms handle the substantive outcome (preventing the vote that would alter the tax subsidy).

Watch items:
- Whether S.969 receives a committee vote in the 119th Congress — would test whether the blocking mechanism is durable or contingent on Senate composition
- Whether institutional investor acquisition of single-family homes accelerates, stabilises, or reverses in the absence of legislative action — tests whether the market self-corrects or requires intervention
- Whether the racial targeting dimension enters the legislative record — its continued absence would confirm the Axiom 8 signal as structural rather than incidental
- Whether the single-family rental securitisation market expands — would indicate whether financial engineering is outpacing legislative response
- Whether any Republican co-sponsors emerge — would test whether the bill's failure is partisan or structural (if bipartisan support exists and the bill still fails, the blocking mechanism is institutional rather than ideological)

## Circumventions

### Structural

**Electoral / Democratic**: The bill itself constitutes a democratic circumvention attempt — legislators using the legislative process to contest an economic arrangement. Observed outcome: the attempt produced a bill but not a vote. The power response matches the documented pattern for Electoral / Democratic circumventions: procedural obstruction (no committee vote), which prevents the democratic mechanism from completing its cycle. The bill's existence may itself function as a pressure mechanism (threat of future action) but two years without a vote suggests the threat has been absorbed.

**Legal Challenge**: Tax code changes require legislation, foreclosing the litigation route. The advocacy coalition (NLIHC, Americans for Financial Reform, Enterprise, LISC, NCST, NHLP, NHRC) operates through the legislative channel, not the judicial one. Fair housing litigation against racially targeted acquisition patterns could constitute a separate legal challenge, but the press release does not frame the issue in terms that would support such litigation — the racial dimension is absent.

**Transparency / Naming**: The press release performs a naming function — "predatory investing," "drive up housing prices," "buy up local homes." The language names the mechanism (institutional investors acquiring housing stock) and the harm (price increases, reduced homeownership). The naming is partial: it names the economic mechanism but not the racial targeting that academic research documents. Failure mode: the naming may be captured by the appearance of democratic action — "we introduced a bill" serves as evidence of responsiveness without producing change.

### Hegemonic

The bill operates entirely within the market capitalism and property rights hegemonic backgrounds. It contests who profits from housing markets, not whether housing should be structured as a market. A hegemonic circumvention would require contesting the background assumption that housing is properly an investment asset — building alternative institutions (community land trusts, social housing, decommodified tenure) that operate outside the ownership-rental binary the bill accepts. Counter-hegemonic construction in housing has been observed at small scale (community land trusts in Burlington, Vienna's social housing model) but has not achieved the institutional density required to shift common sense at the national level in the United States. The bill's limitation — removing a tax subsidy rather than questioning the asset model — is not a failure of the legislators' ambition but a reflection of the hegemonic boundary within which housing policy operates. The alternative (decommodified housing) is not politically unthinkable — it exists in other countries — but it is outside the range of proposals the American legislative process can currently process.

## Null case

**Initial null case**: This is the democratic system functioning normally. Senators identify a problem, hold hearings, build a coalition, introduce legislation. The bill has not received a vote because the legislative process is slow, committees have competing priorities, and bipartisan support has not been secured. The racial dimension is absent because the senators chose a universal framing strategy (all homebuyers are harmed, not just Black homebuyers) that they judged more likely to succeed. No power dynamics explanation is required.

**Assessment**: The null case has genuine force at the component level. Legislative delay is real — committees have limited floor time, and many bills die without a vote. Universal framing is a legitimate political strategy with its own logic. The bill's eight Democratic sponsors reflect a lack of bipartisan support that would make committee action more likely.

The null case weakens at the configurational level. The bill addresses the tax subsidy that makes institutional residential acquisition profitable — the interest deductibility and depreciation provisions are not minor regulatory details but structural features of the tax code that channel capital toward leveraged acquisition. The actors who benefit from these provisions (institutional investors, the real estate industry, financial institutions that securitise single-family rental portfolios) have documented lobbying capacity that the Wealth Defense Industry pattern describes. The bill's failure to receive a committee vote — not a floor vote, not a veto, but a committee vote — is consistent with the procedural obstruction mechanism documented in Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence: democratic institutions produce the bill; institutional mechanisms prevent the decision.

The racial dimension adds analytical weight. The omission of race from a bill addressing a racially structured phenomenon is not explained by legislative delay or committee priorities. It is either a strategic calculation (naming race is politically costly) or a genuine blind spot. Either explanation involves a power mechanism: the first reflects the electoral cost of naming racial harm in American politics (a Thought & Narrative layer constraint); the second reflects the hegemonic background in which housing is understood through markets rather than through racial political economy.

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM-HIGH at component level (each element — legislative delay, universal framing, lack of bipartisan support — has a non-power explanation). MEDIUM at configurational level (the combination of no committee vote + documented lobbying capacity of beneficiaries + absent racial dimension exceeds what legislative inertia alone explains, but does not conclusively demonstrate that power dynamics are the primary explanation). The null case is plausible but incomplete — it explains the delay but not the directionality (why the delay consistently favours the actors who benefit from the existing tax subsidy).

**IC-5 note**: Training data likely overrepresents the institutional-legislative perspective on this material — the view from the committee room rather than from the neighbourhoods where investor purchases concentrate. Perspectives of renters in institutionally owned single-family homes, of families priced out of homeownership, and of Black communities experiencing the documented targeting are absent from both the source material and likely underrepresented in training data. Their inclusion would not change the null case assessment but would deepen the harm analysis and sharpen the Axiom 8 finding.
