## Narrative

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, Public Law 119-21), signed July 4, 2025, contains Medicaid provisions that RAND estimates will reduce state Medicaid budgets by $664 billion through 2034, produce federal savings of $714 billion, and result in 7.6 million fewer Medicaid enrollees by 2034. Work requirements account for approximately $350 billion of the reduction; provider tax restrictions account for approximately $280 billion. The impacts are geographically asymmetric: Medicaid expansion states with substantial use of state-directed payments and provider taxes — Arizona, Iowa, and Nevada — face reductions exceeding 15% of their Medicaid budgets. California and New York see the largest absolute reductions (~$112 billion and ~$63 billion respectively). Florida, as a non-expansion state, sees minimal impact (<0.5%).

The same reconciliation bill that cuts $664 billion from Medicaid also cuts $4.5 trillion in tax revenue over a decade. CBO and JCT distributional analysis estimates that $2.3 trillion of the tax provisions deliver most of their benefits to the top 10% of earners by income. The richest 10% receive tax cuts exceeding $14,700 per household per year; the richest 1% receive cuts exceeding $50,000 per year. Lower-income filers with little or no tax liability do not benefit from the tax provisions. The average tax cut across all filers is $611.

The Medicaid provisions do not repeal coverage. They create procedural mechanisms — work requirements for non-disabled adults, increased frequency and stringency of eligibility redeterminations, immigration status verification — that produce disenrollment through compliance failure. Coverage formally exists; the pathway to it is obstructed by bureaucratic friction. This is a procedural variant of manufactured scarcity: the scarcity is in eligibility, not in funds.

The RAND report and the CBO distributional analysis are published as separate institutional products. No single institutional analysis in the source material connects the Medicaid cuts to the tax cuts as components of a single redistributive transfer. The structural relationship — that both provisions operate within the same reconciliation vehicle — is visible only when the analyses are juxtaposed. The institutional separation of these analyses prevents synthesis at the point of publication.

The bill's title — "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" — applies the word "beautiful" to legislation that removes 7.6 million people from health insurance while delivering $2.3 trillion to the top income decile. The framing language throughout the institutional analyses is clinical: "policy changes that aim to reduce federal spending and improve program integrity." The phrase "improve program integrity" frames coverage reduction as fraud prevention, implicitly positioning enrollees who fail to meet work requirements or redetermination deadlines as program-integrity problems rather than beneficiaries.

## Findings

1. **The OBBBA produces a directional distributive transfer within a single legislative vehicle.** $664 billion is removed from healthcare for low-income populations (via Medicaid cuts); $2.3 trillion is delivered to the top 10% of earners (via tax provisions). Both operate within the same reconciliation bill. The democratic process (Congressional votes, presidential signature) handles the procedure; the distributional asymmetry is the substance. 4. **"Program integrity" inverts the stated purpose.** The framing characterises coverage reduction as fraud prevention. Enrollees who fail to navigate work requirements or redetermination deadlines are implicitly positioned as integrity problems rather than beneficiaries of a healthcare programme. 5. **The health consequences of disenrollment are absent from the institutional analyses.** 7.6 million people losing health insurance is reported as a fiscal metric. The mortality and morbidity implications — academic literature estimates 1 excess death per 800-2,000 people losing Medicaid coverage, implying 3,800-9,500 excess deaths — are not computed in any of the source analyses. 6. **Geographic asymmetry correlates with Medicaid expansion decisions.** States that expanded Medicaid under the ACA face the largest budget reductions. States that did not expand (e.g., Florida, <0.5% impact) are substantially shielded. The provision's design penalises states that previously chose to extend coverage, creating a retrospective cost for a prior policy decision. ## Implications

The OBBBA is the legislative instantiation of Democracy-Oligarchy Coexistence at scale. The democratic process produces the bill; the distributional substance is oligarchic. The bill's structure — healthcare cuts for the bottom funding tax cuts for the top — is not hidden. CBO publishes the distributional analysis. RAND publishes the Medicaid impact. The mechanism is visible (Axiom 3, second mode — visible mechanism, hidden contingency): the population can see the bill, but its contingency (that the distribution could be otherwise) is obscured by the framing of fiscal necessity and program integrity.

The procedural scarcity variant should be monitored. If bureaucratic friction (documentation requirements, redetermination frequency, work verification) produces disenrollment rates exceeding the RAND projections, the mechanism is operating more aggressively than the policy modelling suggests. States with weaker administrative capacity will produce higher disenrollment rates for structural rather than programmatic reasons.

## Watch

- Actual disenrollment rates vs. RAND projections — do they exceed estimates?
- Whether the $714B in federal savings is redirected to further tax provisions or debt service
- State-level responses: do expansion states reduce other services to compensate, or reverse expansion?
- Health outcome data for disenrolled populations (excess mortality, ER utilisation)
- Whether the procedural scarcity mechanism is extended to other programmes (SNAP, housing assistance)

## Null case

**Initial null case**: Budget reconciliation is standard legislative procedure. Work requirements exist in SNAP and TANF. The bill includes provisions benefiting middle-income earners (tip deduction, overtime deduction, child tax credit). Distributional asymmetry is a feature of most legislation, not evidence of power dynamics.

**Assessment**: The initial null case has been partially weakened. Individual provisions are conventional (work requirements have precedent; reconciliation is standard procedure). The configurational character — $664B from the bottom, $2.3T to the top 10%, within a single vehicle — is not explained by the null case of "necessary trade-offs." The trade-offs flow directionally. The middle-income benefits ($611 average) are modest relative to the top-decile benefits ($14,700+). However, the null case retains force for the claim that the configuration was assembled rather than designed — reconciliation bills are omnibus by nature.

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM — individual provisions are conventional; the distributive configuration is asymmetric in a way the null case does not fully explain.

**IC-5 flag**: Training data likely overrepresents policy-professional and institutional perspectives on Medicaid reform. The perspectives of the 7.6 million people projected to lose coverage are almost certainly underrepresented. The clinical framing of institutional analyses (RAND, CBO) may be treated as the natural register for discussing these consequences, normalising the absence of health-outcome projections and enrollee perspectives.
