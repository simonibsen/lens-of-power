## Hegemonic context

Both the material and the analyst operate within **technological progress** — the assumption that technological capability implies deployment right, that the burden of proof falls on those who object rather than those who alter, and that commercial applications of new technology are a form of innovation that benefits society unless proven otherwise. The secondary context is **property rights** — specifically the emerging question of whether the night sky, darkness itself, and the light-dark cycle that has organized biological life for billions of years are resources that can be enclosed by private actors through unilateral deployment. Maintaining forces include the FCC's mandate (structural reproducer: a regulatory body designed for radio spectrum allocation, not planetary environmental governance, naturalises the treatment of satellite deployment as a licensing question rather than an environmental one), the technology industry's framing apparatus (architect: the presentation of every new capability as a "service" that responds to "demand"), and the assumption shared by regulator, applicant, and much of the public that technological deployment is the default and restriction requires justification (feedback mechanism: objections are processed as obstacles to innovation rather than as competing claims about the commons).

## Narrative

Two proposals before the US Federal Communications Commission would, if deployed at scale, alter the night-dark cycle at planetary scope. Reflect Orbital proposes satellites with reflective mirrors that redirect sunlight on demand, producing ground illumination adjustable "from full moon to full noon" across 5-6 kilometre areas. SpaceX proposes up to one million satellites for a solar-powered orbital AI computing network, intended to reduce the energy and cooling demands of terrestrial data centres. Neither proposal is before an environmental regulator. Both are before the FCC — a body whose jurisdiction covers radio frequencies and orbital slots, not sky brightness, circadian biology, or ecosystem integrity.

The scientific response is remarkable for its institutional weight and its language. The presidents of four international chronobiology societies representing approximately 2,500 researchers across more than 30 countries describe "significant alteration of the natural night-time light environment at a planetary scale." Five sleep research society presidents characterise circadian disruption as "a physiological mechanism driving major adverse health consequences" rather than an inconvenience. Scientists explicitly compare the scale of the intervention to climate change and ocean acidification: "The alternation of light and dark is not a trivial background condition. It is one of the oldest organising principles of life on Earth." The framing is not precautionary hedging. It is a categorical claim that what is being proposed is a transformation of a planetary system, advanced through a regulatory process designed for a different category of decision entirely.

The scale mismatch between what is proposed and the forum in which it is proposed constitutes the central structural finding. The FCC has no framework for assessing planetary-scale alteration of the night-dark cycle. It evaluates whether a satellite will interfere with other radio services, whether its orbital parameters are safe, whether it meets debris mitigation guidelines. It does not evaluate — because it was never designed to evaluate — whether the aggregate effect of satellite deployment will alter the light environment for every organism on Earth. The regulatory void is not an accident of bureaucratic oversight. It is a structural feature: the technology outpaced the categories. The unprecedented nature of the proposal is not merely descriptive. It is the operating environment.

Reflect Orbital's framing deserves precise attention. The company describes a service: targeted illumination "only to locations approved by local authorities," with applications in solar energy extension, construction lighting, disaster response, and agriculture. The scientists describe a planetary transformation: brightness effects that are global, cumulative, and cannot be escaped by moving to a more remote location. Both descriptions are accurate — at different scales. The company describes the product; the scientists describe the externality. The 5-6 kilometre illumination zone is the product. The planetary alteration of sky brightness is the structural consequence. The framing as "service to local authorities" is not false; it is a scale-of-description choice that renders the planetary dimension invisible within the regulatory filing. Reflect Orbital declined to comment for the article. SpaceX did not respond.

The existing baseline is already degraded. DarkSky International reports that satellites have already increased diffuse sky brightness by approximately 10 percent. Kocifaj of the Slovak Academy of Sciences quantifies current artificial sky brightness at 3-8 microcandela per square metre, projected to reach 5-19 microcandela by 2035. The effect, Kocifaj notes, "is global and cannot be escaped by moving to a more remote location." This is the condition into which the two proposals would be deployed — not a pristine baseline but an already-altered commons whose alteration has proceeded without authorisation, without assessment, and without any institution claiming jurisdiction over the cumulative outcome.

## So What

This analysis documents the first environmental-domain case in the framework and populates the technological_progress hegemonic context in a non-AI domain. The structural question it surfaces is jurisdictional: who has authority to decide whether a planetary-scale environmental system is altered, and through what process? The FCC proceeding is functioning as designed — companies file, scientists comment, the regulator evaluates within its statutory mandate. The structural problem is that the statutory mandate does not encompass the scope of the decision being made.

Watch items:
- Whether the FCC evaluates sky brightness, circadian disruption, or ecosystem effects in its assessment of these applications — or whether it confines its evaluation to radio interference and orbital safety, leaving the planetary-scale questions unaddressed because no statute assigns them to any regulator
- Whether Reflect Orbital deploys any satellites before a regulatory framework for sky brightness assessment exists — the Dispossession Cycle predicts deployment before regulation, habituation before assessment
- Whether the scientific opposition (9 society presidents, 2,500+ researchers, 30+ countries) translates into regulatory action or is absorbed as public comment within a proceeding that lacks the statutory tools to act on the substance of the objection
- Whether the SpaceX orbital AI computing network proposal is evaluated in conjunction with the company's existing Starlink constellation or treated as a separate filing — cumulative assessment versus per-project review determines whether the aggregate effect is ever evaluated
- Whether any jurisdiction (national, international, or treaty body) claims authority over the light-dark cycle as a commons requiring protection — the absence of such a claim is itself the structural condition that permits unilateral alteration
- Whether "darkness" begins to acquire economic value as a scarce resource — real estate markets, tourism, agricultural productivity — creating a constituency for protection through property logic rather than ecological logic

## Circumventions

### Structural

**Transparency / Naming**: The Guardian article, with its institutional scientific sourcing (nine society presidents, quantified brightness data, explicit comparison to climate change), constitutes a high-quality naming operation. The article makes visible the scale mismatch between what is proposed (planetary alteration) and the forum in which it is proposed (FCC filing). Observed limitation: naming has been observed to be least effective against processes that can absorb it — if the FCC lacks statutory authority to act on the substance of the scientific objection, the naming may produce awareness without institutional response.

**Legal Challenge**: The absence of a regulatory framework is itself the structural obstacle to legal challenge. Environmental law requires a jurisdictional hook — a statute that assigns authority to a regulator and defines the scope of review. If no statute assigns sky brightness to any regulator, there may be no legal basis for challenge within existing law. The National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) could in principle require environmental impact assessment for federal actions significantly affecting the environment, but FCC licensing may not trigger NEPA review at the scope required, and the current administration has been narrowing NEPA application (documented in the Big AI State analysis). International environmental law (the Outer Space Treaty, the Moon Agreement) does not address cumulative sky brightness from commercial satellites.

**Institutional Reform**: The structural circumvention most directly relevant — the creation of regulatory authority over sky brightness and the light-dark cycle as a protected commons. This would require legislative action to assign jurisdiction, define standards, and mandate cumulative assessment. No such legislation has been proposed. The temporal dynamics of the Dispossession Cycle (deploy first, regulate later) suggest that regulatory frameworks will develop after deployment, not before.

### Hegemonic

The **technological progress** assumption structures the entire proceeding: the satellite proposals are filed as applications for permission to deploy a technology, not as requests for permission to alter a planetary system. The regulatory process assumes that deployment is the default and restriction requires justification. A hegemonic circumvention would require inverting this assumption — establishing that the light-dark cycle is a commons whose alteration requires justification, that the burden of proof falls on the party proposing alteration rather than the party objecting, and that "can we build it?" is not the same question as "should we deploy it?" This is a precautionary principle at planetary scale, which the technological_progress hegemonic context treats as an obstacle to innovation rather than as a condition of responsible deployment. The generational sedimentation is deep: the assumption that technological capability implies deployment right has been constructed over centuries of industrial development and is reproduced through patent law, venture capital structures, regulatory frameworks designed for approval rather than assessment, and a cultural narrative that equates technological progress with human progress.

The **property rights** context surfaces an emerging hegemonic question: if a company can sell illumination on demand, darkness becomes an enclosed resource — not through restriction of access but through unilateral elimination. The enclosure of darkness does not follow the historical pattern of enclosure (fencing off common land). It follows the pollution pattern (degrading a commons until it ceases to function). But unlike pollution, which typically has identifiable local sources and local effects, the alteration of the night sky is global, cumulative, and irreversible at any given point in time. Contesting this at the hegemonic level would require establishing darkness — the absence of artificial light at planetary scale — as a commons with standing, which no legal or institutional framework currently provides.

## Null case

**Procedural level: MEDIUM-HIGH.** The FCC process is functioning as designed. Companies file applications. Scientists submit public comments. The regulator evaluates within its statutory mandate. This is bureaucratic normalcy, not concealment. The FCC did not create the jurisdictional gap — Congress never assigned sky brightness to any regulator because the technology to alter it at planetary scale did not exist until recently.

**Substantive level: MEDIUM.** The scientific claims about circadian disruption and ecosystem effects are based on established chronobiology, but the specific effects of orbital mirror deployment at scale have not been empirically measured because the deployment has not occurred. The nine society presidents represent institutional scientific consensus, but their projections are modelled rather than observed. Reflect Orbital's stated intention to illuminate "only approved locations" could, in principle, be technically enforced through beam targeting — the global brightness effect may be less than the scientists project if deployment is limited.

**Structural level: LOW.** The null case does not explain why a decision with planetary-scale environmental consequences is being processed through a spectrum-licensing body with no environmental mandate. The procedural normalcy is the structural finding — the system is working exactly as designed, and what it is designed to do does not encompass what is at stake. The absence of a regulatory framework for assessing cumulative sky brightness is not explained by bureaucratic oversight; it is a structural feature of a regulatory architecture built before the technology existed.

IC-5 CHECK: Training data likely overrepresents the technology-industry perspective on orbital deployment (SpaceX/Starlink coverage, space industry optimism) and Western regulatory frameworks (FCC proceedings, US environmental law). Perspectives structurally underrepresented: Global South populations and equatorial communities with greatest satellite visibility; indigenous astronomical traditions for which the night sky carries cultural and spiritual significance beyond the utilitarian frame; non-human interests (nocturnal species, phytoplankton dependent on light-dark cycles, migratory birds that navigate by starlight) which have no representation in any regulatory process. The framework's own layer taxonomy — designed around human power relations — has structural difficulty seeing non-human stakes except as externalities of human arrangements.
