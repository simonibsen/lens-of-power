## Narrative

The African Union adopted a motion, put forward by Ghana, to label slavery and colonialism as crimes against humanity. The motion is scheduled for the UN this month. The AU declared 2025 the year of reparations and subsequently extended the timeline to a Decade of Reparations (2026–2036). Ghana has established an Office for Global Reparations. This institutional push has a 30-year history: the Abuja Proclamation of 1993 first demanded reparations for colonialism and the transatlantic slave trade. The article covers the movement through the Wakati Wetu ("Our Time") festival in Nairobi, which aims to use arts and culture to build momentum for reparative justice.

The structural analysis identifies the word "reparations" itself as the contested terrain. Germany in 2011 recognised the colonial-era genocide of more than 70,000 Herero and Nama peoples in Namibia and offered €1.1bn — but explicitly refused to call the payment "reparations," citing the risk of legal precedent. France discussed the 1899 Niger massacre but refused to acknowledge responsibility. The former colonial powers can pay; the resistance is to the category. The word "reparations" would create a legal framework that transforms voluntary generosity into obligatory redress, converting **Constitutive Violence** (the pattern where foundational acts of force are converted into property, law, and custom) from an invisible substrate into a named liability. Germany's semantic strategy instantiates **Appearance as Structural Terrain** at the state level: the €1.1bn maintains the appearance of magnanimity while the refusal to name it "reparations" preserves the structural arrangement.

The reparations movement deploys **Ideological Judo** — holding former colonial powers to their own stated standards of human rights, rule of law, and justice. Germany's explicit avoidance of the "reparations" label confirms that the judo mechanism has leverage: the gap between the legitimating ideology (human rights) and the actual practice (refusal to acknowledge liability) is precisely the terrain the movement exploits. The "crimes against humanity" classification at the UN is the legal formalisation of this judo — attempting to convert moral leverage into binding obligation.

The arts and culture strategy represents an extension of the **Cultural Production** circumvention type beyond the preservation of hidden transcripts toward active institutional deployment. Kyeretwie Osei of the AU's civil society organ described this explicitly: "You need pressure points from both sides. We need everyone on board and so people in the creative sector are really important in constructing the necessary narrative." The cultural strategy is instrumentalised — not hidden resistance but deliberate narrative construction aimed at institutional change. Yvonne Adhiambo Owuor's call for "moral autopsy before moral exorcism" and her warning against an "unseemly rush" into restitution suggests the movement is operating in what Fanon described as the synthesis phase of **The Three Phases of Cultural Resistance**: creating new frameworks for understanding rather than merely inverting colonial categories.

The article does not address the **Relay Class** risk — that reparations flowing through state institutions may reproduce extraction under a new flag. The low turnout of African heads of state at the September 2025 Addis Ababa summit, combined with Ghana's positioning as the reparations champion, suggests that some states see reparations as a political instrument (Ghana has declared the diaspora its "13th ethnic group") while others treat it as diplomatically peripheral. The Herero and Nama descendants' exclusion from the Germany-Namibia negotiations — and their characterisation of the €1.1bn as insufficient to cover the loss of life, land, and livestock — demonstrates that the gap between state-level reparations diplomacy and community-level redress is already operative. Former colonial powers hold the colonial-era archives necessary for what Owuor calls the "historical audit," creating an information asymmetry at the **Surveillance & Information** layer that the article does not address.

## Implications

Germany's semantic strategy reveals that former colonial powers have already conceded the moral argument — they are defending the legal and economic terrain. The analytical question is whether the "crimes against humanity" classification can convert moral leverage into binding obligation, or whether it will be absorbed by the institution it targets (the UN), producing formal recognition without material consequence.

The arts/culture strategy may prove more durable than the legal strategy. Legal classifications can be absorbed, but cultural frameworks that make colonial extraction visible and contingent are harder to neutralise — they operate at Axiom 3 (making the contingent visible) in a way that institutional diplomacy does not.

The Relay Class risk should be monitored. Ghana's positioning of the diaspora as its "13th ethnic group" is instrumentally clever but creates a channel through which reparations claims could be captured by state interests.

## Null case

**Initial null case**: The AU's reparations push is primarily institutional performance — a mechanism for African leaders to project moral authority without expecting material outcomes. European reluctance is pragmatic (liability precedent), not power-protective.

**Assessment**: The null case has been partially weakened. However, the null case retains force for some elements: the Decade declaration and cultural festival may be more aspirational than operational, and the low head-of-state turnout at Addis Ababa suggests uneven commitment within the AU itself.

**Null case plausibility**: MEDIUM — plausible for some elements (Decade declaration, festival), less plausible for others (legal strategy, Germany's word-avoidance). The power-dynamics reading adds analytical value by identifying specific mechanisms but does not entirely displace the null case.
