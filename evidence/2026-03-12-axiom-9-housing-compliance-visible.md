# Axiom 9 complication: housing compliance with visible coercion

## Content

Axiom 9 states: "Compliance can be manufactured without explicit
coercion." The axiom emphasizes that "the absence of visible force
does not indicate the absence of control." Tenants in Desmond's
*Evicted* complicate this formulation in the same way Terkel's workers
did: they are not subjects of invisible coercion. They see the eviction
threat clearly, name the landlord's incentives, understand the court's
function, and comply because the alternative — homelessness — is
prohibitive, not because the coercion is concealed.

The eviction process is explicitly coercive: the landlord files a legal
action, the court issues a judgment, the sheriff executes a physical
removal. None of this is hidden. The compliance is manufactured through
structural constraint (no affordable alternatives, no legal
representation, no savings buffer) rather than through concealment of
the mechanism.

This is the third IC-1 complication of Axiom 9 from a Position 2
source, extending the pattern from the workplace (Terkel) to housing.
In both domains, the governed see the mechanism clearly and comply
because the cost of refusal is prohibitive.

## Significance

Third Position 2 complication of Axiom 9. The pattern is consistent
across domains: workplace compliance (Terkel) and housing compliance
(Desmond) both operate through visible constraint, not invisible
coercion. Axiom 9's emphasis on "without the appearance of coercion"
may describe elite/middle-class compliance mechanisms (narrative
control, manufactured consent) more accurately than it describes the
experience of the poor, who face visible, explicit, structural coercion.
