## Facts

- Intel: $8.9 billion investment from restructured CHIPS Act funds for 9.9%
  equity stake; no board seats
- MP Materials: $400 million DoD investment for 15% stake; DoD becomes
  largest shareholder; offtake agreements at double market rate for ten years
- Vulcan Elements: $670 million ($50M Commerce + $620M DoD); $50M equity
  and future stock purchase rights
- Lithium Americas: $182 million deferred debt for 5% stake
- ReElement Technologies: $80 million DoD investment; future stock purchase
  rights
- The AI Action Plan explicitly rejects "extraneous policy requirements"
  — a category that includes CHIPS Act worker protections (childcare mandates)
  — characterizing them as "sweeping ideological agendas"
- The failed Sanders/Warren CHIPS amendment would have authorized equity
  "for the benefit of taxpayers, in equity appreciation in the case of a
  warrant or other equity interest, or a reasonable interest rate premium,
  in the case of a debt instrument" — this alternative was rejected
- Six independent legal experts cited in the brief concluded the Intel
  CHIPS restructuring "departs from the text and spirit of the CHIPS Act"
- The government acknowledged it may influence firms by merely threatening
  stake sales, even without formal governance mechanisms

## Significance

This is primary-source documentation of the structural mechanism by which
democratic state investment and oligarchic outcomes coexist: the state
invests public capital at scale ($10+ billion) while explicitly and
deliberately declining every governance mechanism (board seats, worker
protections, environmental covenants) that would convert the investment
into public accountability.

The mechanism is documented at three levels:
1. The fact: multi-billion dollar equity without governance rights
2. The justification: the AI Action Plan's explicit framing of governance
   as "extraneous" and worker protections as "ideological agendas"
3. The rejected alternative: the Sanders/Warren proposal that would have
   structured equity for public benefit

The combination provides unusually complete documentation of Democracy-
Oligarchy Coexistence — not as abstract structural observation (Winters,
Piketty) but as a concrete policy mechanism with traceable actors,
amounts, and deliberate governance choices. The rejected alternative
demonstrates that the governance gap is not an oversight or structural
necessity but a documented design choice.
