# Counter-evidence: constitutive violence has no "well-used" form from below

DATE RECORDED: 2026-03-09
SOURCE: Fanon, *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961), P1 — via patterns-detail.md NOTE
SOURCE TYPE: case study

## Tags
AXIOMS: 9
PATTERNS: The Economy of Violence
LAYERS: Physical & Coercive
RELATIONSHIP: complicates

## Content

The Economy of Violence pattern describes violence's temporal calibration
as a ruler's strategic variable: concentrated violence at acquisition
establishes control, distributed violence during maintenance sustains it.
The pattern frames this as an empirical observation about when and how
violence is deployed.

Fanon (P1) challenges this from the subjected perspective. Colonial
violence is total and constitutive — it does not merely establish a
political order but produces the categories (colonizer/colonized,
human/subhuman) through which subjects experience reality. From below,
there is no distinction between "well-used" and "badly-used" foundational
violence. All constitutive violence delegitimizes the system it
establishes, because the system's legitimacy requires the subjected to
accept their own dehumanization as natural.

The challenge is one of perspective asymmetry, not logical contradiction.
The pattern accurately describes the ruler's calculus (concentrated
violence is cheaper than distributed violence). Fanon accurately
describes the subject's experience (all foundational violence is total).
Both observations can be simultaneously true. But the pattern, as stated,
carries an implicit positional bias: it adopts the ruler's analytical
frame (violence as calibrated instrument) without acknowledging that this
frame is invisible or meaningless to those subjected to it.

## Significance

This complicates the Economy of Violence pattern's claim to be a neutral
empirical description. The pattern is empirically accurate from Position
1 (the exerciser of violence) but analytically incomplete from Position
2 (the subjected). A pattern that describes violence only from the
position of those who deploy it risks normalizing the deployment by
treating it as a resource allocation problem. Fanon's challenge requires
that the pattern carry a positional caveat: it describes the ruler's
optimal strategy, not the reality of violence as experienced by those
it is used against.
